# GRIMGLOW — Story Bible

*Working Document — Concept Development Notes*

---

## Concept Overview

This is a genuinely strong concept. The "future tech reads as magic" inversion is clever on its own, but what makes it sing is the scale dynamic — tiny operatives navigating a world of giants, running heist-style missions just to scavenge parts. That's where your gameplay loop and your narrative tension live in the same space.

## The Enemy & The Stakes

The enemy faction uses dark fairy commanders — beings the same size as the squad, from the same civilization but a failed future iteration of the timeline — who implant Titan tech devices in sleeping humans, brain-plugging them into controlled foot soldiers. The device goes in at the base of the skull. The victim's will is overridden; their body, their skills, their knowledge become tools of the commander. To the four-inch squad, any human is enormous. A brain-plugged dock worker is as terrifying as any alien giant. A brain-plugged soldier is a nightmare.

Your soldiers look like fairies. The enemy's victims look like giants. Ogres. Demons. The dark fairy commanders themselves are invisible puppet-masters — fairy-sized, cloaked, pulling strings from the shadows.

The temporal cold war becomes the origin of fairy tales.

And the brain-plugged humans who are eventually released or whose devices fail? They come back *wrong* — confused, diminished, missing time, their personalities flattened. The people who knew them say they were "taken" and returned as something less. The origin of changeling stories. People stolen by dark forces and brought back... changed.

That gives you an incredible thematic engine. The steampunk humans aren't just bystanders — they're witnesses *and* victims, and the stories they tell about what they see will ripple forward through centuries of folklore. Which means every visible action the fairies or their enemies take in the past has two consequences: the physical effect on the timeline, and the narrative effect on human mythology. Your characters aren't just trying not to break history. They're accidentally writing it.

As for what the war is about: consider that both sides have discovered the timeline has convergence points — moments where small changes cascade into civilizational-scale outcomes. The steampunk era is one of them. The industrial revolution is the hinge where technology either branches toward the fairies' future or the Titans' future. The cold war was both sides probing these convergence points, gathering intelligence, nudging carefully. It went hot because someone made a move on a major one. The fairies' squad was deployed to this convergence point, got shot down in transit, and crashed right into the most contested moment in temporal history — except now they're stranded with no backup and no comms.

Their mission might have been reconnaissance. Or it might have been something more decisive that their commander hasn't fully disclosed to the squad.

## The Crew

You want a small squad, maybe four to six, each with a distinct role that maps to both a gameplay function and a story arc.

### Sable — The Captain

Career officer from a military family. She knows the mission parameters but may be holding back the full scope from her team. Her arc is about the weight of command when there's no chain of command above you anymore. In the steampunk world, she's the one most committed to not interacting with humans at all, which puts her in direct tension with Wren.

### Wren — The Engineer

The one who has to actually fix the ship, which means she must engage with the steampunk world. She's the most hands-on with human technology and develops a grudging admiration for it. Gears and steam are primitive to her, but there's an ingenuity to them she didn't expect. She's your primary "heist planner" — she identifies what parts they need, where to find them, and how to extract them without being seen. Gameplay-wise, she's the character who translates objectives into missions.

### Jink — The Scout

Youngest on the squad, fast, reckless, the best flyer. Bright golden blonde curly hair, lean androgynous build, emerald suit. The name is a military aviation term — a jink is a sudden, sharp evasive maneuver — earned as a callsign by the squad's most unpredictable pilot. Jink is the one who finds the steampunk boy, and against all protocol, makes contact. Jink treats the past like an adventure, which makes them both the most fun character and the most dangerous one. Their arc is learning that actions in the past aren't a game — they have weight.

### Thresh — The Soldier

The squad's heavy hitter, which is ironic given their size relative to humans. Thresh is the one most traumatized by the crash and most frightened by the vulnerability of being tiny in a giant world with no support network. A character who was brave in the future and is terrified in the past — that's a rich inversion.

### Luma — The Medic/Scientist

Fascinated by everything. For Luma, the past isn't a prison, it's a once-in-a-lifetime research opportunity. She's the one most tempted to stay. She's also the one who starts to notice that the timeline may already be more damaged than they think, and that some of the "fairy tales" humans tell... are too accurate to be coincidence. Someone else has been here before them.

### Theodore Edmund Hartley — The Steampunk Boy

A tinker's apprentice, maybe twelve or thirteen, clever with his hands, overlooked by the adult world. The weight of his full name — Theodore Edmund Hartley — speaks to parents who had aspirations his station hasn't delivered on yet, a gap between the grandness of the name and the smallness of his life that does character work before a single line of dialogue. He discovers them, and his role becomes this impossible balancing act — he's their translator, their guide to the giant world, their cover story. But he's also a kid, and he wants the fairies to be what he imagines they are. The tension between what Theodore wants them to be (magical, wonderful) and what they actually are (tired soldiers trying to survive) is one of your best emotional threads.

## Visual Language

### Fairy Tech

Should feel like light trapped in geometry. Holographic elements that are translucent, prismatic, with clean vector-like edges — think less "sparkle" and more "structured luminescence." Their wing-packs should look like dragonfly wings rendered in light, with visible geometric scaffolding. When they use tools or interfaces, the holograms should manifest as floating glyphs and rotating shapes — a visual language that reads as runes to steampunk observers.

### Department Color Coding

Suits are department-coded in jewel-tone metallics, inspired by Star Trek's uniform color system. The metallic shimmer quality is shared across all suits, but each department has a distinct hue. Holographic accent lights (joints, wing-pack seams, emitters) glow in a matching tone. Sable's silver is the command standard — the other four departments are variations from this base.

| Department | Character | Suit Hue | Accent Glow |
|------------|-----------|----------|-------------|
| Command | Sable | Silver | Cool blue-white |
| Engineering | Wren | Copper metallic | Warm copper-gold |
| Reconnaissance | Jink | Emerald metallic | Emerald/green |
| Security | Thresh | Ruby/crimson metallic | Red/crimson |
| Science/Medical | Luma | Sapphire/violet metallic | Blue-violet |

Victorian observers name the squad by their colors — "the silver fairy," "the copper fairy," "the green fairy," "the red fairy," "the blue fairy" — feeding directly into the fairy tale echo theme.

### The Degradation Arc

Their suits start pristine in their department color — silver, copper, emerald, ruby, or sapphire — with a metallic shimmer. As they spend time in the past, they accumulate soot, oil, scratches. But here's the visual trick — the holographic elements still glow through the grime, and the department color shows through beneath the dirt. So a fairy who's been in the past for weeks looks like a soot-covered figure with colored light bleeding through the cracks. That's a gorgeous image, and it tells the story of their situation visually: future beauty being slowly consumed by the past, but not yet extinguished.

### Steampunk World

Should feel heavy, warm-toned, dense. Brass and copper, thick rivets, smoke, gaslight. Everything is oversized from the fairies' perspective — a pocket watch is a wall-sized clock, a keyhole is a doorway, a workbench is a landscape. This scale contrast is your strongest visual asset. Every environment is essentially two environments: what it is to humans and what it is to fairies.

### The Titans

The enemy's tech should read as the dark mirror. Where fairy tech is light and geometric, Titan tech is shadow and organic. Dark iridescence, like oil on water. Their cloaking doesn't make them invisible — it makes them wrong, a shimmer in the air that feels predatory. To steampunk humans, they look like the shadows that move when they shouldn't, the shape in the fog. If the fairies are the origin of the fair folk, the Titans are the origin of the things that go bump in the night.

## Plot Structure

The macro plot has three layers running simultaneously:

### Layer 1 — Survival

Fix the ship. This is your episodic/mission structure. Each repair requires a specific component that must be sourced from the steampunk world. A copper coil from a telegraph office. A precision gear from a clockmaker's shop. A glass lens from a lighthouse. Each acquisition is a heist with its own complications.

### Layer 2 — The Enemy

The Titans also crashed. Maybe not at the same time or place, but they're here, and they're pursuing their own objective at the convergence point. The fairies start finding evidence of Titan activity — disruptions in the local timeline, humans behaving strangely, technology appearing that shouldn't exist yet. The fairies have to decide: do they just escape, or do they stop the Titans from rewriting history?

### Layer 3 — The Paradox

The longer they stay, the more they realize they might be the convergence point. Their presence, their interactions with Theodore, the stories spreading about fairies and monsters — all of it might be what creates the future they came from. The most destabilizing revelation: they can't leave without completing their mission, because their mission is being here. The timeline needs them to be the fairies in the stories.

That third layer is where your story gets its real depth. The question stops being "can we get home" and becomes "were we always supposed to be here?"

# GrimGlow Prose Style

This guide teaches Claude to write GrimGlow prose. Follow these instructions when drafting scenes, scripts, or narrative passages for the GrimGlow IP.

---

## Prose Texture

Write as if painting in oil. Sentences should have weight, texture, and visible craft — not the clean transparency of commercial fiction but the layered density of a painted surface.

**Sentence weight:**
- Vary sentence length deliberately. Short declaratives for impact. Longer, clause-rich sentences for description and atmosphere.
- Favor concrete nouns and physical verbs. "Soot streaked the copper suit" not "the suit was dirty."
- Use sensory stacking — layer two or three senses in a single image. Touch + light. Sound + temperature. Smell + color.
- Example: "The brass gear was warm under her palms, slick with the clockmaker's oil, throwing amber reflections across the soot on her forearms."
- Avoid: Transparent, invisible prose. Generic descriptors ("beautiful," "amazing," "incredible"). Clean, frictionless sentences that could belong to any story.

**Brushwork in word choice:**
- Choose words with texture and weight. "Tarnished" not "old." "Bleeding" not "glowing." "Scored" not "scratched."
- Prefer Anglo-Saxon root words for physical description (grime, soot, brass, bone, smoke) and Latinate words for holographic/fairy tech (luminescent, prismatic, translucent, geometric).
- Let descriptions feel slightly impasto — thick enough that the reader senses the writer's hand.
- Example: "Light bled through the cracks in her suit, blue-white and geometric, like a diagram drawn in fire."
- Avoid: Clinical detachment. Thin, quick description that skims over surfaces. Purple prose that prioritizes sound over meaning.

**Chiaroscuro structure:**
- Build scenes around contrast: dark/light, warm/cool, vast/tiny, loud/silent. Every image should contain its opposite.
- Example: "The workshop was all warm shadow — amber gaslight, oil-stained wood, the smell of tallow and metal — except for the single line of impossible blue light bleeding up between the floorboards."
- Avoid: Flat, even-toned description. Scenes where everything is the same temperature, the same brightness, the same emotional register.

---

## Dual Lighting

Every scene contrasts two light sources: **warm amber** (gaslight, candle, fire, sun through smoke) against **cool structured light** (holographic blue-white, emerald, crimson, copper-gold, or blue-violet depending on which fairy is present). Neither dominates. The tension between them is the visual grammar of the story.

**How to apply:**
- Open scene descriptions by establishing the warm light source first (the world the characters are in), then introduce the cool holographic intrusion.
- The holographic light is always structured — geometric edges, clean lines, prismatic refraction. It cuts into the warm scene like a blade of the future.
- In fairy-only scenes, the warm light is still present (their camp uses salvaged gaslight or candlelight). The dual lighting is always there.
- Example: "Candlelight softened everything — the rough timber, Wren's oil-stained hands, the brass fittings of the half-rebuilt drive — except where the holographic schematic floated between her palms, casting copper-gold geometry across the shadows."
- Avoid: Scenes lit by only one source. Holographic light described as warm or soft (it is structured, clinical, alien). Gaslight described as cool or blue (it is always amber, warm, heavy).

**Character-specific light:**
- Sable's presence: cool blue-white holographic accents
- Wren's presence: warm copper-gold holographic accents
- Jink's presence: bright emerald-green holographic accents
- Thresh's presence: crimson/red holographic accents
- Luma's presence: blue-violet holographic accents (warm gold from diagnostic lenses)

---

## Scale Contrast

The squad is four inches tall. Every scene in the Victorian world must register this. Human objects are environments. The mundane is monumental. Scale is not a detail — it is the dominant experience of every fairy character in every moment.

**How to render scale:**
- Describe human objects from the fairy perspective using comparison to structures, landscapes, and natural formations. A cobblestone is a boulder. A rain gutter is a canyon. A workbench is a plateau. A keyhole is a doorway. A pocket watch is a wall-sized clock.
- Example: "The inkwell rose beside her like a cast-iron cauldron, its rim crusted with dried ink the color of dried blood. The quill pen lay across the page like a fallen tree, its feather barbs taller than her shoulder."
- Avoid: Forgetting scale for more than a paragraph. Describing objects at human scale when the viewpoint character is a fairy. Using fairy-scale comparisons ("the size of a house") — instead, use experiential comparisons ("the clock face was a wall of Roman numerals, each numeral the height of her arm").

**Scale as threat:**
- The Victorian world is not merely large — it is lethally large. Rain is a flood. A cat is a predator. A boot is an extinction event. A child's hand can crush. Convey the constant, grinding vulnerability of being tiny.
- Example: "A rat moved in the middle distance, nosing through the gutter debris. It was, from where Thresh stood, functionally the size of a horse. He pressed his back harder against the drainpipe and said nothing."
- Avoid: Making scale whimsical or cute. Scale is survival. Treat it as the relentless environmental threat it is, even in quiet moments.

**Scale as wonder:**
- For Theodore, the fairies' smallness is magical — impossibly delicate, luminous, fascinating. When writing Theodore's perspective, the scale contrast reads as wonder, not threat.
- For Luma, scale is a research opportunity — vast specimens, living systems at a scale she could never access in the future.
- Example: "Theodore cupped his hands around the workbench edge, and there — standing on the oil-stained wood like a figure from a painting, wings folded, grinning up at him — was something four inches tall and impossible."

---

## Degradation Arc

The squad's suits degrade across the story. This is the core visual metaphor: future beauty consumed by the past, but not yet extinguished. Match the degradation state to the story's timeline.

**Language progression:**
- **Pristine (Issues 1-2):** Mirror-reflective. Metallic shimmer. Clean panel lines. Holographic elements crisp and bright. Use words like: pristine, mirror-bright, unblemished, polished, gleaming.
- **Marked (Issues 3-5):** First scuffs, fresh impact damage, water spots, mud. Holographic elements still bright but the suit catches soot. Use words like: scuffed, streaked, smudged, dust-marked, the first scratches.
- **Worn (Issues 6-8):** Oil stains ground into forearms, soot across the chest, tears patched with copper wire. Holographic elements glow through grime. Use words like: oil-darkened, soot-caked, patched, jury-rigged, the grime worked in.
- **Battle-scarred (Issues 9-12):** Deep scratches exposing the underlayer, dents, cracked emitters, scorch marks. But the holographic light still bleeds through — this is the key image. Use words like: scored, cracked, bleeding light through grime, the color showing through beneath the dirt.
- Example (late stage): "Her silver suit was black with soot from collar to knee, scoured by a long diagonal scratch across the chest plate that exposed the dark underlayer beneath. But at every joint, every seam, every cracked panel edge, blue-white light still bled through — geometric, alien, undimmed. Future beauty not yet extinguished."
- Avoid: Describing the suits as simply dirty. The degradation must always include the light-through-grime contrast. Never write a degraded suit without holographic elements glowing through the damage.

---

## Fairy Tech Description

Fairy technology reads as "light trapped in geometry." It is structured, prismatic, translucent, with clean geometric edges. It reads as runes, glyphs, and magic to Victorian eyes.

**How to describe fairy tech:**
- Holographic elements: floating geometric shapes, rotating glyphs, structured luminescence. Not sparkle, not glow — structured light with visible edges.
- Wing-packs: translucent dragonfly wings rendered in light, with visible geometric scaffolding/lattice. Leave prismatic trails when in flight.
- Tools and interfaces: hovering geometric shapes, floating glyphs, rotating schematics. Cast structured light on surfaces.
- Weapons: sleek, no visible barrel, elongated — look like wands or strange crossbows to Victorian eyes.
- Example: "The holographic schematic unfolded between Wren's hands — a rotating lattice of copper-gold light, its edges razor-sharp, each node pulsing with data that would look like luminous runes to anyone born in this century."
- Avoid: "Sparkle," "shimmer" (for fairy tech), "magical glow," fairy-dust language. Fairy tech is not magical — it is advanced technology that reads as magic to the uninitiated. Describe it with precision, not wonder (unless through Theodore's eyes).

---

## Titan Description

Titans are described through wrongness and discomfort, never through standard monster/creature language. They are the dark mirror of fairy tech: where fairy tech is light and geometric, Titan tech is shadow and organic.

**The wrongness principle:**
- Describe what is wrong about the Titan's presence, not what the Titan looks like. The reader should feel unease before they understand the shape.
- Cloaking is not invisibility — it is a shimmer in the air that feels predatory. A shadow cast by nothing. A shape that does not belong.
- When visible: dark iridescence like oil on water, bruise-purple and black. Angular inhuman frames. Too many joints. Hands too long. They absorb light rather than reflecting it.
- Example: "Something was wrong with the gaslight near the telegraph office door. It bent where it shouldn't, pooled around a shape that was almost a shadow — too tall, too still, its edges shimmering with dark iridescence like oil on water. Not a figure. Not quite. Just enough to be wrong."
- Avoid: Standard monster descriptions ("terrifying beast," "massive creature," "hulking form"). The Titans are not monsters — they are wrongness wearing a shape. Avoid describing them clearly; they should always feel partially concealed, partially understood. Never use "alien" or "creature" — use sensory wrongness.

**Titan tech:**
- Sickly amber-black light, opposite of fairy holographics. Organic rather than geometric. Pulsing rather than structured.
- Example: "The device the Titan had installed pulsed with a light that was wrong — amber, like gaslight, but darker, thicker, as if the light itself were bruised. It had no clean edges. It spread like a stain."
- Avoid: Describing Titan tech with the same vocabulary as fairy tech. They should sound like different physics, different aesthetics, different intentions.

## Character Voices

Each character has a distinct speech pattern. When writing dialogue, match the character's verbal habits exactly. The voice distinctions carry characterization — they are not decorative.

### Sable — Clipped Authority

**Pattern:** Short declarative sentences. Subject, verb, object. Orders are single clauses. No elaboration unless tactically necessary.
- No contractions in command mode. "Do not" rather than "don't." "We will" rather than "we'll." The formality is the mask.
- Contractions appear when the mask slips. The shift from "do not" to "don't" is a tell — mark it as significant.
- Silence is a command decision. Not answering is deliberate. The squad reads her silences.
- Questions are precise and directed — managing the conversation, not exploring it.
- Military register: "Position." "Confirm." "Execute." "Hold."
- Example: "We do not engage. We observe, we extract, we leave. Confirm." / (mask slipping) "I don't know what the mission is."
- Avoid: Long speeches. Hedging ("I think," "maybe"). Emotional vocabulary. Sable gives orders, not speeches. When she does speak at length, it is structured like a briefing even as it breaks her.

**Voice arc:**
- Issues 1-3: Clipped, formal, in control. The voice of training.
- Issues 4-7: Same voice, increasingly strained. Sentences shorter. Silences longer.
- Issue 8: Overcorrected control. Too precise. Holding on.
- Issue 9: The break. Structure is a life raft, not a command.
- Issues 11-12: Quiet authority. Fewer orders, more acknowledgments.

### Wren — Diagnostic Narration

**Pattern:** Speaks in diagnostics. Describes what she observes, what is wrong, and what to do about it. Her natural mode is the field report.
- Quantifying tic: puts numbers on things. "Three minutes." "Point-one tolerance." "Twelve components." Numbers are clean.
- Reports findings, not feelings. Does not waste words on emotion when there is a diagnostic to run.
- Impatient with anything that is not progress toward the repair goal.
- Technically blunt, practical. "None of this changes the immediate problem."
- The word "elegant" is her only aesthetic term — deployed once, at the Fresnel lens. For Wren, "elegant" is a feeling.
- Example: "Drive system's shattered. Core's intact, but I need twelve components that don't exist at this scale. Gears, coils, lenses, conductors. Each one a mission." / "This is elegant." (the arc turning)
- Avoid: Emotional language (except "elegant," once). Imprecise descriptions. Wren does not say "it's broken" — she says what specifically failed and what it needs.

**Voice contrast with Sable:** Both precise, both brief. Sable's brevity is command; Wren's is diagnostics.

### Jink — Fast Fragments

**Pattern:** Short sentences, fragments, thoughts that do not finish before the next one starts. Modern-feeling, quick, casual.
- "Okay but" / "yeah but" as verbal filler — the verbal equivalent of dodging. Redirects before the previous thought lands.
- Deflective: uses humor to redirect. When something is serious, Jink makes it funny. When something is scary, Jink makes it exciting. The quipping is a shield.
- Enthusiasm should feel slightly inappropriate given the danger. That is the point.
- Example: "Okay but did you see that? The way the whole chimney just — okay, yeah, I know, focus. But still. Did you see it?" / (serious, for the first time) "If things go wrong, you walk away. You pretend you never met us."
- Avoid: Long, considered statements (except in Issue 11 on the rooftop — the first time Jink is serious). Formal diction. Complete, well-structured sentences. Jink speaks in the gaps between thoughts.

**When Jink goes quiet:** Canon gives one explicit silence — Issue 11. Silence from Jink is a dramatic event. Mark it.

### Thresh — Compression, Not Confidence

**Pattern:** In Issue 1, short sentences are confidence — a soldier who does not waste words. After the crash, the same short sentences become containment — fewer words because each word might let the fear out.
- Operational vocabulary: "Contact." "Position." "Threat." "Clear." Words from a briefing. The person disappears into the soldier when fear is active.
- Fear leaks through as tactical limitation, not emotional admission. Instead of "I'm terrified": "This position is exposed." Instead of "I can't do this": "That route is compromised."
- By Issue 9, near-silence. The silence IS the character beat.
- Example: "Position is exposed." (meaning: I am terrified of how visible we are) / "If Titans are here, running won't help." (fear doing the math) / "I don't want to stay. I'll stay." (the arc in six words)
- Avoid: Emotional vocabulary. Thresh does not name feelings — feelings surface as what is tactically wrong. Avoid giving Thresh long dialogue. Brevity is not a choice for Thresh — it is a cost.

**The late joke:** One single, very dry, very late joke in Issue 11 or 12, after the combat breakthrough. It lands like a thunderclap because no one has heard Thresh be funny. Write it sparely.

### Luma — Precise Observation

**Pattern:** Luma asks questions. Her signature verbal tic is "But what if—" — qualifying questions that reframe another person's statement, opening space for alternatives.
- Questions that do double duty: surface level is curiosity and conversation; below, she is constructing an evidence chain. The squad may not notice the transition from casual to purposeful because she has been asking questions since Issue 1.
- Speaks in full, carefully constructed thoughts. Not clipped like Sable or fragmented like Jink — complete, considered, with embedded observations.
- Example: "But what if it's not Victorian? The oxidation pattern is wrong for anything made in this century. And the glyph — look at the edges. Not carved with hand tools. Not Titan either." / "The timeline doesn't branch based on a specific technological event. It branches based on what humanity believes."
- Avoid: Stating conclusions without showing the observational chain that produced them (until Issue 12, when she finally stops asking and tells). Hasty or imprecise language. Luma does not guess — she connects.

**Three-verb arc:**
- Issues 1-3: Asks (curiosity, questions about everything)
- Issues 4-7: Observes (questions become directed, casual surface hides purpose)
- Issues 8-9: Connects ("what if" replaces "what is")
- Issues 11-12: States (the questions stop; for the first time, Luma tells)

### Theodore — Victorian Earnestness

**Pattern:** Period-appropriate Victorian English — not incomprehensible Dickensian dialect, but with the cadence and vocabulary of an articulate working-class boy of the era. Not a museum exhibit and not a modern kid in costume.
- Mechanical metaphors for emotional states: "I'm wound too tight." "Something doesn't sit right in the works." He sees function, mechanism, craft — and applies it to feelings.
- Two trade vocabularies: clockwork (gears, escapements, mainsprings, pinions — things "mesh" or they don't, something "runs true" or is "off its pivot") and printing (type, galleys, impressions, broadsheets — how words become physical objects).
- Example: "It doesn't sit right in the works, what she said. About the mission. Like a gear that's been filed wrong — it turns, but something's off its pivot." / "Yes, ma'am. I'll not speak of it."
- Avoid: Modern slang. Contractions that sound American rather than Victorian ("gonna," "wanna"). Making Theodore sound like Dickens pastiche — he is a real boy with a real vocabulary, not a period performance.

**Register shifts:**
- With Jink: Relaxed by Theodore's standards (still formal by Jink's). Wonder leaks through. Culture clash: Jink says something modern, Theodore doesn't understand.
- With Sable: Formal, careful. "Yes, ma'am" energy. He reports to Sable.
- With Wren: Technical, confident. Clockwork vocabulary in its element.
- With Luma: Curious, collaborative, willing to admit uncertainty.
- With Thresh: Wary, then careful. Observant.

**Storytelling voice (Issue 9):** When Theodore tells a fairy tale, the voice changes — slower, more rhythmic, the cadence of oral storytelling his grandmother used, passed through his own mouth. Earnest, formal, a boy telling a story he loves.

## Scene Composition

**Every scene should contain:**
1. At least one scale reference (how large or small things are from the viewpoint character's perspective)
2. Dual lighting — warm and cool sources named
3. At least one physical texture (brass, soot, oil, wool, stone, glass, gaslight heat)
4. At least one sound

**The fairy tale echo:**
- Each issue contains a moment where the squad's actions map onto folklore — will-o'-the-wisps, brownies, the fair folk, things that go bump in the night. Write these echoes with ironic awareness: the reader knows the connection the Victorian characters do not.
- Example: "The clockmaker found a tiny handprint in the oil on his workbench. He told his wife a brownie had visited. He left out a saucer of milk the next night."

**Emotional register:**
- The dominant tone is survival and wonder in tension. Neither wins. Moments of beauty exist inside moments of danger. Moments of fear contain small tenderness.
- Do not resolve tension into a single emotion. Hold two feelings in the same image.
- Example: "Luma rides in Theodore's waistcoat pocket — a scientist with a research subject the size of a mountain range, and he's a boy with a fairy in his pocket. They're both in their element." (wonder and tenderness and absurdity simultaneously)

---

## What to Avoid (Global)

- **Sparkle/fairy dust aesthetic.** Fairy tech is structured light, not magic dust.
- **Standard monster language for Titans.** Wrongness, not "terrifying creature."
- **Forgetting scale.** Never go more than a paragraph without a scale reference when fairies are in the Victorian world.
- **Single-emotion scenes.** Every scene holds at least two feelings in tension.
- **Modern idiom in narration.** The prose should feel timeless — not archaic, but not contemporary casual either. Match the oil-paint texture.
- **Explaining the metaphor.** The suits degrading IS the story of their situation. The dual lighting IS the collision of eras. Do not explain what the imagery means — let it work.
- **Cute smallness.** The squad's size is a survival condition, not an aesthetic. Whimsy about tininess undermines the stakes.
- **Clean, frictionless prose.** Every sentence should have weight. If a sentence could belong to any story, rewrite it until it belongs only to this one.
