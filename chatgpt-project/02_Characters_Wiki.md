# Captain Sable

Captain of the five-person fairy squad stranded in 1885 London. Career officer from a military family, Sable carries a classified mission file she cannot fully decrypt and a command style built on control she can no longer maintain. Her arc across Volume 1 is "command without a chain of command" -- learning that authority backed by an institution must become authority backed by nothing but her squad's choice to follow her. (Source: Story Bible; Plot Outline, Arc Threads)

## Physical Description

Late thirties in appearance. Dark brown skin, sharp angular features, high cheekbones. Close-cropped silver-white hair -- "a deliberate choice, not age." Athletic, lean, precise build. Military bearing; weight on her back foot when in command posture. She carries herself with "authority that's costing her something." (Source: Character Prompts; Character Turnarounds)

### Suit -- Clean State

Form-fitting command silver, pristine and mirror-reflective. Subtle segmented texture at joints suggesting advanced flexible armor. Geometric panel lines trace the suit like circuit paths. Compact holographic emitter near left temple -- a silver disc with geometric lens, inactive when pristine. Rank insignia on left collar: small geometric glyph etched in luminous blue. Slim utility belt with minimal pouches. (Source: Character Turnarounds)

**Wing-pack:** Stowed as compact angular module between shoulder blades, rectangular with beveled edges, approximately torso-width. Hairline seams glow faintly with pale blue-white light even when pristine. (Source: Character Turnarounds)

**Sidearm:** Sleek, elongated, no visible barrel -- "resembles a silver wand." Holstered at right hip. Would look like a wand to Victorian eyes. (Source: Character Prompts; Character Turnarounds)

### Suit -- Mid-Mission (Degraded)

Command silver scuffed and streaked with soot along arms and knees. Scratches across right shoulder and left forearm expose the darker underlayer. Suit still catches light on undamaged surfaces. Faint holographic heads-up display glows in pale blue geometric shapes near left eye -- floating glyphs like luminous runes, semi-transparent. Wing-pack lightly scratched but intact, seams still glowing. (Source: Character Turnarounds; Character Prompts)

The degradation arc is the core visual metaphor: "Future beauty being slowly consumed by the past, but not yet extinguished." Command silver consumed by grime but holographic light still bleeding through at joints, collar, wing-pack seams, and temple emitter. (Source: Story Bible, Degradation Arc)

### Fairy Tale Echo

Victorian observers call her "the silver fairy" -- the leader. The command silver is the most visible suit color and the one associated with authority. (Source: Suit Color Coding brainstorm)

## Personality

### Command Style

Decision-first. She decides, then communicates. No open discussion unless she invites it. Information management is doctrine -- she controls what the squad knows, not out of deception but out of trained compartmentalization. Emotional suppression is discipline: she treats emotion as a failure of composure, not a source of information. "Crisis management is her training." (Source: Script Prompts, Issue 2; Sable Backstory brainstorm)

### Core Traits

- **Controlled.** Every action is deliberate. She gives orders, not speeches. Silence is a command decision.
- **Isolated.** The weight of command separates her from the squad. She carries information alone because that is what captains do.
- **Adaptive -- reluctantly.** Each time her control model fails (Jink's protocol breach, Theodore's presence, Wren's confrontation), she adjusts. The adaptation costs her, but she does it.
- **Wrong about key things.** She told Theodore "this is not a story" -- the irony being that it absolutely is. She wanted to wipe his memory -- he becomes essential. Her initial judgments are often wrong, and the wrongness is part of her arc.

(Source: Story Bible; Plot Outline; Sable Backstory brainstorm)

## Backstory

Career officer from a military family. Beyond these two facts, backstory details remain deliberately open. The silver-white hair is "a deliberate choice, not age" -- its specific meaning is unspecified. She has been trained for situations where things go wrong fast. (Source: Story Bible; Character Turnarounds; Sable Backstory brainstorm)

## The Mission File

The classified intelligence arc is the narrative spine of Sable's Volume 1 story.

### What the File Contains

- **"CONVERGENCE"** -- this convergence point is critical to the temporal cold war
- **"PRIOR"** -- references to prior insertions at this convergence point; none reported back
- The mission was **intervention, not reconnaissance** -- the squad was sent to ensure the timeline branches correctly
- Operational specifics were destroyed in the crash -- Sable knows the mission is critical but not what she was supposed to do

(Source: Plot Outline, Issues 6 and 9; Script Prompts, Issues 2 and 9; Sable Backstory brainstorm)

### Why She Hides It

**Training.** Compartmentalization is doctrine. Classified information stays classified until subordinates need it for their current task. **Protection.** If the squad knew they were on an intervention mission with no extraction plan, morale would collapse. **Fear.** She does not know the full mission. Admitting "we are on a critical mission but I don't know what it is" would be worse than silence. A captain who does not know the mission cannot lead the squad to its completion. The file is evidence of her own inadequacy as a commander. (Source: Sable Backstory brainstorm, Section 2)

### The Revelation Sequence

### The Issue 9 Break

Wren confronts Sable about the encrypted file. Wren "reads systems, and Sable's system doesn't add up." Sable breaks -- "not dramatically -- she's too disciplined for that." She shares the file with the full squad. Even breaking, she structures the information like a briefing. "Reports truth like a briefing." The real break is not sharing the file -- it is admitting "I don't know what the mission is." (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 9; Script Prompts, Issue 9; Sable Backstory brainstorm)

## Relationships

### Sable and Wren

Direct tension over human interaction protocol. Sable wants zero contact with humans; Wren makes the pragmatic case for using Theodore. Wren is Sable's most capable squad member and the one most likely to challenge her. Wren confronts Sable about the mission file in Issue 9 -- not to undermine her but because she cares about the squad's survival and knows Sable is hiding something. The confrontation is an act of loyalty, not rebellion. After the truth-sharing, the squad functions better -- trust replaces control. (Source: Story Bible; Plot Outline, Issue 9; Sable Backstory brainstorm)

### Sable and Jink

"Furious at Jink, terrified of the exposure" when Jink brings Theodore to the squad in Issue 4. Considers the Jink-Theodore friendship a "liability" in Issue 7. Jink represents everything Sable's command style cannot contain -- instinct over procedure, connection over caution. The Theodore breach is the first moment Sable's authority does not hold. Jink is furious at being lied to after Issue 9 -- Jink trusted Sable the way a soldier trusts a commander, and the trust was broken by exactly the kind of calculated decision Sable prides herself on. (Source: Plot Outline, Issues 4, 7, 9; Sable Backstory brainstorm)

### Sable and Thresh

Thresh is the squad member Sable worries about the least and the most. The least: he follows orders, holds positions, does his job. The most: he is afraid, constantly, and Sable can see it. The Issue 7 surprise -- Thresh siding with Jink in the Titan debate -- is the first time Sable realizes she has misjudged him. She assumed Thresh would support protocol because fear makes people crave structure. Instead, Thresh chose the braver option. (Source: Sable Backstory brainstorm; Plot Outline, Issue 7)

### Sable and Luma

Every discovery Luma makes is a countdown timer on Sable's secret. The glyph in Issue 4, the cache in Issue 8 -- Luma is finding the truth independently. Sable's "too controlled" reaction to Luma's findings in Issue 8 reveals to Wren that Sable already knew. Sable must eventually reckon with the possibility that the squad member she might have dismissed as self-indulgent was the first to see the truth. Luma sees the paradox from field evidence; Sable carries the classified version of the same truth. They hold pieces of the same puzzle without knowing the other has one. (Source: Plot Outline, Issues 4, 8, 12; Sable Backstory brainstorm)

### Sable and Theodore

Wanted to wipe his memory in Issue 4. Relented "with conditions." Told him: "They are not magical, they are not here to grant wishes, and this is not a story." The memory-wipe impulse reveals that Sable's first response to civilian exposure is erasure -- the most absolute solution. She was wrong: Theodore is not a security risk but an essential ally. By Issue 12, he is coordinating with the squad during the final operation. The wrongness of her initial judgment is part of her arc. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 4; Sable Backstory brainstorm)

**Authority without hierarchy.** Command does not require a chain of command. Luma found the mission; Sable had to accept it. (Source: Sable Backstory brainstorm; Plot Outline, Issues 11-12)

## Voice

### Speech Patterns

**Short sentences.** Subject, verb, object. Orders are single clauses. No elaboration unless tactically necessary. **No hedging.** Does not say "I think" or "maybe." Says what is, or says nothing. **Silence as communication.** Not answering is a command decision. The squad has learned to read her silences. **Questions as control.** Precise and directed -- managing the conversation, not exploring it. **Military register.** "Position." "Confirm." "Execute." "Hold." (Source: Script Prompts; Sable Backstory brainstorm; Squad Voice Tics brainstorm)

### Contraction Tell

No contractions in command mode. "Do not" rather than "don't." "We will" rather than "we'll." The formality is the mask. Contractions appear when the mask slips. The shift from "do not" to "don't" is a tell. (Source: Squad Voice Tics brainstorm -- LOCKED; Sable Backstory brainstorm)

### When the Mask Slips

- **Issue 4:** Fury at Jink. Sable's control usually prevents visible anger. Jink got past the mask.
- **Issue 8:** "Too controlled" -- overcompensating, the mask too tight, the effort visible. The inverse of a slip.
- **Issue 9:** The break. "Reports truth like a briefing" -- the mask cracks while still being worn. She structures the information even as it breaks her.
- **Issue 9 final page:** "For the first time, her expression isn't commander or burden-carrier. She's lost." The mask is fully off.
- **Issue 12:** "Something quiet." The mask is not back on. It is not needed anymore. She is not performing command. She is just Sable, looking at her people.

(Source: Script Prompts; Sable Backstory brainstorm)

## Hidden Layer

<hidden>The leader of the dark fairy commanders in London IS Sable -- older, from a failed future iteration of the timeline. She lived through the full loop: trusted her squad, chose cooperation at every fork, stayed at the convergence point, fought the story war. It wasn't enough. The fairy tales lost. Her squad died. She came back with two survivors to try different methods -- control instead of trust.

Alt-Sable runs the London operation the way Sable runs the squad -- with tactical precision, strategic patience, and hard calls. But her "squad" is brain-plugged humans, not trusted companions. Every parallel between her command style and Sable's cuts deeper now: she IS Sable, with more experience and fewer constraints. She knows exactly how this squad fights because she once commanded one.

Sable's command decisions are replayed by alt-Sable throughout Volume 1. The reader does not see this yet. Sable does not see this yet. When she discovers the truth, every Volume 1 decision doesn't just change meaning -- it becomes a replay. The Issue 9 break, the choice to stay, "they look like fairy tales" -- alt-Sable did all of it first.

The reveal is devastating because Sable sees her own face, aged, and knows: she was ME. She did everything I'm doing. She believed everything I believe. And it didn't work. The question that breaks her: "If cooperation failed in the original timeline, why should it work this time?"

This revelation belongs to a future volume. Volume 1 groundwork: the intelligence behind Titan operations mirrors Sable's own tactical instincts -- because it IS her own tactical instincts.

# Wren

Chief engineer of the fairy squad stranded in 1885 London. Wren is the squad member most physically engaged with the Victorian world -- she must source, adapt, and install human-made components to repair the crashed ship, which makes her the primary heist planner and the character who translates objectives into missions. Her arc across Volume 1 is "growing admiration for 'primitive' human ingenuity" -- she arrives thinking the past is simply inferior and leaves recognizing that ingenuity is not a function of era. (Source: Story Bible; Plot Outline, Arc Threads)

## Physical Description

Early thirties. East Asian features, oval face, wiry build. Dark black hair pulled back in a functional knot at the back of her head, a few deliberately loose strands framing her face. (Source: Character Prompts; Character Turnarounds)

### Suit -- Clean State

Form-fitting copper metallic with a warm shimmer. Reinforced forearm panels with recessed tool slots -- light armor focused on "reinforced joints, not plates." Chest-mounted holographic projector: a small circular lens centered on the sternum. Magnetic attachment points on thighs and hips for carrying salvaged components. Multiple slim tool pouches on a low-slung utility belt, fuller than Sable's -- wrenches, probes, and scanner instruments in miniature. (Source: Character Turnarounds)

**Wing-pack:** Stowed between shoulder blades, similar geometric module to Sable's but with two additional small antennae extending upward -- sensor arrays for technical scanning. Wing-pack seams glow warm copper-gold. (Source: Character Turnarounds)

### Suit -- Mid-Mission (Degraded)

The most visibly worn suit of the squad. Oil stains darken both forearms from wrist to elbow. Soot and copper-colored dust coat her knees and shins. A scorch mark runs along her left shoulder -- dark charring that has partially melted the suit surface. A small tear at her right hip has been patched with a strip of copper wire wound tightly around the suit material -- "steampunk repair on future tech." Despite the damage, warm copper-gold holographic light pulses faintly beneath the grime at her joints, collar seams, and chest-mounted projector lens. Tool pouches are partially open, some tools missing, one replaced with a Victorian-era brass caliper jury-rigged into the magnetic mount. Wing-pack has one sensor antenna bent. (Source: Character Turnarounds)

The degradation arc is the core visual metaphor: "Future beauty being slowly consumed by the past, but not yet extinguished." Wren's suit embodies this most directly -- an engineer who has been working hard in hostile conditions, improvising with local materials. (Source: Story Bible, Degradation Arc; Character Turnarounds)

### Action Pose

Crouched inside the open back panel of a massive Victorian clockwork mechanism, gears the size of doors surrounding her. Both hands manipulating a hovering holographic schematic that floats between her and a brass gear, the hologram casting structured copper-gold light across the tarnished metal. Geometric holographic tools orbit her hands like floating runes. Expression focused, problem-solving. Wing-pack half-deployed -- one translucent dragonfly wing extended for balance. (Source: Character Prompts)

## Personality

### Engineering Mindset

Technically blunt, practical. Reports findings, not feelings. Does not waste words on feelings when there is a diagnostic to run. Impatient with anything that is not progress toward the repair goal. She assesses damage, produces a component list, assigns risk levels, and plans infiltration routes. Every problem is a system to diagnose. (Source: Story Bible; Script Prompts, Issues 1-2)

### Core Traits

- **Pragmatic.** Makes the case for using Theodore not on emotional grounds but on operational utility: "they need someone who can move through the human world." The pragmatism is real, but it also becomes cover for growing admiration she may not acknowledge.
- **Most integrated into the physical world.** She has to fix the ship, which means engaging with Victorian technology, which means understanding it, which means -- eventually -- admiring it.
- **Mask is technical detachment.** Where Sable's mask is emotional control, Wren's is reducing everything to a system to diagnose. The mask slips when she encounters something she cannot reduce to a system.

(Source: Story Bible; Plot Outline; Wren Technology brainstorm)

The ship becomes visually hybrid: "Victorian brass components surrounded by holographic scaffolding." (Source: Steampunk Tech Rules brainstorm; Ship Repair Chain brainstorm)

## Arc Summary

**Arc statement:** "Growing admiration for 'primitive' human ingenuity." She arrives thinking the past is primitive and leaves recognizing that ingenuity is not a function of era. This matters because it is the thematic argument for the convergence point itself -- human creativity is what both sides are fighting over. (Source: Story Bible; Plot Outline, Arc Threads)

### Three-Tier Reaction Spectrum

Wren's response to Victorian technology follows three tiers of increasing respect. (Source: Wren Technology brainstorm; Steampunk Tech Rules brainstorm)

**Tier 1 -- "Brute force but works."** Steam engines, cast iron, industrial manufacturing. She respects the scale but not the method.

**Tier 2 -- "Genuinely clever."** Clockwork precision (0.1mm tolerances by hand), optical instruments (Fresnel lens), telegraph signaling. She sees intelligence constrained by tools.

**Tier 3 -- "How is this even possible?"** Jacquard loom (programmable mechanics from punch cards), Bessemer steel (metallurgical transformation from empirical observation), Bazalgette's sewers (city-scale engineering over-built for the future). These challenge her assumption that intelligence requires advanced tools.

### The Progression

| Phase | What Wren Sees | How She Responds |
|-------|---------------|-----------------|
| Issues 2-4 | Victorian tech as a **parts bin** -- components to extract and adapt | The human world is a supply chain |
| Issues 5-7 | **Engineering** -- not just parts but principles; the clockwork's precision, the Fresnel lens's elegance | She stops categorizing and starts studying |
| Issues 8-10 | **Lineage** -- the telegraph is the conceptual parent of everything she works with | She sees the system, not just the parts |
| Issue 12 | **Recognition** -- Theodore's hands are doing what her hands do, in different materials | The past is not primitive; it is the beginning of her |

(Source: Wren Technology brainstorm; Plot Outline)

### Two Shocks

Wren receives two distinct revelations in Volume 1. (Source: Wren Technology brainstorm)

**Shock 1 -- Titan discovery (Issue 8):** "This isn't alien. This is us. Backwards." Wren analyzes the Titan nudge engine and recognizes fairy engineering principles inverted. Same physics, opposite application. A technical shock -- her understanding of the enemy changes completely.

**Shock 2 -- The temporal paradox (Issue 12):** Luma's synthesis -- the squad IS the convergence point. A philosophical shock that reframes everything Wren has done. Luma sees the paradox as a scientist (patterns, evidence, hypothesis); Wren sees it as an engineer (systems, causation, feedback).

## Voice

### Speech Patterns

**Diagnostic narration.** Wren speaks in diagnostics -- she describes what she observes, what is wrong, and what to do about it. Her natural mode is the field report. (Source: Script Prompts; Wren Technology brainstorm)

**Quantifying tic.** She puts numbers on things because numbers are clean. "Three minutes." "Point-one tolerance." "Twelve components." (Source: Squad Voice Tics brainstorm -- LOCKED; Wren Technology brainstorm)

## Hidden Layer

<hidden>If Wren's improvements to Victorian technology persist -- if Theodore remembers what she taught him, if the feedback pulse leaves a trace in the telegraph, if any of her adaptations influence human engineering -- then she has become part of the causal loop. Her engineering is feeding forward. The same way the fairy tales feed forward through culture, Wren's technical interactions feed forward through technology. She might be an unacknowledged channel in the loop: not stories, but methods.

The telegraph argument she lost to herself: In Issue 12, Wren pushes back on Luma's synthesis. But she already proved Luma's point in Issue 10. Wren understood that the telegraph controls which innovations succeed -- that is the Titan strategy, and she understood it as an engineer. But what does the telegraph carry? Stories. Reports. Information. The same mechanism Wren used to disrupt Titan cloaking is the mechanism that carries the fairy tales, the folklorist's pamphlet, the newspaper reports. Wren argued herself into understanding the paradox two issues before she knew it existed. Her Issue 10 action was the proof of Luma's Issue 12 theory.

# Jink

Scout of the fairy squad stranded in 1885 London. The youngest member, the fastest flyer, and the most reckless. Her callsign is a military aviation term -- a jink is a sudden, sharp evasive maneuver -- earned by the squad's most unpredictable pilot. Her arc across Volume 1 is "learning actions in the past have weight" -- she starts treating the past as an adventure and ends understanding that what she does here lasts forever. Her unauthorized first contact with Theodore in Issue 3 is the inciting event for the core emotional relationship of the volume. (Source: Story Bible; Plot Outline, Arc Threads)

Expression: "a grin -- reckless, alive, loving this." Even at rest, her stance carries restless energy -- weight on one leg, slight asymmetry. (Source: Character Prompts; Character Turnarounds)

### Suit -- Clean State

Sleeker and lighter than the others, emerald metallic with an emerald shimmer. Less armor, more mobility. The suit is thinner at the joints, with visible flex panels at elbows, knees, and waist that allow extreme range of motion. Soot-streaked across the chest and back -- evidence of flying through chimney smoke. A visible tear along the right forearm has been patched with tightly wound copper wire, a rough field repair. Emerald holographic elements glow brightly through the grime, especially along the spine and wing-pack mount points -- the flight systems run hot and burn through the soot layer. Wing-pack seams pulse with visible emerald light, brighter than any other squad member's degraded suit. (Source: Character Turnarounds)

Mid-flight through a Victorian chimney forest -- a roofscape of terracotta pots, iron cowls, and billowing smoke. Expression is a grin -- reckless, alive. ## Personality

### Core Traits

## The Theodore Bond

The core emotional relationship of the volume. (Source: Plot Outline, Arc Threads)

### Discovery (Issues 3-4)

**Issue 3 -- First contact.** Jink, scouting alone against protocol, is caught in the open on Theodore's workbench when he enters unexpectedly. "Jink doesn't panic. Jink grins." Theodore thinks he has found a fairy. Jink does not correct him, "partly because it's simpler and partly because Jink finds the whole thing delightful." Against every protocol Sable has established, Jink talks to the boy. Before leaving, she shows Theodore the wing-pack in full deployment -- "Jink hovers at eye level, wings of structured light filling the workshop. Theodore's face in that light is the defining image." Jink returns to the squad and does not tell Sable what happened. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 3; Script Prompts, Issue 3)

**Issue 4 -- Exposure.** Jink cannot keep the secret -- the squad needs help, and Theodore is help. She brings him to the squad. "Sable is furious at Jink, terrified of the exposure, and immediately calculating." Theodore demonstrates his value with the charging cradle. Sable relents with conditions. The fairy tale breaks here -- Theodore loses the magic, Jink loses the secret. What survives determines the rest of the volume. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 4)

### Partnership (Issues 5-8)

The relationship develops a practical foundation. Theodore creates diversions, acquires components, becomes "genuinely useful and embedded." By Issue 7, "Jink and Theodore have developed a rapport that Sable considers a liability -- they're friends now, not just allies, and friends take risks for each other." The transition from allies to friends is the transition from function to choice. (Source: Plot Outline, Issues 5, 7; Script Prompts, Issue 7)

### Reckoning (Issues 9-12)

**Issue 9 -- The fracture.** The squad fractures over Sable's secret. Jink is furious. Theodore visits, reads the mood, and tells his grandmother's fairy tale -- "not naive -- insightful in a way the squad can't be because they're too close to their own situation." The dynamic inverts: Jink is the one who needs comfort; Theodore is the one who offers it. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 9; Script Prompts, Issue 9)

**Issue 11 -- The rooftop.** "Jink and Theodore have a conversation on a rooftop at night. Jink, for the first time, is serious. She tells Theodore that if things go wrong, he has to walk away -- pretend he never met them, go back to his life. Theodore refuses. Jink says it isn't his choice. Theodore says it isn't Jink's either. It's the most honest conversation they've had." This is the emotional centerpiece of the volume. Everything about the scene is a reversal: Jink, who never takes things seriously, is serious; Jink, who broke protocol to make contact, is trying to undo it; Theodore, who was in awe, is standing firm. "Scale difference makes emotional equality more powerful." (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 11; Script Prompts, Issue 11)

**Issue 12 -- Completion.** Jink diverts to help Thresh during the final operation, abandoning her own target -- recklessness transformed, the same instinct now in service of loyalty rather than adventure. She is absent from the final scene, but the final scene exists because of her: Theodore writes the first fairy tale because he met Jink. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 12)

### What Each Offers the Other

**What Jink offers Theodore:** Being seen -- Theodore's world is full of adults who do not notice him; Jink notices, grins, comes back. The extraordinary -- wings of light, evidence the world is bigger than Cribbage's shop. Play -- "Finds the whole thing delightful." For a boy who works alone and eats alone, someone treating him as fun is transformative. (Source: Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm; Script Prompts)

**What Theodore offers Jink:** Not being the kid -- with Theodore, Jink is the older one, the experienced one, the one who knows things. In the squad, she is junior and reckless; with Theodore, she is capable and extraordinary. Wonder -- no one in the squad looks at Jink with wonder; Theodore does, even after the fairy tale breaks. (Source: Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm; Jink Recklessness brainstorm)

"Each is the other's impossible creature. But the friendship isn't built on the extraordinary. It's built on the ordinary -- Jink's humor, Theodore's competence, shared time, accumulated trust. ## The Temptation

The temptation to stay is Luma's defining internal conflict and the source of the volume's most uncomfortable ambiguity.

### Before the Paradox

Luma wants to stay because the past fascinates her. This is selfish -- she is prioritizing her curiosity over the squad's mission to go home. The draw is real, the investigation is genuine, but the motivation is personal. (Source: Luma Temptation brainstorm; temporal mechanics brainstorm)

### After the Paradox

Luma wants to stay because the timeline needs them here. Staying IS the mission. The evidence supports this. The logic holds. But the conclusion is awfully convenient for the one squad member who wanted to stay all along. (Source: Luma Temptation brainstorm; temporal mechanics brainstorm)

### The Motivated Reasoning

Luma recognizes the ambiguity in herself, and it torments her. She has done the epistemological work: am I seeing this because it is there, or because I want it to be there? She cannot answer. The evidence is real. The conclusion is supported. But the motivation behind the investigation was never pure. She presents the Issue 12 synthesis with rigor and visible doubt -- not uncertain about the evidence, but uncertain about herself. (Source: Luma Temptation brainstorm)

### The "STAY" Message

Four readings coexist:
- **Confirmation** -- someone else reached the same conclusion
- **Horror** -- they stayed and died alone
- **Herself** -- she is looking at her own future if the squad does not come around
- **Evidence** -- it is not just a message but a data point in the causal loop

The shaking hands are all of these at once. Scientific excitement, personal dread, ethical weight, and the recognition that the person who carved this word felt exactly what she feels. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 8; Luma Temptation brainstorm)

Curiosity. A scientist's reflexive need to observe, catalogue, and understand. Professional interest in the convergence point that becomes personal fascination with the world around it. A medical ethic that says "do no harm" and a scientific hunger that says "know everything." (Source: Story Bible; Script Prompts, Issues 1-2)

### What She Finds

The paradox. The evidence chain that connects fairy glyphs, Titan traces, a corroded wing-pack, a dead data core, and one carved word into a conclusion that reframes the entire volume: the squad's presence in 1885 is not an accident but a causal loop, and the convergence point is the stories humanity tells about fairies and monsters. (Source: Plot Outline, Issues 4-12)

### The Cost

The synthesis is inseparable from the desire. She wanted to stay before she knew she had to. The squad cannot be certain her conclusion is not motivated reasoning. She cannot be certain herself. She asks them to stake their lives on her analysis, and her analysis happens to recommend the thing she wants. The weight is not just "should we stay?" but "should the squad trust the person whose conclusion conveniently matches her desire?" (Source: Luma Temptation brainstorm; temporal mechanics brainstorm)

### How the Squad Receives the Synthesis

- **Wren** pushes back: "Stories don't build civilizations." But she has already proved Luma's point -- the telegraph carries information that shapes development. She argued herself into understanding.
- **Jink** does not care about the theory: "So we're not stuck here. We're *needed* here."
- **Thresh:** "I don't want to stay. I'll stay."
- **Sable** listens. Quiet. Looks at her squad. "They look like fairy tales. They always did."

(Source: Plot Outline, Issue 12; Luma Temptation brainstorm)

## Hidden Layer

<hidden>When scanning a brain-plugged human, Luma notices something that unsettles her: the physiological changes -- dark veins, iridescent eyes, bone density shifts, nervous system alterations -- map directly to ogre physiology in the squad's medical databases. The brain-plugging process is not just controlling humans. It is the early stage of what eventually becomes full ogre biology in the far future. The Titans are not using an existing slave race. They are creating one, from humanity, across centuries of convergence point interventions.

Luma notices the resemblance in Volume 1. She files it, does not share it with the squad. It is a seed, not a revelation. The full implications -- that the squad could potentially erase the entire ogre army from the future timeline by stopping the process here -- belong to Volume 2.

This gives Luma a devastating new dimension to her temptation to stay. The stakes are not just fairy tales and cultural mythology. The stakes are the future shape of an entire species. And acknowledging this means staying even longer -- which is exactly what she already wants.

(Source: Ogre Origin brainstorm; Luma Temptation brainstorm)</hidden>

# Theodore Edmund Hartley

The squad's human ally. A Victorian tinker's apprentice, twelve or thirteen years old, clever with his hands, overlooked by the adult world. Theodore discovers the squad and becomes their translator, guide, and cover story -- the giant pair of hands that can move through the human world on their behalf. His bond with Jink is the core emotional relationship of the volume. His arc is "wants fairies to be magical; learns they're something better" -- and "something better" is real people who chose to stay. In the final scene of Volume 1, alone by candlelight, he writes a story about his friends. He does not know he is writing the first fairy tale. (Source: Story Bible; Plot Outline, Arc Threads)

## Identity

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Full Name** | Theodore Edmund Hartley |
| **Age** | 12-13 |
| **Occupation** | Clockmaker's apprentice (to Mr. Cribbage) |

The weight of his full name -- Theodore Edmund Hartley -- "speaks to parents who had aspirations his station hasn't delivered on yet." The gap between the grandness of the name and the smallness of his life does character work before a single line of dialogue. (Source: Story Bible)

## Physical Description

Slight build, average height for his age but narrow in the shoulders -- he has not grown into himself yet. Narrow face, slightly pointed chin, untidy sandy-brown hair that falls over his forehead and ears, pushed behind the right ear but not the left. Wide hazel eyes, a scattering of freckles across the nose and cheeks. His expression is neutral but alert -- "a boy who watches more than he speaks." (Source: Character Turnarounds)

### Hands

His hands are his most important feature. Stained with machine oil that has worked into the creases of his knuckles and under his fingernails. A small circular burn scar on the knuckle of his right index finger. A brass gear ring worn on his left index finger -- a fidget object he made himself, with a tiny working gear that spins. These are the hands that build the charging cradle in Issue 4, create diversions in Issue 5, and disarm the final Titan device in Issue 12. (Source: Character Turnarounds; Character Prompts)

### Clothing

Working-class apprentice clothing: a collarless linen shirt, off-white, with sleeves rolled to the elbows revealing thin forearms. A worn brown leather waistcoat that is slightly too large for him -- it was made for someone broader, the shoulder seams sit too wide, the hem hangs past his hips. The waistcoat has two small front pockets and a visible repair stitch along the left side seam. Patched wool trousers in dark gray-brown, with a visible square patch on the right knee in slightly mismatched fabric. A simple leather belt. Scuffed brown leather boots, laced, with worn soles. His pockets are full of small tools and salvaged parts. "A tinker's apprentice, not a gentleman's son." (Source: Character Turnarounds; Character Prompts)

Theodore has no clean/degraded split. He only has one state. His clothing does not change. (Source: Character Turnarounds, Notes for 3D Generation)

### Action Pose

Kneeling on the wooden floor of a cramped tinker's workshop, peering into a gap between the floorboards where a faint, impossible light glows up at his face. His expression is "the exact moment between fear and wonder, mouth slightly open, not yet decided what he's seeing." The workshop surrounds him at human scale: workbenches cluttered with clockwork parts, hanging tools, a half-assembled automaton on a shelf, gaslight warm and smoky. The only cool light in the frame is the holographic glow from between the floorboards -- structured, geometric, alien -- bleeding up around his fingers and across his face. "He is ordinary in an extraordinary moment." (Source: Character Prompts)

## Personality

### Core Traits

- **Clever.** Observant, notices details others miss, fixes things, tinkers with his own small projects in stolen moments. He fixes something Cribbage cannot, and Cribbage takes credit.
- **Resourceful.** Manages his days, feeds himself, does the work. "Alone in the way a resourceful child is alone: managing, but not thriving."
- **Curious.** Watches everything. Notices details others miss. His curiosity is his own creative space.
- **Earnest.** Sincere, genuine, takes things seriously. "Not a museum exhibit and not a modern kid in costume."
- **Isolated.** His world is small: the workshop, errands, a narrow orbit of streets. No one sees his cleverness. No one notices him.

(Source: Story Bible; Script Prompts, Issue 3; Plot Outline, Issue 3)

### What He Needs

Theodore needs to be seen. Cribbage dismisses him. His father is absent. The adult world treats him as furniture -- useful, unremarkable. When Jink grins at him, talks to him, comes back -- that fills the wondering gap. He needs the fairies to be magical because magical means: this is happening for a reason. You were chosen. You are special. The alternative -- that it is random, that they crashed here by accident, that he could have been anyone -- is harder for a lonely boy to accept. (Source: Theodore Inner World brainstorm; Story Bible)

## Backstory

### Mr. Cribbage

Apprentice to a clockmaker named Mr. Cribbage, "a man who is competent but unkind in a mundane way -- dismissive, impatient, uninterested in Theodore's cleverness." Skilled enough to teach but uninterested in what Theodore can do. The learning is cold. No encouragement. No recognition. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 3)

Cribbage's workshop sits near telegraph infrastructure the Titans are corrupting. Cribbage is brain-plugged by the dark fairy commanders -- the man who dismissed Theodore, took credit for his work, and treated him as invisible becomes a shambling, iridescent-eyed puppet. Theodore has to reconcile his resentment with the horror of seeing Cribbage reduced to this. The power dynamic inverts completely: the master who did not need Theodore now genuinely needs his help. (Source: Ogre Origin brainstorm, Section 8)

### Family

Father is alive, runs a printing press. Works long hours. Present in the city but absent from daily life. Not absent by choice -- absent by circumstance. Mother passed away. Theodore was placed with Cribbage for practical reasons, not abandonment. But the effect on a child is similar. (Source: Theodore Inner World brainstorm)

### The Grandmother

Theodore's grandmother told him fairy tales -- the source of his fairy mythology, the frame he puts around Jink when they meet. In Issue 9 he tells the squad "a story his grandmother told him -- about a fairy queen who sent her knights on a quest, but the quest was a riddle, and the answer was that they were already where they needed to be." The fairy tales are inheritance. They are what Theodore has left of her. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 9; Theodore Inner World brainstorm)

### The Shoe-Shine Alcove

Theodore earns extra pennies shining shoes from a tiny recessed alcove at street level, beneath a grocer's shop front on a Clerkenwell side street. He sits on an upturned crate at pavement height -- invisible to the adult world, but eye-level with the cast-iron storm drain grate beside him. This is the first contact point: Theodore notices faint blue-white light in the drain, hears sounds too deliberate to be rats, and eventually sees a tiny armored figure staring up at him through the iron bars. He discovers the fairies not because he is chosen, but because he is the only person in London who sits at drain-grate height for hours at a time. The alcove is semi-private -- recessed into the building facade -- so Theodore can lean toward the grate and whisper without passers-by noticing. (Source: Theodore Shoe-Shine brainstorm; visual reference: "The Smallest Shop in London" c. 1900)

### The Two Workshops

Theodore moves between two worlds of making. Cribbage's clockshop: clocks, mechanisms, precision -- one makes time. His father's printing press: words, ink, paper, type -- the other makes stories. Both are vocabularies of making. The two workshops converge in Theodore at Issue 12, when his clockwork hands disarm the device and then he writes the story. (Source: Theodore Inner World brainstorm)

## The Bond with Jink

The core emotional relationship of the volume. "Jink starts treating the past as an adventure and learns it has weight; Theodore starts wanting the fairies to be magical and learns they're something better -- real people who chose to stay." Their arcs are mirrors: Jink moves from lightness to weight. Theodore moves from magic to reality. They meet in the middle -- at something honest. (Source: Plot Outline, Arc Threads; Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm)

### Bond Progression

| Phase | Jink's Position | Theodore's Position | What They Are |

| Issue 3 | Delighted, reckless, breaking rules | Awestruck, believing, the fairy tale is real | A fairy and a boy |
| Issue 4 | Exposed, facing consequences | Disillusioned, choosing to stay anyway | A soldier and an asset |

### What Jink Offers Theodore

**Being seen.** Theodore's world is full of adults who do not notice him. Jink notices. Jink grins at him. Jink comes back. (Source: Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm)

**The extraordinary.** In Theodore's narrow orbit -- workshop, errands, streets -- Jink is the first evidence that the world is bigger than Cribbage's clockshop. (Source: Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm)

**Play.** Jink does not treat Theodore as an asset (Wren's pragmatic case), a threat (Sable's calculation), or a subject (Luma's scientific interest). Jink treats him as fun. For a boy who eats alone and works alone, someone who treats him as fun is revolutionary. (Source: Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm)

### What Theodore Offers Jink

**Not being the kid.** With Theodore, Jink is the older one. The experienced one. The one who knows things. In the squad, Jink is the junior, the reckless one. With Theodore, the dynamic inverts. (Source: Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm)

**Wonder.** No one in the squad looks at Jink with wonder. They see a junior pilot with a reckless streak. Theodore sees something magical. Even after the fairy tale breaks, Theodore still watches Jink fly with awe. (Source: Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm)

**A connection outside the hierarchy.** The squad has rank, roles, protocols. Theodore has none. The bond with Theodore is Jink's only relationship not mediated by military structure. "Theodore is the first thing in 1885 that belongs to Jink alone." (Source: Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm; Jink Recklessness brainstorm)

### The Mutual Inversion

Jink is extraordinary to Theodore (a fairy, impossibly small, glowing with light). Theodore is extraordinary to Jink (a giant, impossibly large, capable of crushing her without noticing). Each is the other's impossible creature. But the friendship is not built on the extraordinary. It is built on the ordinary -- Jink's humor, Theodore's competence, shared time, accumulated trust. The extraordinary is how they met. The ordinary is why they stayed. (Source: Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm)

### The Scale

Theodore is a giant to Jink. Jink is a fairy to Theodore. The physical power dynamic is inverted from the emotional one. Theodore could crush Jink without meaning to. Jink has already changed Theodore's life without meaning to. "Scale difference makes emotional equality more powerful." (Source: Script Prompts, Issue 11; Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm)

## Relationships

### Theodore and Sable

Formal. Careful. Theodore instinctively addresses Sable as authority -- she carries herself like someone who commands, and a working-class boy in Victorian London knows how to recognize rank. He is slightly afraid of her. Not because she is four inches tall -- because she is precise and unsmiling and makes him feel like he is being evaluated. "Yes, ma'am" energy -- not because she asked for it, but because Theodore's world teaches deference to authority. Sable told Theodore "this is not a story." His voice around her reflects that: careful, factual, useful. He reports to Sable. He talks to Jink. (Source: Theodore Inner World brainstorm; Plot Outline, Issue 4)

### Theodore and Wren

Technical kinship. "They speak the same language of how things work, just centuries apart." This is where Theodore's clockwork vocabulary is most at home. He can describe a mechanism to Wren and she understands, even though the technology is separated by millennia. Theodore is more confident with Wren than with anyone else in the squad, because with Wren his cleverness is visible. Cribbage does not notice what Theodore can do. Wren notices immediately -- she is "reluctantly impressed" by the charging cradle he builds in Issue 4. (Source: Script Prompts; Theodore Inner World brainstorm; Plot Outline, Issue 4)

### Theodore and Luma

The investigation partnership. From Issue 6 onward, they move through the city together -- Luma riding in Theodore's waistcoat pocket. Theodore knows the streets, the old buildings, which structures predate the current era. Luma has the scanner and the analytical framework. He asks Luma questions the way he asks the world questions -- genuinely wanting to understand. Luma may be the squad member Theodore is most comfortable being uncertain around. With Sable he performs competence. With Jink he performs bravery. With Luma he can admit he does not understand. (Source: Plot Outline, Issues 6 and 8; Theodore Inner World brainstorm)

### Theodore and Thresh

Wary, then careful. Theodore's first encounter with Thresh is intimidating -- Thresh is the most armored, the most visibly a soldier, the one holding a weapon in a white-knuckle grip. Theodore is a boy who watches everything, who "notices details others miss." He may recognize Thresh's trembling hands before anyone in the squad acknowledges it. (Source: Theodore Inner World brainstorm; Plot Outline, Issue 4)

### Why Choice Is More Meaningful than Magic

The fairy tale version: magical beings who appeared for a purpose, whose presence is fated, whose power solves problems. The "something better" version: ordinary people -- extraordinary by circumstances, not nature -- who had the option to leave and stayed. Not because they were compelled. Because they decided this mattered.

For a boy who has been left by everyone -- mother dead, father absent, master indifferent -- someone choosing to be present is the most extraordinary thing imaginable. Not magic. Loyalty. Magic is given. Choice is earned. (Source: Theodore Inner World brainstorm; Plot Outline, Issue 12)

### The Issue 9 Fairy Tale

Theodore tells the squad a story his grandmother told him -- about a fairy queen who sent her knights on a quest, but the quest was a riddle, and the answer was that they were already where they needed to be. He tells the story without knowing why it is relevant. The squad does. "Not naive -- insightful in a way the squad can't be because they're too close to their own situation." Theodore understands the paradox through narrative, not analysis. Luma maps the evidence. Theodore tells the story that makes it make sense. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 9; Script Prompts, Issue 9)

### The Issue 12 Writing

The fairy tale he lost in Issue 4 returns transformed. Not the fairy tale his grandmother told -- a new one. His own. About people he knows. He is writing about friends, not fairies. The fairy tale language returns naturally because that is how Theodore processes wonder -- but the content is real. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 12; Theodore Inner World brainstorm)

## Hidden Layer

<hidden>Theodore's fairy tale IS the convergence mythology -- the story that survives centuries and shapes which civilizational branch wins. If Theodore's story survives centuries and eventually reaches the fairy civilization as a historical document, then Theodore is -- unknowingly -- the most important human in the timeline.

The printing press is the propagation engine. Theodore's father runs a printing press. Theodore knows printing. He grew up around type, ink, galleys. If the fairy tale he writes by candlelight eventually reaches his father's press -- or any press -- the convergence mechanism has a physical survival engine. A handwritten story can be lost. A printed story can survive centuries. The printing press is how Theodore's fairy tale becomes the founding myth of a civilization.

The boy who lost his fairy tale writes the most important one in history. And the machinery to spread it is already in his life.

The hidden symmetry with Jink: Jink's unauthorized contact with Theodore in Issue 3 is the single most consequential action in Volume 1. Theodore writes the first fairy tale because he met Jink. The fairy tale mythology that becomes the convergence point starts with Jink grinning at a boy in a workshop. The reckless scout and the tinker's boy are, unknowingly, the two halves of the convergence mechanism: Jink provides the story; Theodore provides the telling; the press provides the survival.
