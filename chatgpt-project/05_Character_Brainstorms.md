## 1. Sable's Verbal Habits

### Canon Speech Notes

- "Gives orders, not speeches" (Script Prompts, Issue 1 Dialogue Notes)
- "Clipped authority" (Plot Outline, Issue 2)
- "Sable gives orders. Luma observes." (Script Prompts, Issue 2 Dialogue Notes)
- "Reports truth like a briefing" even while breaking (Script Prompts, Issue 9 Dialogue Notes)
- "Precise questions" — steers what gets discussed (Plot Outline, Issue 8)
- "Something quiet — not a speech. An acknowledgment." (Plot Outline, Issue 12)
- "Every voice distinct. Sable's confession is precise even as it breaks — she reports truth like a briefing." (Script Prompts, Issue 9 Dialogue Notes)

### Verbal Habits

**Short sentences.** Subject, verb, object. Orders are single clauses. No elaboration unless tactically necessary. (Sable backstory brainstorm, Section 5)

**No hedging.** She doesn't say "I think" or "maybe." She says what is, or she says nothing. (Sable backstory brainstorm, Section 5)

**Silence as communication.** She uses silence deliberately. Not answering is a command decision. The squad has learned to read her silences. (Sable backstory brainstorm, Section 5)

**Questions as control.** When she asks questions, they're precise and directed — managing the conversation, not exploring it. (Sable backstory brainstorm, Section 5)

**Military register.** "Position." "Confirm." "Execute." "Hold." Not jargon-heavy, but the structure of military communication. (Sable backstory brainstorm, Section 5)

**Contraction tell.** No contractions in command mode. "Do not" rather than "don't." "We will" rather than "we'll." The formality is the mask. Contractions appear when the mask slips — the shift from "do not" to "don't" is a tell. (Sable backstory brainstorm, Section 5) — LOCKED

### How She Addresses Squad Members

Open question. Canon does not specify whether Sable uses rank, first name, or both depending on context. (Sable backstory brainstorm, Open Questions)

### When the Mask Slips

- Issue 4: Fury at Jink — "Sable's control usually prevents visible anger. Jink got past the mask." (Sable backstory brainstorm, Section 5)
- Issue 8: "Too controlled" — overcompensating, the mask too tight, the effort visible. (Sable backstory brainstorm, Section 5)
- Issue 9: The break. "Reports truth like a briefing" — the mask cracks while still being worn. (Script Prompts, Issue 9)
- Issue 9 final page: "For the first time, her expression isn't commander or burden-carrier. She's lost." (Script Prompts, Issue 9)
- Issue 12: "Something quiet." Not performing command — just Sable looking at her people. (Script Prompts, Issue 12)

### Locked Decisions (formerly Open Questions)

- **No catchphrase or verbal tic** — LOCKED. The ABSENCE of tics is the character. She's too controlled for unconscious habits. The contraction tell (already LOCKED) is the closest — and it's a tell, not a tic.
- **Very rare, very dry humor** — LOCKED. One wry observation in Issue 12, after the squad decides to stay. Not a joke — an observation that happens to be dry. It surprises everyone because they've never heard it. The rarity IS the impact.
- **Last names in command mode, first names when personal** — LOCKED. The shift is a tell, like contractions. "Thresh, take position" vs. a quiet "Thresh" when checking in. Theodore is always "Theodore" — she never shortens it to "Theo" (that's Jink's).
- **Authority that transcends scale** — LOCKED. Four inches tall but sounds like the tallest person in the room. Theodore instinctively addresses her as "ma'am." The authority is in the voice structure, not the volume.

## 2. Wren's Verbal Habits

### Canon Speech Notes

- "Wren reports findings, not feelings." (Script Prompts, Issue 1 Dialogue Notes)
- "She doesn't waste words on feelings when there's a diagnostic to run." (Script Prompts, Issue 1)
- "Impatient with anything that isn't progress toward that goal." (Script Prompts, Issue 2)
- "Technically blunt, practical." (Story Bible)
- "Wren's component list should be specific enough to feel real but not so technical it loses the reader." (Script Prompts, Issue 2 Dialogue Notes)

### Verbal Habits

**Diagnostic narration.** Wren speaks in diagnostics. She describes what she observes, what's wrong, and what to do about it. Her natural mode is the field report. (Wren technology brainstorm, Section 4)

<AI>**Short declarative sentences.** "It's shattered." "I need a gear." "He's useful." Not clipped like Sable (authority compressed) — blunt like a diagnostic readout. (Wren technology brainstorm, Section 4, AI suggestion)</AI>

<AI>**No qualifiers.** She doesn't say "I think" or "maybe" or "it seems like." If she's uncertain, she says "I don't know yet" — which is also a diagnostic. Uncertainty is data, not weakness. (Wren technology brainstorm, Section 4, AI suggestion)</AI>

**Quantifying tic.** She puts numbers on things because numbers are clean. "Three minutes." "Point-one tolerance." "Twelve components." (Wren technology brainstorm, Open Questions) — LOCKED

**"This is elegant."** One sentence that contains everything. The only aesthetic word Wren uses, deployed twice — at the Fresnel lighthouse lens (Issue 7). Not "functional" or "effective" or "precise." For someone who reports findings not feelings, "elegant" is a feeling. (Wren technology brainstorm, Section 4; Plot Outline, Issue 7)

**"This isn't alien. This is us. Backwards."** — Titan discovery (Issue 8). Short declaratives, flat presentation of catastrophic things. (Plot Outline, Issue 8)

**Talks to/about machines.** Canon doesn't establish whether Wren talks to machines conversationally.

<AI>Two options: (a) She narrates her work in short diagnostic phrases — "come on," "there," "hold" — the way a mechanic talks to a stubborn bolt. Not anthropomorphizing, just vocalizing the work. (b) She talks ABOUT machines the way others talk about people: "This clock is honest." "The telegraph is elegant." She attributes qualities to mechanisms because she reads character in engineering choices. (Wren technology brainstorm, Section 4, AI suggestion)</AI>

<AI>**Understatement as humor.** Wren doesn't make jokes. But she says things that are funny because of how flat they are. A ship that's functionally destroyed: "It's fixable." A mechanism that almost killed her: "That gear runs fast." The humor is in the gap between what she says and what it cost. (Wren technology brainstorm, Section 4, AI suggestion)</AI>

### Locked Decisions (formerly Open Questions)

- **Technical jargon constantly** — LOCKED. She forgets others don't share her vocabulary. Jink makes fun of it. The jargon is both future-tech and increasingly Victorian-tech as her fascination arc develops.
- **Split register develops across the arc** — LOCKED. By Issue 7 ("elegant"), she's mixing Victorian technical terms into future vocabulary. The split register IS the fascination made audible.
- **Machine-characterization as second verbal habit** — LOCKED. Beyond quantifying, she talks about machines the way others talk about people: "This clock is honest." "The telegraph is elegant." She attributes qualities to mechanisms because she reads character in engineering choices.
- **Simplifies for Theodore — practically, not condescendingly** — LOCKED. Accurately but without jargon. She explains the way a good engineer explains to a non-specialist. With the squad: shorthand, assumed shared knowledge.

## 4. Thresh's Verbal Habits

### Canon Speech Notes

- "Thresh speaks in short sentences." (Script Prompts, Issue 1 Dialogue Notes)
- "Thresh should speak less than in Issue 1 — the silence IS the character beat." (Script Prompts, Issue 2 Dialogue Notes)
- "Thresh barely speaks." (Script Prompts, Issue 9 Dialogue Notes)

- "Thresh and Wren share economy of words but for different reasons." (Wren technology brainstorm, voice contrast)
- "Military bluntness masking fear" (README)

### Verbal Habits

**Compression, not confidence.** In Issue 1, short sentences are confidence — a soldier who doesn't waste words. By Issue 2, the same short sentences are containment — fewer words because each word might let the fear out. By Issue 9, near-silence. (Thresh fear arc brainstorm, Section 5)

<AI>**Operational vocabulary.** "Contact." "Position." "Threat." "Clear." Words that belong in a briefing. Thresh's conversational language IS his professional language — he has no other register available when the fear is active. (Thresh fear arc brainstorm, Section 5, AI suggestion)</AI>

<AI>**Status reports instead of feelings.** Instead of "I'm terrified," Thresh might say: "This position is exposed." Instead of "I can't do this," it's: "That route is compromised." The person disappears into the soldier. (Thresh fear arc brainstorm, Section 5, AI suggestion)</AI>

<AI>**Negatives leak fear.** "Don't like this." "Not good." "Can't hold here." The fear leaks through as what ISN'T working, what ISN'T safe, what CAN'T be done. The language of limitation. (Thresh fear arc brainstorm, Section 5, AI suggestion)</AI>

**"If Titans are here, running won't help."** The most significant Thresh line before Issue 10. Flat. No emotion. The fear did the calculation and the calculation is done. This is the moment the squad hears Thresh's fear as intelligence rather than weakness. (Plot Outline, Issue 7; Thresh fear arc brainstorm, Section 5)

**Late joke.** One option: a single, very dry, very late joke — Issue 11 or 12, after the combat breakthrough. It would land like a thunderclap because no one has ever heard Thresh be funny. The humor itself signals the change more than any dramatic speech. (Thresh fear arc brainstorm, Open Questions) — LOCKED

<AI>"I don't want to stay. I'll stay." — If this line stands, it's the arc in six words. The first sentence is the fear, stated aloud without tactical cover. The second is the soldier. (Thresh fear arc brainstorm, Section 5, AI suggestion from temporal mechanics brainstorm)</AI>

- "Luma connects." (Script Prompts, Issue 9 Dialogue Notes)

### Verbal Habits

**Three-verb arc: asks, observes, connects, states.** Canon gives Luma three verbs across the volume. These are a progression, not just descriptions. (Luma temptation brainstorm, Section 4)

| Phase | Verb | What It Sounds Like |
|-------|------|---------------------|
| Issues 1-3 | **Asks** | Curiosity. Questions about everything. Some relevant, some not. |
| Issues 4-7 | **Observes** | Investigation. The questions become directed. Casual surface hides purpose. |
| Issues 8-9 | **Connects** | Synthesis beginning. "What if" replaces "what is." |
| Issues 11-12 | **States** | The questions stop. Luma tells. The synthesis is delivered as conclusion. |

(Luma temptation brainstorm, Section 4)

**Questions that do double duty.** Surface level: curiosity, conversation. Below: constructing an evidence chain. The squad may not notice the transition from casual to purposeful because Luma's been asking questions since Issue 1. The habit provides cover. (Luma temptation brainstorm, Section 4)

**"But what if--" qualifying questions.** Signature move. Or: echoing what someone says back as a question, reframing their statement as inquiry. (Luma temptation brainstorm, Open Questions) — LOCKED

**Shift from asking to stating.** The Issue 12 synthesis would be the longest sustained speech from Luma in the volume. Every other time she asks. Here she tells. The change in mode IS the arc beat. (Luma temptation brainstorm, Section 4)

### Locked Decisions (formerly Open Questions)

- **Stops asking in Issue 12** — LOCKED. The shift is gradual (questions narrow from casual to loaded across Issues 4-11) but the STATE CHANGE is Issue 12 when she states instead of asks. The synthesis is the longest sustained speech from Luma in the volume. Every other time she asks; here she tells.
- **Three kinds of questions: medical, investigative, philosophical** — LOCKED. Medical (to Thresh): "What's your breathing?" Investigative (to Theodore): "When did the Tall Ones stories start?" Philosophical (to the squad): "What if we were supposed to be here?" The type tells you what mode she's in.

- **Thresh talk-down (Issue 7): medical first, then personal** — LOCKED. Starts with the medic's steady diagnostic voice ("Your breathing — match mine"). Transitions to something quieter and personal ("You're here. You're standing. That's enough."). She asks questions even here — because questions give Thresh something to answer, which means something to DO.
- **Quiet self-narration while working** — LOCKED. Barely audible. The investigation vocalized for her own benefit. Different from Wren's machine-talking — Luma is narrating observations, not addressing mechanisms.

## 7. Voice Contrast Grid

How each pair sounds in dialogue together. Key contrasts from canon and character brainstorms.

### Sable + Wren

Both precise. Both brief. But military precision vs. technical precision. "Sable chooses few words. Wren is efficient — she says what's needed and stops." Sable's brevity is command; Wren's brevity is diagnostics. The confrontation in Issue 9 is the collision: Wren reads Sable's "system" the way she reads a mechanism. (Wren technology brainstorm, Section 4; Sable backstory brainstorm, Section 3)

### Sable + Jink

Formal vs. informal. Authority compressed into few words vs. many words to say little. "The contrast is volume and density." The tension is in register — Sable gives orders, Jink evades them. (Jink recklessness brainstorm, Section 5; Sable backstory brainstorm, Section 3)

### Jink + Theodore

Future slang meets Victorian English — the comedy and the warmth. "The gap between how they speak is part of the culture clash." (Script Prompts, Issue 3). Speed vs. deliberation. Feeling vs. precision. Jink says what they feel; Theodore describes what he observes. Together they cover what the other misses. Over the volume, they develop a shared vocabulary that belongs only to them. (Jink-Theodore relationship brainstorm, Section 3)

### Jink + Thresh

Quips vs. compression. "Jink uses speech to avoid feeling. Thresh uses silence to contain it." Jink fills silence; Thresh creates it. Jink is fast, many words, deflection. Thresh is slow, few words, containment. Both are managing fear differently — Jink avoids, Thresh compresses. (Thresh fear arc brainstorm, Section 5; Jink recklessness brainstorm, Section 5)

### Wren + Theodore

Technical respect — two engineers, different eras. "They speak the same language of how things work, just centuries apart." Theodore's clockwork vocabulary is most at home with Wren. Theodore is more confident here than with anyone else in the squad — his cleverness is visible. (Script Prompts; Theodore inner world brainstorm, Section 5)

### Luma + Anyone

Questions that make the other character reveal themselves. Luma asks; the other person answers, and in answering reveals more than they intended. With Thresh (Issue 7): Luma talks Thresh down — the medic speaking to the fear directly. With Theodore (Issue 6): genuine curiosity, the investigation partnership, "both in their element." With Wren: both analytical, but Luma asks where Wren states. (Luma temptation brainstorm, Section 4; Wren technology brainstorm, Section 4)

### Thresh + Sable

Operational reports — shortest exchanges, most information. "Both brief. Sable's brevity is command — each word carries rank. Thresh's brevity is compression — each word is squeezed past fear. Sable chooses few words. Thresh has few words left." (Thresh fear arc brainstorm, Section 5)

### Full Squad Scenes

<AI>The ensemble effect: Sable's orders set the frame. Wren's diagnostics fill in the facts. Jink's quips break the tension (or raise it). Thresh's silences carry weight by absence. Luma's questions redirect the conversation. Theodore's Victorian earnestness grounds the scene in a different register entirely. The mix is distinctive because no two characters process information the same way — orders, diagnostics, quips, silence, questions, and wonder all occupy different frequencies.</AI>

# Brainstorm — Department Suit Color Coding

## Problem

All five squad members wear identical metallic silver suits with blue-white holographic accents. At comic-panel or game-thumbnail size, Jink and Wren are nearly interchangeable (similar dark hair, similar builds, same suit). The squad needs instant visual distinction by silhouette AND color.

## Decision — LOCKED

**Department color-coded suits**, inspired by Star Trek's uniform system (gold/red/blue per department), executed as jewel-tone metallic hues. The suit material itself changes color per department — not just accent lights. All suits retain a metallic shimmer quality (jewel-tone metallics, not matte).

| Character | Role | Suit Hue | Holographic Accents | Hair |
|-----------|------|----------|---------------------|------|
| **Sable** | Command | **Silver** (command standard — the original) | Cool blue-white (unchanged) | Silver-white crop |
| **Wren** | Engineer | **Copper** metallic shimmer | Warm copper-gold glow | Black knot |
| **Jink** | Scout | **Emerald** metallic shimmer | Emerald/green glow | **Bright golden blonde** curly (changed from dark) |
| **Thresh** | Soldier | **Ruby/crimson** metallic shimmer | Red/crimson glow | Copper-red buzz |
| **Luma** | Science/Med | **Sapphire/violet** metallic shimmer | Blue-violet glow | Dark braid (luminous filament) |

## Design Rules

- All suits retain metallic shimmer quality — jewel-tone metallics, not matte colors
- Holographic accent lights (joints, wing-pack seams, emitters) glow in a matching or complementary tone per department
- Degradation arc still applies: suits accumulate soot/damage, but the department color shows through grime alongside the holographic glow
- Sable's silver stays as "command standard" — the four department colors are variations from this base
- Wing-pack seam glow matches department color

## Jink Hair Change — LOCKED

Jink's hair changed from "dark curly" to **bright golden blonde curly**. Same wild, voluminous style — just a vivid blonde that immediately distinguishes from Wren's black hair, even at small sizes. Golden blonde + emerald suit = strong contrast and high readability.

## Fairy Tale Echo

Victorian observers would name the squad by their suit colors, feeding directly into the fairy tale echo theme:

- "the silver fairy" (Sable — the leader)
- "the copper fairy" (Wren — always near machinery)

### Three-Tier Reaction Spectrum (established in steampunk tech brainstorm)

**Tier 2 — "Genuinely clever":** Clockwork precision (0.1mm tolerances by hand), optical instruments (Fresnel lens), telegraph signaling ("digital communication with copper wire and a battery"). She sees intelligence constrained by tools.

**Tier 3 — "How is this even possible?":** Jacquard loom (programmable mechanics from punch cards), Bessemer steel (metallurgical transformation from empirical observation), Bazalgette's sewers (city-scale engineering over-built for the future). ### What Fascinates Her — The Underlying Principle

Wren isn't fascinated by WHAT Victorians build. She's fascinated by HOW they build it — solving problems without the tools she takes for granted.

- **Light without photonics.** The Fresnel lens bends light with ground glass — the functional ancestor of holographic optics, built with "sand and fire" (steampunk brainstorm).
- **Information without electronics.** Telegraph: near-light-speed communication using copper wire and a battery. Morse code as a binary-adjacent encoding scheme.
- **Programming without processors.** Jacquard loom: punch cards controlling mechanical patterns. The concept of programmable systems implemented in cardboard and steel pins.

<AI>The fascination escalates because each tier gets closer to what Wren works with every day. Tier 1 is alien to her (thermal energy → mechanical force). Tier 2 is recognizable (precision, optics). ### The Collision

<AI>Specific ways the voices collide:

**Speed vs. deliberation.** Jink talks fast, in fragments, mid-thought. Theodore speaks in complete sentences with Victorian cadence. Jink is already three ideas ahead while Theodore is finishing one. Theodore's pauses feel long to Jink; Jink's speed feels chaotic to Theodore.

**Slang vs. formality.** Jink uses future expressions Theodore can't parse. Theodore uses Victorian phrasing Jink finds quaint. The misunderstandings are a source of humor early and tenderness later — when they start translating for each other instead of past each other.

**Feeling vs. precision.** Jink says what they feel ("this is amazing," "that's terrifying," "let's go"). Theodore describes what he observes ("the mechanism appears to be — ," "I should think it's a species of — ," "the construction suggests — "). <AI>How they start to borrow from each other:

- Shared vocabulary: small borrowings in both directions. Jink picks up a Victorian expression. Theodore picks up a future one. Each borrowed word is a marker of how far the friendship has come.

## 2. Methodology

<AI>**Collection methods for 1885 London:**

Recovery is "mostly reversible" — but Cribbage returns as a "simpler version" of himself (canon, LOCKED).

- **Remembers Theodore but diminished** — LOCKED. He knows Theodore. He knows the workshop. But something is missing — the sharpness, the precision, the unkindness. He's simpler. He calls Theodore "boy" but can't remember why he used to be angry. The shift from "boy, fetch the file" to "Theodore... could you..." is small and enormous.

The master-apprentice relationship is permanently altered.
