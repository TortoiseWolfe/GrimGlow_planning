## 1. What Specifically Terrifies Him

### The User's Three Options

**Scale** — the vulnerability of being tiny in a giant world. Everything mundane is lethal. A pigeon, a cat, a dockworker's boot, a child's hand.

**Primitiveness** — the world itself is alien. No extraction, no comms, no backup. No holographic displays, no temporal corridors, no chain of command above them. The infrastructure that made bravery possible is gone.

**Something personal** — unspecified. A fear that predates the crash or that the crash revealed.

### What Canon Points Toward

Canon anchors the fear in the **gap between who Thresh was and who they are now:**

- "In the future, Thresh was the door-kicker, the first through the breach. Here, a pigeon is a legitimate threat." (Script Prompts, Issue 2)
- "The vulnerability of being tiny in a giant world with no support network" (Story Bible)
- "The squad's heavy hitter, which is ironic given their size relative to humans" (Story Bible)

The irony is structural: Thresh's role is to be the strongest fighter. Their identity is built on being the one who goes in first, the one who stands between danger and the squad. In this world, that identity is meaningless — or worse, a reminder of what they've lost.

### The Fear Beat Table

| Issue | What Triggers Fear | What Kind of Fear |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| 2 | A pigeon. Rain. A stray cat. | Scale — mundane threats become lethal |
| 4 | Theodore's size — "could crush any of them with one hand" | Scale — even allies are dangerous by existing |
| 5 | Exposure on a window ledge — "high above a street, visible, vulnerable" | Vulnerability — no cover, no protection |
| 7 | Dockworker's boot — near-miss | Scale + proximity — almost killed by something that wasn't even aimed at them |
| 8 | A child's gentleness — "freezes — not at the danger but at the gentleness" | Something else entirely |
| 10 | The Titan — "David and Goliath if Goliath were a nightmare" | The enemy they were trained to fight, but at annihilating scale |

### Scale, Infrastructure, or Both?

<AI>The three options aren't mutually exclusive. Consider:

**Scale is the surface.** Everything is too big. That's the fear others can see — the white-knuckle grip, the scanning shadows, the flinch when something moves.

**Infrastructure is the root.** In the future, Thresh was brave because bravery had backup. The door-kicker role assumes: behind you is a squad supported by a command structure, supported by a civilization. You kick the door because extraction exists if it goes wrong. In 1885, there is no extraction. No medical evac. No reinforcements. No temporal corridor home. The door-kicker role without the infrastructure behind it is just a person standing in front of danger alone.

**The personal layer:** Thresh may have been brave in the future because they'd never had reason NOT to be. Bravery that's never been tested against true helplessness isn't courage — it's confidence. The crash didn't make Thresh afraid. It revealed that the bravery was conditional. That's the terrifying part — not the world, but the discovery that the person they thought they were might not exist without the system that created them.

This would make the Issue 10 breakthrough more powerful: "training wins" doesn't mean the infrastructure reasserts itself. It means Thresh finds the bravery that's IN them, not the bravery the system gave them. Real courage, not institutional courage.</AI>

### The Issue 8 Exception

The child encounter breaks the pattern. Every other fear trigger is about danger — the world is big, the world is lethal, the squad is vulnerable. The child is none of those things.

"Thresh freezes — not at the danger but at the gentleness." (Plot Outline)

<AI>Why gentleness terrifies a soldier who can handle danger:
- Danger has a response — training, weapons, protocol. Gentleness has no counter-protocol.
- A child reaching out a hand isn't a threat to Thresh's body. It's a threat to the frame. Thresh has been surviving this world by treating everything in it as hostile. The child breaks the frame — not everything here is trying to kill them. Some of it is kind. And kindness from something that could crush you with one finger is a different kind of overwhelming.
- Thresh's fear has been about vulnerability. The child encounter is vulnerability without threat. The terror is: what does Thresh do with vulnerability when there's nothing to fight?</AI>

### What Theodore Represents

"Thresh watches Theodore and is viscerally aware this child could destroy any of them without meaning to." (Plot Outline, Issue 4)

Theodore is the child-shaped version of everything Thresh fears — enormous, unpredictable, capable of casual destruction. But Theodore is also an ally. Thresh has to trust a being that could kill them by accident.

<AI>The Issue 8 child encounter might reframe Theodore for Thresh. Before Issue 8, Theodore is a useful giant who Thresh tolerates while being viscerally aware of the danger. After Issue 8 — after a child's gentleness stops Thresh cold — Theodore might become something else. Not less dangerous. But the danger might stop being the only thing Thresh sees.</AI>

### Open Questions — What Terrifies Him

- Is the fear purely situational (this world is terrifying) or does it reveal something about who Thresh was before?
- In the future, was Thresh genuinely brave or just never tested?
- Does Thresh fear the Titans differently from the world itself? (The Titans are a trained enemy at wrong scale vs. the world which is an untrained environment at wrong scale)

### The User's Three Suggestions

**Shaking hands** — visible tremor. Involuntary. The body betraying the soldier.

**Over-checking corners** — hypervigilance. Scanning constantly. The trained response to threat amplified past functionality into compulsion.

**Snapping at Jink** — fear expressed as aggression. The tension has to go somewhere, and Jink's recklessness is an easy target.

### Physical vs. Behavioral

Canon already establishes two categories:

**What Thresh's BODY does** (involuntary):
- White-knuckle grip on the weapon
- Eyes scanning constantly
- Shoulders hunching — the posture degradation from clean to mid-mission
- The holographic elements "pulsing faintly like a heartbeat" — do they pulse faster under stress?

**What Thresh DOES** (behavioral):
- Goes silent — fewer words, then no words
- Withdraws — spatial distance from the squad
- Freezes — the Issue 8 child encounter
- Controlled spiral — the Issue 7 near-miss, where the fear overwhelms the behavioral controls

<AI>The soldier's mask — what hides it vs. what fails:

**What hides the fear:**
- Stillness. Thresh is trained to hold a position. Stillness reads as composure from the outside, even when it's freeze response from the inside.
- Routine. Weapons check, perimeter scan, equipment maintenance. Repetitive actions that look professional but might be self-soothing — the hands doing something predictable in an unpredictable world.
- Economy. Short sentences aren't just voice — they're fear management. Say less, reveal less. The silence in Issue 2 isn't composure. It's the fear being held in the jaw.

**What fails to hide it:**
- The grip. White-knuckle is visible. A relaxed soldier holds a weapon loosely. Thresh's grip says everything.
- The scan rate. A soldier checks corners. A terrified soldier checks the same corner twice. Then again. The frequency of the scan reveals what the composure doesn't.
- The flinch that gets suppressed a beat late. Thresh hears a sound and twitches — then stills. The stillness comes, but the flinch came first. Others notice the delay.

The Issue 7 spiral is what happens when ALL the masks fail at once. The near-miss overwhelms every containment strategy simultaneously. It takes Luma — the medic — to talk Thresh back. Not to calm. To functional.</AI>

### The Degradation Arc — Fear Made Visible

Canon describes Thresh's suit degradation: "A long diagonal scratch runs across the chest plate from right shoulder to left hip — deep enough to show the darker underlayer. The left pauldron has a visible dent. Soot and grime darken the suit unevenly." (Turnarounds, mid-mission)

The suit tells the story the voice won't. Every scratch is a hit Thresh absorbed for the squad. The armor isn't just damaged — it's evidence of the cost of being the heavy hitter in a world that's too big.

<AI>Visual idea: the holographic edge-lighting on Thresh's armor pulses in sync with fear. Canon says it pulses "like a heartbeat." Under stress, the pulse might quicken, flicker, or grow uneven — a tell that reads as magical malfunction to Victorian eyes but is actually a soldier's heart rate visible in light. The one panel on the right forearm that's "dark entirely" (Turnarounds) could be the first system to fail under sustained stress.</AI>

## 3. The Transformation — Fighting Afraid

### The Arc Statement

"Thresh doesn't stop being afraid. They learn to act anyway, which is a different and harder kind of bravery." (Plot Outline, Arc Threads)

The user's framing: not fearless, but "trained action despite fear."

### Who Thresh Was Before

Issue 1: "Confident, composed, competent — the person you'd want beside you in a fight. This is who Thresh WAS. The fear that defines their arc in this world hasn't started yet. Show us the 'before' so the 'after' hits harder." (Script Prompts)

<AI>What "before" bravery felt like — the easy kind:
- Thresh was the door-kicker. First through the breach. That takes courage, but it's courage supported by infrastructure: backup, extraction, medical, comms, chain of command.
- In the future, Thresh didn't have to think about being brave. It was muscle memory inside a system that made bravery survivable.
- The "before" is important because it's what Thresh is mourning. They didn't lose courage — they lost the version of themselves where courage was the default. The gap between who they were and who they are in this world is the fear itself.</AI>

### The Issue 7 Surprise

"Thresh, surprising everyone, sides with Jink — if Titans are here, running won't help." (Plot Outline)

This is a critical beat. Thresh, the most afraid, agrees with Jink, the most reckless. Canon notes this is unexpected.

<AI>What Thresh's reasoning might be — from inside the fear:

Everyone else in the debrief is arguing tactics. Sable says: mission is repair and extraction, not engagement. Jink says: we should investigate. But Thresh's position comes from a different place than either of them.

Thresh is the one who understands threat best because Thresh is the one who's been afraid of EVERYTHING since they arrived. The dockworker's boot. The cat. The rain. The pigeon. All of these were threats. And Thresh has been right about that — the world IS dangerous at this scale. Now Jink has seen something bigger, meaner, and actively hunting them.

"If Titans are here, running won't help" isn't bravery. It's fear doing the math. Thresh knows what happens to a prey animal that runs from a predator that's already tracking it. The fear response says: better to know where it is than to hope it goes away.

This is the first moment where fear and tactical intelligence produce the same answer. Thresh doesn't stop being afraid. But the fear is thinking.</AI>

### The Turning Point — Issue 10

"Thresh engages — and this is Thresh's moment, because the fear that's been consuming them meets the thing they were trained to do, and training wins. Thresh doesn't stop being afraid. They fight afraid." (Plot Outline)

"Scale between Thresh and Titan is the defining image: David and Goliath if Goliath were a nightmare." (Script Prompts)

### What "Training Wins" Means

<AI>Three layers of what this could mean:

**Muscle memory.** The body knows what to do even when the mind is screaming. Raise weapon. Aim. Fire. Move to cover. Reload. The training is in the hands, not the head. The fear is in the head, not the hands. They can coexist.

**Identity recovery.** Thresh was the door-kicker. For eight issues, that identity has been stripped away by a world that mocks it. The Titan encounter restores the context — this is the enemy Thresh was trained to fight. Wrong scale, wrong world, wrong odds. But the fight itself is familiar. Training "wins" because the Titan gives Thresh back the frame they lost in the crash.

**The fear becoming fuel.** Not suppressed, not overcome — used. Adrenaline is adrenaline whether it comes from courage or terror. A terrified soldier who acts anyway moves faster, scans harder, reacts sharper than a calm one. "Fighting afraid" isn't a failure mode. It's an operational state. The fear doesn't go away. It becomes the engine.</AI>

### The Issue 8 Turn — The Other Kind of Breakthrough

The child encounter (Issue 8) comes BEFORE the combat breakthrough (Issue 10). The arc isn't: afraid → fights → brave. It's: afraid → gentleness breaks through → fights afraid.

<AI>Why the child encounter matters for the combat transformation:

The child doesn't threaten Thresh. The child reaches out a hand. And Thresh freezes — "not at the danger but at the gentleness."

Every fear trigger before this has been about the world being hostile. The child breaks that pattern. Not everything here is trying to kill them. The child's hand is an offer of connection from the very world Thresh has been terrified of.

If the child encounter cracks the frame — if Thresh can no longer sustain "everything here is hostile" as a worldview — then the Issue 10 combat encounter happens to a Thresh who has already been changed. Not less afraid. But aware that the fear isn't about the world. It's about Thresh. The world contains children who reach out and Titans who destroy. Thresh can't control which they encounter. They can control whether they act.

The transformation sequence: the child teaches Thresh the world isn't all threat. The Titan teaches Thresh they can fight despite the threat. Together: the world is complicated, and Thresh can function in it.</AI>

### The End State — Issue 12

"Thresh holding a position against a Titan drone." "Thresh is overwhelmed. Jink diverts to help." (Plot Outline, Issue 12)

"Fear joined by purpose." (Script Prompts, Issue 11)

<AI>"I don't want to stay. I'll stay." (temporal mechanics brainstorm, AI suggestion)

If this line stands, it's the arc in six words. Not "I'm not afraid anymore." Not "I choose to be brave." Just: I don't want this. I'll do it anyway. That's what "fighting afraid" sounds like when it becomes a life choice instead of a combat moment.</AI>

### The Bravery Distinction

The user's framing: not fearless, but "trained action despite fear."

Canon draws the same line: "Thresh doesn't stop being afraid. They learn to act anyway, which is a different and harder kind of bravery." (Plot Outline)

The distinction matters because:
- Fearlessness is absence. Fighting afraid is presence — of both the fear and the action.
- Fearlessness can't be sustained in a world where everything is genuinely lethal. Fighting afraid can, because it doesn't require the fear to go away.
- Issue 1 Thresh was brave. Issue 10 Thresh is courageous. The difference is that Issue 10 Thresh knows the cost and pays it.

## 4. Thresh and Sable

### The User's Two Options

**Resent her calm** — Sable doesn't seem afraid. She gives orders, makes decisions, holds composure. For Thresh, who is fighting fear every moment, Sable's calm could feel like a reproach — either she's stronger, or she doesn't understand what it costs.

**Depend on it** — Sable's calm is the last piece of infrastructure. In a world where everything that made Thresh brave is gone, the captain's composure is the one remaining structure. Thresh follows orders because orders are the framework that holds the fear in check.

### What Canon Establishes

"Thresh is the squad member Sable has to worry about the least and the most. The least: he follows orders, holds positions, does his job. The most: he's afraid. Constantly. And Sable can see it." (Sable brainstorm)

<AI>"Sable's responsibility to Thresh: she's the one who has to send him into danger knowing how much it costs him. Does she protect him by keeping him back? Or does she honor him by trusting him with the hard tasks?" (Sable brainstorm, AI suggestion)</AI>

"Thresh, surprising everyone, sides with Jink" in the Issue 7 debrief — the first time Thresh deviates from Sable's position (Plot Outline).

"Thresh withdraws further" after Sable's secret is revealed in Issue 9 (Plot Outline).

### Both, Not Either

<AI>Resent and depend aren't exclusive. Consider the progression:

**Issues 1-4: Depend.** Sable is the chain of command. Thresh follows orders because orders are structure, and structure is the only thing between Thresh and the fear. Sable's calm is a lifeline — not warm, not comforting, but reliable. As long as Sable is composed, the world has a framework.

**Issues 5-7: Strain.** Thresh is getting worse. The fear deepens. Sable's calm doesn't change — and that starts to grate. Is she not seeing how bad it is? Does she not care? Or is she just better at this? The dependence is still there, but resentment creeps in at the edges. The Issue 7 surprise — siding with Jink against Sable — might be the first moment Thresh acts on the resentment. Not consciously. Thresh isn't rebelling. But the fear has done its own math, and Sable's answer (run, avoid, stick to the mission) doesn't solve the problem.

**Issue 9: Break.** Sable lied. She held back the mission file. The one piece of infrastructure Thresh was depending on — the captain who knows the mission, who has the plan, who holds the composure — was built on withheld information. "Thresh withdraws further." The withdrawal isn't anger like Jink's. It's the loss of the last structure. If Sable was hiding things, then the composure isn't reliable. The framework Thresh was standing on had a crack in it the whole time.

**Issues 10-12: Something new.** After the combat breakthrough, Thresh's relationship with Sable shifts. Thresh doesn't need Sable's calm the same way — they've found something in themselves that holds without external structure. The dependence becomes something more like respect between soldiers who both know the cost. Sable sends Thresh into danger (Issue 10). Thresh goes. Not because orders, but because the fear has been joined by purpose.</AI>

### What Sable's Calm Looks Like from Thresh's Position

<AI>From the outside, Sable is: composed, clipped, in control. She gives orders. She doesn't show fear.

From Thresh's position — the position of someone who is afraid every moment — Sable's composure could read as:
- **Strength** Thresh doesn't have. She can do this. Why can't I?
- **Denial** Thresh can't afford. She's not afraid. That means she's not paying attention. Or she's not like me.
- **Performance.** What if she's afraid too, and she's just better at hiding it? That would be both reassuring (I'm not alone) and terrifying (if even Sable is scared, how bad IS it?).
- **The last wall.** As long as Sable is composed, the squad holds. If Sable breaks, everything breaks. Thresh might be watching Sable's composure the way a soldier watches fortifications — checking for cracks, not because they want them, but because they need to know they'll hold.</AI>

### Open Questions — Sable

- Does Thresh ever acknowledge the fear directly to Sable? Or is it the thing they both know and never name?
- Does Sable adjust Thresh's assignments because of the fear? (Keeping him back from the most dangerous positions? Or deliberately putting him forward because holding back would confirm the fear?)
- The Issue 10 moment — does Sable give Thresh the engagement order? Or does Thresh engage without orders? <AI>If Sable orders it: she trusts Thresh with the hardest task. If Thresh engages independently: the fear has been joined by something that doesn't wait for orders.</AI>
- After Issue 9, does Thresh ever trust Sable's composure the same way again?

## 5. Voice — Military Shorthand and What Leaks Through

### What Canon Establishes

- "Thresh speaks in short sentences." (Script Prompts, Issue 1)
- "Thresh should speak less than in Issue 1 — the silence IS the character beat." (Script Prompts, Issue 2)
- "Thresh barely speaks." (Script Prompts, Issue 9)
- "Short sentences. Fear compressed into silence." (Wren brainstorm)
- "Military bluntness masking fear" (README)

### The Pattern: Compression

Thresh's voice is defined by what gets compressed out. In Issue 1, short sentences are confidence — a soldier who doesn't waste words. By Issue 2, the same short sentences are containment — fewer words because each word might let the fear out. By Issue 9, near-silence — not confidence, not containment, just nothing left to say.

The user's observation: blunt, military shorthand, but fear leaks through in word choice.

### What Military Shorthand Sounds Like

<AI>Possible speech characteristics:
- **Operational vocabulary.** "Contact." "Position." "Threat." "Clear." Words that belong in a briefing. Thresh's conversational language is his professional language — he has no other register available when the fear is active.
- **Status reports instead of feelings.** Instead of "I'm terrified," Thresh might say: "This position is exposed." Instead of "I can't do this," it's: "That route is compromised." The fear translates into tactical assessment. The person disappears into the soldier.
- **Negatives.** "Don't like this." "Not good." "Can't hold here." The fear leaks through as what ISN'T working, what ISN'T safe, what CAN'T be done. The language of limitation.
- **No qualifiers.** Like Sable, Thresh doesn't hedge. But where Sable's lack of hedging is authority, Thresh's is compression — no room for "maybe" when every sentence is already being squeezed through fear.</AI>

### The Issue 7 Voice

"If Titans are here, running won't help."

This is the most significant Thresh line in the volume before the potential Issue 12 resolution. It comes during the heated debrief, where Jink wants to investigate and Sable shuts it down. Thresh siding with Jink "surprises everyone."

<AI>What this line sounds like:
