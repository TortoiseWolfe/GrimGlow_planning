## 1. Inventions That Fascinate Her

### The Admiration Arc (canon beats)

### Three-Tier Reaction Spectrum (established in steampunk tech brainstorm)

**Tier 1 — "Brute force but works":** Steam engines, cast iron, industrial manufacturing. She respects the scale but not the method.

**Tier 2 — "Genuinely clever":** Clockwork precision (0.1mm tolerances by hand), optical instruments (Fresnel lens), telegraph signaling ("digital communication with copper wire and a battery"). She sees intelligence constrained by tools.

**Tier 3 — "How is this even possible?":** Jacquard loom (programmable mechanics from punch cards), Bessemer steel (metallurgical transformation from empirical observation), Bazalgette's sewers (city-scale engineering over-built for the future). These challenge her assumption that intelligence requires advanced tools.

### What Fascinates Her — The Underlying Principle

Wren isn't fascinated by WHAT Victorians build. She's fascinated by HOW they build it — solving problems without the tools she takes for granted.

- **Precision without computation.** Clockwork tolerances of 0.1mm achieved with files and loupes. No digital measurement, no holographic calibration.
- **Light without photonics.** The Fresnel lens bends light with ground glass — the functional ancestor of holographic optics, built with "sand and fire" (steampunk brainstorm).
- **Information without electronics.** Telegraph: near-light-speed communication using copper wire and a battery. Morse code as a binary-adjacent encoding scheme.
- **Programming without processors.** Jacquard loom: punch cards controlling mechanical patterns. The concept of programmable systems implemented in cardboard and steel pins.

<AI>The fascination escalates because each tier gets closer to what Wren works with every day. Tier 1 is alien to her (thermal energy → mechanical force). Tier 2 is recognizable (precision, optics). Tier 3 is uncanny — she sees the SEED of her own technology in materials she'd consider junk. The Jacquard loom in particular: "The principle is identical. Only the substrate is different."</AI>

### The Progression: From Parts Bin to Lineage

- Issues 2-4: Wren sees Victorian tech as a **parts bin.** Components to extract and adapt. The human world is a supply chain.
- Issues 5-7: Wren starts seeing **engineering.** Not just parts but principles — the clockwork's precision, the Fresnel lens's elegance. She stops categorizing and starts studying.
- Issues 10-12: Wren sees **lineage.** The telegraph is the conceptual parent of everything she works with. Theodore's hands are doing what her hands do. The past isn't primitive — it's the beginning of her.

<AI>This progression could be mirrored in how she talks about Victorian tech:
- Early: "It'll work." (functional assessment)
- Middle: "That's clever." (respect)
- Late: "That's the same thing." (recognition)</AI>

## 2. When Admiration Becomes Dangerous

### The Canon Tension

- Sable wants zero human interaction. Wren makes "the pragmatic case" for using Theodore (Plot Outline, Issue 4). This is the first policy clash.
- "Direct tension" between Wren and Sable over human interaction (Story Bible)
- Wren's pragmatism is her justification, but her growing admiration adds another motive she may not acknowledge

### The Line

The mission protocol is clear: no interaction with the past. Observe, repair, leave. But Wren's engineering role makes this impossible — she needs human-made components, which means engaging with human technology, which means understanding it, which means admiring it.

**Where the line is:**
- **Studying** — acceptable. Scanning tech, analyzing mechanisms, extracting components. This is the mission.
- **Adapting** — necessary. Taking Victorian parts and modifying them to work with fairy tech. Still the mission.
- **Collaborating** — grey zone. Directing Theodore to acquire or manipulate components. Using a human as a tool? Or as a partner?
- **Teaching** — dangerous. Does Wren ever explain to Theodore HOW something works, not just WHAT to do?
- **Improving** — forbidden? If Wren fixes a mechanism that isn't part of her ship repair, she's changing the past.

### The Specific Danger Moments

**Issue 4 — Theodore's charging cradle:** He built something that works. Wren didn't teach him how — he figured it out. But does she tell him WHY it works? Does she correct his design? The temptation to improve on someone else's ingenuity is an engineer's reflex.

**Issue 5 — The clockmaker's shop:** "Page 22: Wren integrating the gear. Her holographic schematic now has a human clockwork diagram open beside it. She's studying both." (Script Prompts). She's not just using the gear — she's studying how the clockmaker achieved the precision. This is admiration becoming education (self-education, but still).

**Issue 10 — The telegraph/feedback pulse:** "Wren rigs a feedback pulse through the telegraph wires that scrambles the Titan's cloaking." She uses Victorian infrastructure as a weapon platform. This is the most active collaboration with human technology in the volume — she's not extracting a component, she's USING the system as built. <AI>Does this count as "improving"? She's adding a fairy-tech signal to the telegraph network. If any trace of that signal persists, she's left future-tech fingerprints in Victorian infrastructure.</AI>

**Issue 12 — Theodore's disarm:** "Wren talks him through the disarm over a holographic comm link." She's telling a human how to manipulate alien technology. She's teaching. <AI>This is the most direct protocol violation in the volume, but it's also the moment her arc completes. The mission REQUIRES collaboration. The line she crossed isn't a failure — it's the point.</AI>

### The Unacknowledged Motive

Wren's pragmatism is real. But underneath it:

- She WANTS to engage with Victorian tech. The admiration isn't just professional respect — it's pleasure. She likes this work.
- Her most worn suit isn't just from hard labor — it's from going deeper, staying longer, touching more of the human world than the mission strictly requires
- <AI>The caliper jury-rigged into her magnetic mount (Turnarounds) might be more than field expediency. She could have improvised from fairy tech. She chose a Victorian tool. That choice might be the most honest thing about Wren.</AI>

### The Paradox Layer

<hidden>If Wren's improvements to Victorian technology persist — if Theodore remembers what she taught him, if the feedback pulse leaves a trace in the telegraph, if any of her adaptations influence human engineering — then she has become part of the causal loop. Her engineering is feeding forward. The same way the fairy tales feed forward through culture, Wren's technical interactions feed forward through technology. She might be an unacknowledged channel in the loop: not stories, but methods.</hidden>

## 3. Engineering Mind Meets the Paradox

### Two Shocks, Not One

Wren gets hit with two revelations, and they're different:

**Shock 1 — Titan discovery (Issue 8):** "This isn't alien. This is us. Backwards." Wren analyzes the Titan nudge engine and recognizes fairy engineering principles — inverted. Same physics, opposite application. This is a TECHNICAL shock — her understanding of the enemy changes completely. The "dark mirror" is literal in the technology.

**Shock 2 — The temporal paradox (Issue 12):** Luma's synthesis — the squad IS the convergence point. The fairy tales ARE the mission. This is a PHILOSOPHICAL shock — it reframes everything Wren has done.

### How an Engineer Processes a Paradox

Luma sees the paradox as a scientist — patterns, evidence, hypothesis. Wren sees it as an engineer — systems, causation, feedback.

<AI>Wren's framework would be different from Luma's:

**Luma asks:** "What does this MEAN?"
**Wren asks:** "How does this WORK?"

For Wren, the causal loop is a feedback system. She understands feedback — she works with it daily. A governor on a steam engine. A Watt regulator. The escapement in a clock. The feedback pulse she rigged through the telegraph. All feedback systems. The temporal paradox is just... a very large one.

Her resistance in Issue 12 — "That's not how technology works. Stories don't build civilizations" — breaks down because she's already proven herself wrong. The telegraph shapes which innovations succeed. Stories shape values. Values shape priorities. Priorities shape technology. She literally used the telegraph as a weapon because she understood that information flow controls development. Luma is just applying the same principle at a longer timescale.</AI>

### The Clockwork Realization

<AI>The metaphor that might break through for Wren: clockwork.

A clock doesn't create time. It measures and organizes it. The mechanism responds to a force (mainspring, gravity) that exists independently. The clock is a feedback system that shapes how humans experience something that was always there.

The squad is the clock. The causal loop is the force. They don't create the fairy tales — the fairy tales emerge from their presence the way time emerges from a clock's mechanism. The squad shapes how the stories flow, but the stories were always going to happen because the squad was always going to be here.

Wren understands clocks. She might understand the loop through the same framework.</AI>

### What Changes for Her

Before the paradox: Wren is fixing the ship to GO HOME. Every component is a step toward departure. The Victorian world is a resource to be used and left behind.

After the paradox: if the squad is supposed to be here, then everything Wren has built — every hybrid, every adaptation, every jury-rigged repair — isn't a step toward leaving. It's part of the infrastructure of the convergence point. She hasn't been preparing to leave. She's been building the mechanism that holds the loop in place.

<AI>This is devastating for an engineer. She thought she was building a way out. She was building a way to stay. Every component she adapted is a commitment she didn't know she was making.</AI>

### Wren and the Telegraph — The Argument She Lost to Herself

Canon: In Issue 12, Wren pushes back on Luma's synthesis — "stories don't build civilizations." But she already proved Luma's point in Issue 10.

Wren understood that the telegraph controls which innovations succeed. That's the Titan strategy, and she understood it as an engineer. "The telegraph is the nervous system of the industrial age; control it, and you control which innovations succeed and which die." (Plot Outline, Issue 11)

But what is the telegraph carrying? Stories. Reports. Information. The same mechanism Wren used to disrupt Titan cloaking — electrical signals through a copper network — is the mechanism that carries the fairy tales, the folklorist's pamphlet, the newspaper reports. The telegraph doesn't distinguish between technical data and mythology. It carries all of it.

Wren argued herself into understanding the paradox two issues before she knew it existed. Her Issue 10 action was the proof of Luma's Issue 12 theory.

<AI>The moment Wren stops arguing in Issue 12 might be the moment she realizes this. She went silent not because she was convinced by Luma's rhetoric, but because she traced the logic back to her own work and found it sound.</AI>

## 4. Voice — How Wren Talks

### Canon References (sparse)

- "Technically blunt, practical" (Story Bible)
- "Reports findings, not feelings" (Script Prompts)
- "Doesn't waste words on feelings when there's a diagnostic to run" (Script Prompts)
- "Impatient with anything that isn't progress toward that goal" (Script Prompts)
- "This is elegant." (Issue 7, Fresnel lens — Plot Outline)
- "This isn't alien. This is us. Backwards." (Issue 8, Titan discovery — Plot Outline)

### Speech Pattern

Wren speaks in **diagnostics.** She describes what she observes, what's wrong, and what to do about it. Her natural mode is the field report.

<AI>Possible speech characteristics:
- **Short declarative sentences.** "It's shattered." "I need a gear." "He's useful." Not clipped like Sable (which is authority compressed) — blunt like a diagnostic readout.
- **Technical vocabulary but not jargon.** She knows precise terms but uses plain language with the squad. She saves the technical vocabulary for when precision matters — "0.1 millimetre tolerance" not "really precise."
- **No qualifiers.** She doesn't say "I think" or "maybe" or "it seems like." She assesses and states. If she's uncertain, she says "I don't know yet" — which is also a diagnostic. Uncertainty is data, not weakness.
- **Questions are operational.** "What's the clearance?" "How long do we have?" "Can he reach it?" She doesn't ask rhetorical questions or hypotheticals.</AI>

### The Humor Question

User asks: dry humor? talks to machines?

<AI>Options for humor:
- **Understatement.** She describes catastrophic things flatly. A ship that's functionally destroyed: "It's fixable." A mechanism that almost killed her: "That gear runs fast." The humor is in the gap between what she says and what it cost.
- **Comparison.** She rates things against each other — Victorian vs. fairy tech, one component vs. another. "Better than the last one." "This would take me an hour with proper tools." The humor comes from the absurdity of comparing future technology to Victorian equivalents.
- **Not jokes.** Wren doesn't make jokes. But she says things that are funny because of how flat they are. Jink makes jokes. Wren makes assessments that happen to be hilarious.</AI>

### Talks to Machines?

The user suggests this. Canon doesn't establish it.

<AI>Two options:
- **Yes, but not conversationally.** She narrates her work in short diagnostic phrases — "come on," "there," "hold" — the way a mechanic talks to a stubborn bolt. Not anthropomorphizing, just vocalizing the work. This would be intimate and distinctive.
- **No, but she talks ABOUT machines the way others talk about people.** "This clock is honest." "The telegraph is elegant." "That engine is trying too hard." She attributes qualities to mechanisms because she reads character in engineering choices. This is her version of empathy.</AI>

### When Does the Mask Slip?

Wren's "mask" isn't emotional control (that's Sable). It's technical detachment — treating everything as a system to diagnose.

<AI>The mask slips when she encounters something she can't reduce to a system:

- **Issue 5 — The pause.** "She pauses, studying the clockwork around her." The pause is the slip. Wren doesn't pause. She assesses, acts, moves. Pausing means something has caught her — not as a component but as a thing worth looking at.
- **Issue 7 — "This is elegant."** She uses an aesthetic word. Not "functional" or "effective" or "precise." Elegant. She's not diagnosing. She's appreciating. For someone who "reports findings, not feelings," this is a feeling.
- **Issue 9 — The confrontation.** She confronts Sable "like an engineer reads a system." But underneath the methodology, she's acting on emotional truth: she cares about the squad, she knows something's wrong, and she can't stay quiet anymore. The system-reading is the mask. The confrontation is the person.
- **Issue 12 — Theodore's hands.** "Oil-stained, burn-scarred, trained on clockwork." She's not assessing his utility anymore. She's seeing him. The description isn't diagnostic — it's respectful. It's the same way she described the Fresnel lens: not what it does, but what it IS.</AI>

### Voice Arc

<AI>How Wren's speech might change across the volume:

| Phase | Voice | Example |
|-------|-------|---------|
| Issues 1-3 | Diagnostic, dismissive of Victorian tech | "It'll work. Barely." |
| Issues 4-5 | Diagnostic, surprised into respect | "...He built this?" / "The engineering is beautiful." |
| Issues 7-8 | Diagnostic giving way to appreciation | "This is elegant." / "This isn't alien. This is us." |
| Issues 9-10 | System-reading applied to people, not just machines | Confronts Sable by reading her "system" |
| Issue 12 | Diagnostic and personal merged | Sees Theodore's hands as both capable tools and human expression |

The arc is: she starts by separating findings from feelings. She ends by recognizing they're the same thing.</AI>

### Voice Contrast with the Squad

- **Sable:** Clipped authority. Gives orders. Few words, each one carries rank. Wren and Sable are both brief — Sable from command, Wren from efficiency.
- **Jink:** Quick, informal, jokes in inappropriate moments. Wren's opposite — feelings first, assessment later.
- **Thresh:** Short sentences. Fear compressed into silence. Thresh and Wren share economy of words but for different reasons.
- **Luma:** Precise, questioning, connects patterns. Luma and Wren are closest in speech — both analytical. But Luma asks questions where Wren makes statements.
