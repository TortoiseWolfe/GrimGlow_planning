## 1. Before the Mission — Military Career

### What Canon Gives Us

Two facts: "career officer" and "from a military family." Everything else is open.

Also: "crisis management is her training" (Script Prompts, Issue 2). She's been trained for situations where things go wrong fast.

### The Military Family

<AI>Options for what "military family" means:
- **Generational service.** Her parents, grandparents served. Command is a family expectation, not a personal discovery. She was raised inside the institution.
- **One influential parent.** A mother or father who was a high-ranking officer. Sable grew up watching how command works from the inside — the cost of it, the loneliness of it. She chose it anyway.
- **Legacy pressure.** The family name carries weight. She has to be better than her surname, not just as good. The silver-white hair as "deliberate choice" could be her rejection of blending in — she refuses to be anonymous inside her own family's institution.</AI>

### What Shaped Her Command Style

Sable gives orders, not speeches. She's clipped, precise, controlled. This suggests training that values efficiency over charisma. Her command style is:

- **Decision-first.** She decides, then communicates. No open discussion unless she invites it. (Issue 4: "relents, with conditions" — she allows input but the decision is hers.)
- **Information management.** She controls what the squad knows. (The mission file sequence from Issues 1-11.) This isn't deception — it's a trained response. Compartmentalization is doctrine.
- **Emotional suppression as discipline.** "Breaks. Not dramatically — she's too disciplined for that." (Plot Outline, Issue 9). She treats emotion as a failure of composure, not a source of information.

<AI>What prior commands might have shaped this:
- **A command where transparency got people killed.** She shared too much, the squad panicked, someone died. She learned: control the information, control the outcome. The mission file secrecy isn't just training — it's scar tissue.
- **A command where she was the XO, not the captain.** She watched a commander above her make choices she disagreed with — and saw those choices work. She learned that command sometimes means acting on incomplete understanding and trusting the process. Now she IS the process, and there's no one above her.
- **Never lost a squad member.** Her record is clean. The pressure of this mission is that it's the first time failure is genuinely possible — and there's no extraction, no backup, no second chance.</AI>

### The Silver-White Hair

"A deliberate choice, not age." This detail is specific enough to be significant.

<AI>What the deliberate choice might mean:
- A military tradition — marking rank, specialization, or a completed campaign
- A personal statement — visible from a distance, unmistakable. She wants to be seen. Command requires being identifiable.
- A memorial — honoring someone or something. The color means something to her that isn't explained.
- Simple aesthetics — she chose it because she wanted to. Not everything needs a trauma origin.</AI>

- **Two years commanding this squad** — LOCKED. Cross-reference: main Open Questions section below.

She makes the team she has work. This matters for the crash: she's not leading people she selected for this situation. She's leading people she was given for a different mission entirely.

## 2. The Mission File — What She Knows

### The Revelation Sequence (Canon)

The mission file unfolds across the volume. This is the most precisely mapped element of Sable's story:

| Issue | What Happens | What She Knows After |
|-------|-------------|---------------------|
| 1 | Checks data module privately; expression changes | Something is wrong with the mission parameters |
| 2 | Checks again; "tightens her expression" | Sees fragments: "CONVERGENCE," "PRIOR" (Script Prompts) |
| 6 | Accesses corrupted file; "reaction is severe"; encrypts it | Something catastrophic — file is corrupted, details fragmentary. Enough to know this is far worse than recon. Not enough for the full picture. |
| 8 | Reaction to Luma's findings "too controlled" | Realizes Luma's glyph discovery connects to the prior insertions she already knows about |
| 9 | Wren confronts her; she shares the file | The squad now knows what she knows. But she STILL doesn't know the operational specifics — destroyed in the crash. |
| 11 | Closes the mission file "for the last time" | Accepts that the specifics don't matter anymore. The mission is what it is. |

### Why She Hides It

Canon says she "held back the truth to protect her team's focus." But there's more to it:

- **Training.** Compartmentalization is doctrine. You don't share classified information with subordinates who don't need it for their current task. She's doing what she was trained to do.
- **Protection.** If the squad knew they were on an intervention mission with no extraction plan, morale would collapse. She's protecting them from information that would paralyze them.
- **Fear.** She doesn't know what the mission IS. The operational details were destroyed. She's hiding the file partly because sharing "we're on a critical mission but I don't know what it is" would be worse than silence. A captain who doesn't know the mission is worse than a captain who's keeping secrets.

<AI>The deepest layer: she hides it because admitting she doesn't know the mission means admitting she can't lead them to its completion. The file is evidence of her own inadequacy as a commander — she has half the picture and no way to complete it. The secrecy isn't about the squad. It's about her.</AI>

### The Issue 6 Moment

"Reaction is severe" when she accesses the full corrupted file. This is the moment she learns the mission was intervention, not reconnaissance.

<AI>What "severe" looks like for Sable:
- She's alone. The mask can slip with no witnesses.
- She encrypts it immediately — muscle memory. Even her reaction to devastating news is procedural.
- The severity: she realizes prior operatives were sent to this convergence point and none reported back. She is leading her squad into something that has swallowed every team before them.</AI>

### The Issue 9 Break

"Sable breaks. Not dramatically — she's too disciplined for that. But she shares the file."

This is the arc's hinge point. What happens inside Sable:

- Wren's confrontation isn't a surprise — Sable has been expecting it. She's been waiting to be caught, maybe hoping for it. The secret is a weight she didn't want to carry alone.
- "Reports truth like a briefing" — even in breaking, she's a commander. The information comes out organized, structured, presented. She can't not command.
- <AI>The real break isn't sharing the file. It's admitting "I don't know what the mission is." That sentence costs her more than any secret. It's a captain saying: I'm lost too.</AI>

### What She Doesn't Know

<hidden>She doesn't know the dark fairy operation is led by herself — older, from a failed future iteration of the timeline. Alt-Sable lived through everything our Sable is experiencing: trusted her squad, chose cooperation, stayed, fought the story war. It wasn't enough. Her squad died. She came back to try control instead of trust. That revelation belongs to a future volume. But the groundwork is here: Sable's command decisions are replayed by alt-Sable's decisions throughout Volume 1 — because alt-Sable IS Sable and made the same calls first. The reader doesn't see this yet. Sable doesn't see this yet. When she does, every Volume 1 decision becomes a replay, not just a mirror. (Alt-Sable brainstorm, Origin — LOCKED.)</hidden>

## 3. Squad Relationships — One by One

### Sable and Wren

**Canon dynamic:** "Direct tension" over human interaction (Story Bible). Wren is the one who confronts Sable about the mission file (Issue 9). Wren "reads systems, and Sable's system doesn't add up" (Plot Outline).

- Wren is Sable's most capable squad member and the one most likely to challenge her. This is a command tension — Sable needs Wren's competence but Wren's independence threatens Sable's control model.
- The human interaction disagreement: Sable wants zero contact. Wren makes the pragmatic case for using Theodore. This isn't just a tactical disagreement — it's about what kind of commander Sable is. Rigid doctrine or adaptive leadership?
- <AI>Underneath the tension: mutual respect. Wren confronts Sable not to undermine her but because Wren cares about the squad's survival and she knows Sable is hiding something that affects it. The confrontation in Issue 9 is an act of loyalty, not rebellion. Sable might recognize this even as it cuts her.</AI>

### Sable and Jink

**Canon dynamic:** "Furious at Jink, terrified of the exposure" when Jink brings Theodore to the squad (Issue 4). Considers Jink-Theodore friendship a "liability" (Issue 7). Jink is "furious at being lied to" after Issue 9.

- Jink is the youngest, most impulsive, and most likely to break protocol. Jink represents everything Sable's command style can't contain — instinct over procedure, connection over caution.
- The Theodore breach is the first moment Sable's authority doesn't hold. Jink acted. Sable can't undo it.
- <AI>What Jink might represent to Sable: the part of command she's suppressed — the impulse to act on what feels right rather than what protocol dictates. Jink's fury in Issue 9 is the one that stings most because Jink trusted Sable the way a soldier trusts a commander, and Sable betrayed that trust by exactly the kind of calculated decision Sable prides herself on.</AI>

### Sable and Thresh

**Canon dynamic:** Less developed than other relationships. Thresh "sides with Jink" in the Issue 7 debate "surprising everyone" — including, presumably, Sable. Thresh is "functional but brittle."

- Thresh is the squad member Sable has to worry about the least and the most. The least: he follows orders, holds positions, does his job. The most: he's afraid. Constantly. And Sable can see it.
- <AI>The Issue 7 surprise — Thresh siding with Jink — might be the first time Sable realizes she's misjudged him. She assumed Thresh would support maintaining protocol because fear makes people crave structure. Instead, Thresh chose the braver option: if Titans are here, running won't help. Thresh fights afraid. He also thinks afraid — and sometimes the afraid answer is the right one.</AI>
- <AI>Sable's responsibility to Thresh: she's the one who has to send him into danger knowing how much it costs him. Does she protect him by keeping him back? Or does she honor him by trusting him with the hard tasks? The Issue 10 Titan encounter is Thresh's moment — Sable has to let him fight.</AI>

### Sable and Luma

**Canon dynamic:** Luma's findings (glyph in Issue 4, cache in Issue 8) directly threaten Sable's secret. Sable's "too controlled" reaction tells Wren that Sable already knew.

- Luma is doing genuine investigation. She's finding the truth on her own. Every discovery Luma makes is a countdown timer on Sable's secret.
- Luma is also the one most tempted to stay. If Luma's synthesis in Issue 12 is correct — the mission IS being here — then Luma's desire to stay is vindicated. Sable has to reckon with the possibility that the squad member she might have dismissed as self-indulgent was actually the first to see the truth.
- <AI>The quiet irony: Luma is the one who sees the paradox first. Sable is the one carrying the classified version of the same truth. They're both holding pieces of the puzzle and neither knows the other has one.</AI>

### Sable and Theodore

**Canon dynamic:** Wanted to wipe his memory. Relented "with conditions." Told him: "they are not magical, they are not here to grant wishes, and this is not a story." (Plot Outline, Issue 4).

- The memory-wipe impulse is revealing. Sable's first response to a civilian threat isn't avoidance or persuasion — it's erasure. She reached for the most absolute solution.
- She told Theodore "this is not a story" — the irony being that it absolutely is. Sable is wrong about this. The whole volume is the story Theodore eventually writes.
- <AI>How this relationship might evolve: Sable starts seeing Theodore as a security risk. Across the volume, she watches him become essential — carrying equipment, creating diversions, knowing the streets. By Issue 12, he's coordinating with them during the final operation. He became exactly what Wren said he would: useful. Sable was wrong, and the wrongness of her initial judgment is part of her arc.</AI>

## 4. The Arc — What She Loses, What She Finds

### The Arc Statement

"Command without a chain of command." (Story Bible)
"Learning that command doesn't mean control, it means choosing which truths to carry and which to share." (Plot Outline)

### What She Starts With (Issue 1)

- Calm, in command, holding it together
- A system that works: classify information, manage the squad, execute the mission, maintain control
- A chain of command above her — even if she's the ranking officer in the field, there are orders, protocols, an institution behind her
- "Crisis management is her training" — she has a framework for everything

### What She Loses

The crash takes away the chain of command. There's no one above her. No extraction. No updated orders. No institution backing her decisions.

What she loses across the volume:

- **Certainty.** The mission file gives her a mission she can't complete because the details are destroyed. She can't fulfill the role she was trained for.
- **Control.** Jink breaks protocol. Theodore can't be erased. Luma finds things Sable can't suppress. Wren sees through her. One by one, her control systems fail.
- **The mask.** Issue 9 — she breaks. The squad sees her without the command face. "For the first time, her expression isn't commander or burden-carrier. She's lost." (Script Prompts, Issue 9)
- **Isolation.** She was carrying the secret alone. Sharing it is a loss — the loss of the wall between herself and the squad's fear.

<AI>What it feels like from inside: every issue strips away one more piece of the system she relies on. By Issue 9, she's standing on nothing. The command structure is gone. The mission is unknown. The squad doesn't trust her the way they did. And she has to keep leading. Not because the chain of command demands it, but because these four people need her to.</AI>

### What She Finds

Issue 12: "She looks at her squad — battered, soot-covered, holographic light bleeding through the grime. They look like fairy tales. They always did. Something quiet — not a speech. An acknowledgment."

What she finds:

- **Authority without hierarchy.** Command doesn't require a chain of command. It requires people choosing to follow and a leader choosing to be honest about what she doesn't know.
- **Trust replacing control.** She held back information to maintain control. The arc teaches her that trust — messy, risky, vulnerability-requiring trust — works better. The squad functions better in Issues 11-12 AFTER they know everything than they did when Sable was managing information.
- **The mission she couldn't read.** She spent the whole volume carrying a file she couldn't fully decrypt. The answer wasn't in the file. It was in the squad itself — their presence, their stories, their fairy tales. Luma found the mission. Sable just had to accept it.

<AI>The arc in one sentence: Sable starts with authority backed by an institution and ends with authority backed by nothing but her squad's choice to follow her. That's scarier. And stronger.</AI>

## 5. Voice — How Sable Talks

### What Canon Establishes

- "Gives orders, not speeches" (Script Prompts, Issue 1)
- "Clipped authority" (Plot Outline, Issue 2)
- "Reports truth like a briefing" even while breaking (Script Prompts, Issue 9)
- "Precise questions" (Plot Outline, Issue 8)
- "Something quiet — not a speech. An acknowledgment." (Plot Outline, Issue 12)

### The Pattern

Sable's voice is defined by what she DOESN'T say as much as what she does.

- **Short sentences.** Subject, verb, object. Orders are single clauses. No elaboration unless tactically necessary.
- **No hedging.** She doesn't say "I think" or "maybe." She says what is, or she says nothing.
- **Questions as control.** When she asks questions, they're precise and directed — she's managing the conversation, not exploring it. "Asks precise questions" about Luma's findings — she's steering what gets discussed.
- **Silence as communication.** She uses silence deliberately. Not answering is a command decision. The squad has learned to read her silences.

<AI>Vocabulary cues:
- Military register. "Position." "Confirm." "Execute." "Hold." Not jargon-heavy, but the structure of military communication.</AI>

**Contraction tell.** No contractions when in command mode. "Do not" rather than "don't." "We will" rather than "we'll." The formality is the mask. Contractions appear when the mask slips. The shift from "do not" to "don't" is a tell. — LOCKED

### When the Mask Slips

The mask slips in specific moments across the volume:

- **Issue 4** — "Furious at Jink, terrified of the exposure" — fury is a slip. Sable's control usually prevents visible anger. Jink got past the mask.
- **Issue 7** — "Increasingly withdrawn" — not a dramatic slip but a slow erosion. The silence gets heavier.
- **Issue 8** — "Too controlled" — this is the INVERSE of a mask slip. She's overcompensating. The mask is too tight, and the effort is visible.
- **Issue 9** — The break. "Reports truth like a briefing" — even breaking, she structures the information. The mask doesn't come off cleanly; it cracks while still being worn.
- **Issue 9 final page** — "For the first time, her expression isn't commander or burden-carrier. She's lost." — The mask is fully off. No one is watching. This is Sable alone.
- **Issue 12** — "Something quiet." The mask isn't back on. It's just... not needed anymore. She's not performing command. She's just Sable, looking at her people.

<AI>The voice arc:
- Issues 1-3: Clipped, formal, in control. The voice of training.
- Issues 4-7: The same voice but increasingly strained. Sentences get shorter. Silences get longer.
- Issue 8: Overcorrected control. Too precise. The voice of someone holding on.
- Issue 9: The break. The voice fractures. She still structures the information, but the structure is a life raft, not a command.
- Issues 11-12: Quiet authority. Fewer orders, more acknowledgments. She speaks like someone who has stopped performing leadership and started doing it.</AI>
