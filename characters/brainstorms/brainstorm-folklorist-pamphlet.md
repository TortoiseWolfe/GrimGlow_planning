## 2. Methodology

He collects accounts — that's established. But how?

<AI>**Collection methods for 1885 London:**

- **Newspaper clippings** — established in canon. He pins articles to a board (Script Prompts, Issue 10). He reads multiple London papers and clips any account of unusual sightings.
- **Witness interviews** — the Folk-Lore Society methodology of the period emphasized collecting stories "from the lips of the people." He'd visit neighborhoods where sightings occurred and talk to residents.
- **Pub conversations** — stories spread fastest in pubs. A folklorist who wants the real stories goes where the stories are told, not where they're reported.
- **Church records and parish gossip** — the church cache legends ("the good folk once lived here") would draw him. Parishes kept informal records of local superstitions.
- **Correspondence** — letters from people who've seen things. Once word gets out that someone is collecting these accounts, people write in.

**His approach:**
- He collects first, interprets later. Captures exact words when possible. Dates and locations. Multiple accounts of the same sighting.
- He's systematic but not clinical — he appreciates the stories as stories, not just as data.
- He doesn't distinguish between "credible" and "incredible" accounts. All accounts are data. A lamplighter's sighting and a child's story get equal space.

**What makes him different from a journalist:**
A journalist wants the sensational version. The folklorist wants the accurate version — what people actually said, not what makes a good headline. He corrects for sensationalism. He's the anti-newspaper.</AI>

## 4. Significance — "The mythology is setting like concrete"

"The fairy tales are becoming codified, written down, made permanent." (Plot Outline, Issue 11)

This is the folklorist's function in the story — he transforms ephemeral oral stories into durable printed culture. Stories told in pubs and at bedsides become text. Text survives.

<AI>**What publication does that oral storytelling doesn't:**

- **Fixity** — an oral story changes with every telling. A printed story is the same every time.
- **Reach** — an oral story travels as far as the teller's voice. A printed pamphlet travels as far as the postal system.
- **Authority** — an oral story is hearsay. A printed, published account by a university-affiliated academic has institutional weight. It becomes a source.
- **Permanence** — oral stories die with the teller. Printed stories survive the teller, the listener, the generation. They survive centuries.

The folklorist doesn't know he's making the mythology permanent. He thinks he's documenting it. But documentation IS permanence. The act of writing it down changes what it is. A story someone told becomes a story everyone can read. A local rumor becomes a published record. A pub tale becomes folklore.

**The title as meta-structure:**

The pamphlet's title is *"The Stories They Tell."* The volume's title is *"The Stories They Tell."* The reader recognizes the volume's title on the pamphlet's title page — the comic is telling you that this pamphlet IS the story you're reading. The folklorist is, in a sense, the first author of GrimGlow. He's writing the story we're reading, 140 years early.

This creates a layered reading experience:
- On the surface: a character publishes a pamphlet
- One layer down: the pamphlet's title matches the volume's title — a meta wink
- Deepest layer: the folklorist is the institutional arm of the convergence mechanism, and the pamphlet is the actual vehicle through which the mythology survives</AI>

<hidden>**The convergence significance:**

The convergence mechanism has two tracks:
1. **Personal** — Theodore's fairy tale, written by a child who knows the fairies personally
2. **Institutional** — the folklorist's pamphlet, compiled by an academic who treats the stories as objects of study

Both tracks produce printed documents. Both survive. But they function differently:

Theodore's story survives as narrative — a tale of wonder, personal and specific. It becomes the fairy tale.

The folklorist's pamphlet survives as record — a catalogue of sightings, institutional and comprehensive. It becomes the historical evidence. Future scholars cite the pamphlet, not the fairy tale.

The two tracks together are what makes the mythology indestructible. One gives the stories emotional power. The other gives them institutional legitimacy. Either alone could be dismissed. Together, they form a cultural foundation.

The folklorist doesn't know this. He's just doing his job. But his pamphlet is the reason the fairy tales survive as more than children's stories. His institutional voice says: "These things happened. People saw them. Here are their accounts." And that authoritative framing protects Theodore's wonder-story from dismissal.

If alt-Sable understands the convergence mechanism (Temporal Mechanics brainstorm, open question), the folklorist becomes a target. Not Theodore — the folklorist. Because you can dismiss a boy's fairy tale. You can't dismiss an academic publication. The institutional track is the harder one to kill.</hidden>

## 5. Direct Encounters

Does the folklorist ever encounter the squad directly, or does he work entirely from secondhand accounts?

<AI>**Options:**

- Option A: **No direct encounter.** He works entirely from newspaper clippings, witness interviews, and hearsay. He never sees a fairy or a Titan. His pamphlet is built from secondhand material — which makes it more authentic as folklore. Folklore is always secondhand. The folklorist's distance from the source is what makes his work credible as scholarship.

- Option B: **One near-miss.** He's interviewing a witness in the right place at the right time. He sees something — a flash of light, a shadow that moves wrong, a sound he can't explain. He adds his own account to the pamphlet as a footnote, clearly marked as personal observation. But he didn't see enough to understand what he saw.

- Option C: **He interviews Theodore.** The folklorist collects accounts from children as well as adults — children are often better witnesses to fairy phenomena (they see more, filter less). He finds Theodore through the neighborhood. Theodore tells him a story — but which version? The real one, or the fairy tale version? Does Theodore know the difference by this point?

Option A is cleanest — the folklorist as pure collector, never contaminated by direct evidence. His academic distance is what gives the pamphlet its authority.

Option C is the most dramatically charged — but it risks making the folklorist too connected. If he interviews Theodore and Theodore tells the truth, the folklorist is holding direct evidence. That changes what the pamphlet is.

A middle approach: the folklorist interviews people in Theodore's neighborhood. He collects versions of Theodore's story from people Theodore has told. But he never interviews Theodore directly. He's one degree removed. The story he publishes is already a retelling of a retelling — which is exactly how folklore works.</AI>

## 6. Convergence Relationship

The folklorist's pamphlet is the institutional codification track of the convergence mechanism (Temporal Mechanics brainstorm).

<hidden>**The three convergence figures:**

The convergence mechanism in Volume 1 involves three figures who don't know they're connected:

1. **Theodore** — writes the fairy tale (personal track). A witness who loves what he saw.
2. **The folklorist** — publishes the collected accounts (institutional track). A scholar who documents what was seen.
3. **The father** — operates the printing press (mechanical track). A craftsman who makes words physical.

Each is necessary. Theodore provides the story. The folklorist provides the authority. The father provides the machinery. None of them knows the significance. All of them think they're doing something ordinary — a boy writing, a scholar publishing, a printer printing.

**The folklorist-press connection:**

If the folklorist uses the father's press (raised in the father brainstorm, Option C), then all three convergence figures are connected through one machine. The folklorist brings his manuscript to the father's press. The father sets the type and prints the pamphlet. Later — in Volume 2 or beyond — Theodore's fairy tale comes to the same press. The same hands, the same type, the same machine.

The press doesn't just print the mythology. It's where the two tracks physically converge.

**Connection to Luma's arc:**

Luma is the character who sees the convergence mechanism (Temporal Mechanics brainstorm). She notices the folklorist's pamphlet as evidence: "She notices the folklorist's pamphlet. She starts counting how many distinct mythology-generating events the squad has caused." The folklorist is unknowingly providing Luma with her primary evidence. His scholarship is her data.

Does Luma ever read the pamphlet? If she does — a four-inch-tall fairy-soldier reading a pamphlet about fairy sightings — the irony is almost unbearable. She's reading about herself, framed as folklore, published by someone who has no idea what he's actually documenting.

**The dark fairy angle:**

The pamphlet codifies BOTH mythologies — fairy and monster. This means the folklorist is also preserving the monster stories. The Titan mythology survives because of his work too. Is this what alt-Sable wants? Or is this a threat to her?

Under the convergence model: the question is which mythology DOMINATES. If the pamphlet gives equal weight to both, the battle is still undecided. If it gives more weight to one — because the fairy sightings are more frequent, or because the witnesses describe them with more wonder and less fear — then the folklorist has unconsciously tipped the balance.

The folklorist's editorial choices — which accounts to include, how to frame them, which mythology gets more pages — may be one of the most consequential acts in the entire IP. And he has no idea.</hidden>
