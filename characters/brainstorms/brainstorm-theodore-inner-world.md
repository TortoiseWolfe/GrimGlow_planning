## 1. Life Before the Fairies

### What Canon Establishes

- "Apprentice to a clockmaker named Mr. Cribbage, a man who is competent but unkind in a mundane way — dismissive, impatient, uninterested in Theodore's cleverness" (Plot Outline, Issue 3)
- "He has no parents present (they're away, or dead, or simply absent — left deliberately ambiguous for now). He's alone in the way a resourceful child is alone: managing, but not thriving." (Plot Outline, Issue 3)
- "Parents who had aspirations his station hasn't delivered on yet" — the gap between the name and the life (Story Bible)
- Issue 3 structure: "Theodore's world is small: the workshop, errands, a narrow orbit of streets" (Script Prompts)
- "Show his cleverness: he fixes something Cribbage can't, and Cribbage takes credit. Show his isolation: he eats alone, works alone, walks home alone." (Script Prompts)
- "Also show his curiosity — he watches everything, notices details others miss, tinkers with his own small projects in stolen moments" (Script Prompts)

### The Family

**Father** — alive, runs a printing press. Works long hours. Not absent by choice — absent by circumstance. Mother's death left him without anyone to care for Theodore during working hours.

**Mother** — passed away. Common for the era (childbirth, disease). Her death explains why Theodore was apprenticed out — the father couldn't manage child care alone while running a press.

Theodore was placed with Cribbage for practical reasons, not abandonment. But the effect on a child is similar. He has a father who is present in the city but absent from his daily life.

Open questions:
- How often does Theodore see his father? Sundays? Less?
- Does the father know Theodore is clever? Does he care?
- Does Theodore resent him, miss him, or simply accept it?
- <AI>The father might be a good man who made a practical decision and doesn't realize how lonely its result is. Not a villain — just a man who thinks providing a trade is the same as providing a parent.</AI>

### The Grandmother

Issue 9: Theodore tells the squad "a story his grandmother told him — about a fairy queen who sent her knights on a quest, but the quest was a riddle, and the answer was that they were already where they needed to be" (Plot Outline).

- The grandmother told fairy tales. She's the source of Theodore's fairy mythology — the frame he puts around Jink when they meet.
- <AI>If the mother died and the father is absent, the grandmother may have been Theodore's primary relationship. Options:
  - Maternal grandmother, now also gone. The fairy tales are what Theodore has left of her. The stories are inheritance.
  - Maternal grandmother, still alive but distant (in another part of the city? too old to care for him?). Theodore visits when he can. The fairy tales are a living connection.
  - The grandmother's death might have been the loss that made Theodore truly alone — mother first, then grandmother, then apprenticed out. Three departures.</AI>

### The Two Workshops

Theodore moves between two worlds of making:

**Cribbage's clockshop** — where he is now. Clocks, mechanisms, precision. Cribbage is "competent but unkind" — skilled enough to teach but uninterested in Theodore's cleverness. Theodore is learning a trade, but the learning is cold. No encouragement. No recognition.

**Father's printing press** — where he came from. Words, ink, paper, type. The machinery of stories. Theodore may have spent early childhood around the press before his mother died and he was apprenticed out.

<AI>The two workshops give Theodore two vocabularies — mechanical (clockwork: gears, springs, escapements, mainsprings) and literary (printing: type, galleys, impressions, broadsheets). Both are about making. One makes time. The other makes stories. In Issue 12, Theodore uses both: his clockwork hands disarm the device, and then he writes the story. The two workshops converge in him.</AI>

### What "Managing but Not Thriving" Looks Like

- He's clever and no one notices. He fixes things Cribbage can't and Cribbage takes credit.
- He eats alone, works alone, walks home alone.
- His orbit is narrow — the workshop, errands, streets.
- His curiosity is his own. "Tinkers with his own small projects in stolen moments." The projects are Theodore's only creative space.
- The burn scar, the oil stains, the gear ring — his body shows the work. The work is all there is.

<AI>Theodore is a boy who is good at being alone but not good at being lonely. There's a difference. He can manage his days, feed himself, do the work. What he can't manage is the silence — the absence of anyone who sees what he sees, wonders what he wonders. The fairies don't fill a practical gap in his life. They fill the wondering gap.</AI>

## 2. What He Thinks Fairies ARE

### The Moment of Contact

Canon: Theodore sees what centuries of humans will see — a tiny glowing figure with wings. Jink is "caught in the open on Theodore's workbench when he enters unexpectedly" (Plot Outline, Issue 3). "Jink doesn't correct him [that they're a fairy], partly because it's simpler and partly because Jink finds the whole thing delightful" (Plot Outline).

From the Character Prompts: Theodore is "peering into a gap between the floorboards where a faint, impossible light glows up at his face... his expression is the exact moment between fear and wonder, mouth slightly open, not yet decided what he's seeing."

### The Story He Already Knows

The grandmother's fairy tales are Theodore's template. When he sees Jink, he doesn't see a stranger — he sees a confirmation. The stories were true.

Open questions:
- What did the grandmother's fairy tales say? (Helpful? Dangerous? Trickster figures? Noble visitors?)
- Does Theodore's Victorian culture have a standard fairy mythology he's drawing from? (Likely yes — fairy tales are deeply embedded in 19th century British culture)
- <AI>Theodore might have multiple layers of fairy knowledge: his grandmother's personal stories (intimate, specific), the broader cultural folklore (Midsummer Night's Dream, Tam Lin, Thomas the Rhymer), and penny dreadful fairy stories he's read or heard. The grandmother's stories would carry the most emotional weight.</AI>

### What He NEEDS Fairies to Be

The user asks: what gap in his life does this fill?

- A mother who died. A father who's absent. A grandmother who told stories. A master who doesn't see him. And then — a fairy in his workshop who looks at him with interest.
- <AI>What Theodore needs isn't magic. It's being seen. Cribbage dismisses him. His father is absent. The adult world treats him as furniture — useful, unremarkable. Jink GRINS at him. Jink talks to him. Jink comes back. Theodore needs the fairies to be magical because magical means: this is happening for a reason. You were chosen. You're special. The alternative — that it's random, that they crashed here by accident, that he could have been anyone — is much harder for a lonely boy to accept.</AI>

### The Fairy Version vs. the Real Version

Theodore builds a story around Jink. The story probably includes:
- Fairies are here because they chose to be (not because they crashed)
- They have powers (not damaged equipment running on salvaged parts)
- They need something from HIM (not just any human-scaled pair of hands)
- This is a story (not a military disaster)

<AI>The fairy version of events is warm, purposeful, magical. The real version is cold, accidental, desperate. The gap between the two is where Theodore's arc lives. What makes the arc work is that the final answer — "something better" — is warmer than the fairy tale, not colder. The real version is eventually MORE meaningful than the magical one.</AI>

## 3. The Disillusionment

### What Canon Establishes

**Issue 4:** "He understands that they are not magical, they are not here to grant wishes, and this is not a story. (The irony, of course, is that it absolutely is.)" (Plot Outline)

**The buildup:**
- Issue 3: First contact with Jink. Wonder, delight, the fairy assumption.
- Issue 4: Meeting the full squad. Sable's fury. The negotiation. Sable's conditions: "They are not magical, they are not here to grant wishes, and this is not a story."

### How It Breaks

The Issue 4 meeting is where the fairy tale shatters. Theodore enters the wall cavity and finds:
- Not one fairy but five
- They're arguing (Sable furious at Jink)
- They have weapons
- They talk like soldiers, not magical beings
- Sable looks at him with tactical calculation, not wonder
- Someone suggests wiping his memory

<AI>What specifically breaks the spell? Options:
- Sable's anger. Fairies aren't supposed to be furious at each other. The argument breaks the enchantment.
- The memory wipe threat. Fairies grant wishes. They don't erase you. The moment Theodore realizes they considered making him forget is the moment they stop being magical.
- The weapons. Thresh's rifle. Sable's sidearm. Fairies don't carry weapons.
- The word "mission." Military language has no place in fairy tales.
- All of the above, in rapid succession. The spell breaks not from one blow but from accumulation — everything Theodore sees in the wall cavity contradicts the fairy tale, and the contradictions arrive too fast to absorb.</AI>

### Does It Happen All at Once?

**In stages** — LOCKED. Issue 4 is a crack, not a break. The memory wipe debate shakes him, but he recovers — partially. The full weight builds across Issues 4-9 as he sees more of the squad's military reality. The fairy tale doesn't die all at once.

Cracks before Issue 4:
- Issue 3: Jink's soot-streaked suit. The tear patched with copper wire. The damage is visible — this isn't a pristine fairy. Theodore notices but explains it away: fairies are wild, fairies get dirty, fairies fight monsters.
- Issue 3: Jink's language. "Modern-feeling, quick, casual." Not how fairy tales sound. Theodore finds this charming rather than wrong.
- The crack becomes wider in Issue 4. One fairy can be explained. Five soldiers arguing about protocol cannot. But it takes until Issue 9 (Sable's secret) for the fairy tale framework to fully break.

### What Theodore Does with the Loss

The gap between Issue 4 (disillusionment) and Issue 5 (earning trust through practical usefulness):

- Issue 4 ends with "Theodore walking home, the shard glowing faintly in his pocket. He's different now. He has a secret."
- Issue 5: "Theodore begins earning the squad's trust through practical usefulness — his knowledge of the city, his ability to move through human spaces, his tinker's skills" (Plot Outline)

<AI>What fills the gap? Options:
- **Grief, then redirection.** Theodore loses the fairy tale and replaces it with something he knows how to do — be useful. The charging cradle he built (Issue 4) is his audition. He can't be magical, but he can be competent. The transition from wonder to usefulness is Theodore choosing to matter on different terms.
- **Denial, then acceptance.** Theodore spends the gap between Issues 4-5 trying to preserve the fairy tale in his mind. Maybe they're magical AND soldiers. Maybe the arguing was part of it. But the evidence keeps piling up, and by Issue 5 he's stopped pretending.
- **Anger, then pragmatism.** Theodore might feel cheated — they're not what he wanted, and they don't need him the way he imagined. The anger is brief but real. The pragmatism is his way of staying connected without the fairy tale as an excuse.</AI>

## 4. "Something Better" — What Replaces the Fairy Tale

### The Arc Progression

| Phase | What Theodore Sees | What He Believes |
|-------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Issue 3 | A tiny glowing figure with wings | A fairy. Magic is real. |
| Issue 4 | Five soldiers arguing, weapons, military language | They're not magical. This is not a story. |
| Issues 5-7 | A squad that lets him help, trusts his hands, needs his streets | They're people. Tired, specific, real. |
| Issue 9 | A squad that's fractured, a captain who lied | They're flawed. They make mistakes. They're afraid. |
| Issue 11 | Jink serious for the first time, telling him to walk away | They care about him. Enough to try to protect him from themselves. |
| Issue 12 | A squad that chooses to stay | They're not trapped here. They CHOOSE to be here. |

### "Real People Who Chose to Stay"

Canon: "something better — real people who chose to stay" (Plot Outline).

The fairy tale version: magical beings who appeared for a purpose, whose presence is fated, whose power solves problems.

The "something better" version: ordinary people (extraordinary by circumstances, not nature) who had the option to leave and stayed. Not because they were compelled. Because they decided this mattered.

<AI>Why "choice" is more meaningful than "magic":
- Magic is given. Choice is earned.
- Magical fairies are here because they HAVE to be. Choosing fairies are here because they WANT to be.
- A fairy who grants wishes does it because that's what fairies do. A soldier who stays in a dangerous time period does it because they looked at the cost and said yes anyway.
- For a boy who's been left by everyone — mother dead, father absent, master indifferent — someone CHOOSING to be present is the most extraordinary thing imaginable. Not magic. Loyalty.</AI>

### The Issue 9 Fairy Tale — Theodore Seeing What They Can't

"Theodore tells the squad a story his grandmother told him — about a fairy queen who sent her knights on a quest, but the quest was a riddle, and the answer was that they were already where they needed to be." (Plot Outline)

"Not naive — insightful in a way the squad can't be because they're too close to their own situation." (Script Prompts)

- Theodore has already intuited the answer before the squad does. The grandmother's fairy tale describes exactly what Luma synthesizes in Issue 12 — the mission IS being here.
- He tells the story without knowing why it's relevant. The squad does.
- <AI>This is Theodore at his most important: not as a pair of useful hands, but as a storyteller. The boy who carries fairy tales in his head is the one who sees the shape of what's happening before the soldiers do. His grandmother's stories aren't just comfort — they're a lens. Theodore understands the paradox through narrative, not analysis. Luma maps the evidence. Theodore tells the story that makes it make sense.</AI>

### The Issue 12 Writing Scene

"Theodore, in his workshop, by candlelight. He's writing. Not a report, not a letter — a *story.* About tiny luminous people with wings of light, who fell from the sky and fought monsters in the fog. He doesn't know he's writing mythology. He thinks he's writing about his friends." (Plot Outline)

"Last panel: His pen on paper. The first words of the oldest fairy tale." (Plot Outline)

- The fairy tale he lost in Issue 4 returns transformed. Not the fairy tale his grandmother told — a new one. His own. About people he knows.
- He's writing about friends, not fairies. The fairy tale language returns naturally because that's how Theodore processes wonder — but the content is real.

<hidden>Theodore's fairy tale IS the convergence mythology — the story that survives centuries and shapes which civilizational branch wins. "If Theodore's story survives centuries and eventually reaches the fairy civilization as a historical document, then Theodore is — unknowingly — the most important human in the timeline." (temporal mechanics brainstorm)

The father's printing press. Theodore knows printing. He grew up around type, ink, galleys. If the fairy tale he writes by candlelight eventually reaches his father's press — or any press — the convergence mechanism has a physical propagation engine. A handwritten story can be lost. A printed story can survive centuries. The printing press is how Theodore's fairy tale becomes the founding myth of a civilization.

The boy who lost his fairy tale writes the most important one in history. And the machinery to spread it is already in his life.</hidden>

## 5. Voice — Victorian Earnest, and How It Shifts

### Canon Foundations

- "Period-appropriate Victorian English — not incomprehensible Dickensian dialect, but with the cadence and vocabulary of an articulate working-class boy of the era" (Script Prompts)
- "Theodore should sound like a real boy of his time — not a museum exhibit and not a modern kid in costume" (Script Prompts)
- "Theodore's dialogue contrasts sharply [with Jink's] — modern-feeling, quick, casual [for Jink]. The gap between how they speak is part of the culture clash." (Script Prompts)
- Issue 9: "Theodore's story is told in his Victorian voice — earnest, formal, a boy telling a story he loves to people he cares about" (Script Prompts)

### Working-Class Articulate

Theodore is not a gentleman's son. He's a tinker's apprentice. But he's clever and he notices things.

<AI>His vocabulary draws from two trades:
- **Clockwork language** (from Cribbage): gears, escapements, mainsprings, balance wheels, pinions. He describes precision in mechanical terms. Things "mesh" or they don't. Something "runs true" or it's "off its pivot."
- **Printing language** (from his father's press): type, galleys, impressions, broadsheets, leading, slugs. He'd know how words become physical objects — how a story gets set in type, pressed into paper, made permanent.
- **Street language** (from his narrow orbit): he knows his neighborhood, the shortcuts, the trades. He can identify a sound by which workshop it comes from. He reads the city mechanically.

The combined effect: Theodore talks about the world in terms of how things work. Not abstractly — specifically. He sees function, mechanism, craft. This is what makes him understand Wren before anyone else does.</AI>

### Register Shifts — Who He's Talking To

**With Jink:**
- Relaxed by Theodore's standards, which still sounds formal by Jink's. The warmth is there but the cadence is Victorian.
- Wonder leaks through. Jink brings out the boy who saw a fairy in his workshop — the voice that asks questions, that marvels.
- The culture clash is ongoing: Jink says something modern and Theodore doesn't understand. Theodore says something formal and Jink teases him.
- <AI>Over time, they develop a shared vocabulary — neither fully Victorian nor fully future. Little borrowings in both directions. Theodore might pick up one of Jink's future expressions. Jink might start saying something Theodore taught them. The shared language is the friendship made audible.</AI>

**With Sable:**
- Formal. Careful. Theodore instinctively addresses Sable as authority — she carries herself like someone who commands, and a working-class boy in Victorian London knows how to recognize rank.
- He's slightly afraid of her. Not because she's four inches tall — because she's precise and unsmiling and makes him feel like he's being evaluated.
- "Yes, ma'am" energy — not because she asked for it, but because Theodore's world teaches deference to authority.
- <AI>Sable told Theodore "this is not a story." His voice around her reflects that: careful, factual, useful. He reports to Sable. He talks to Jink.</AI>

**With Wren:**
- Technical. They share the language of how things work. "They speak the same language of how things work, just centuries apart" (Script Prompts).
- This is where Theodore's clockwork vocabulary is most at home. He can describe a mechanism to Wren and she understands, even though the technology is separated by millennia.
- <AI>Theodore might be more confident with Wren than with anyone else in the squad, because with Wren, his cleverness is visible. Cribbage doesn't notice what Theodore can do. Wren notices immediately.</AI>

**With Luma:**
- Curious. Collaborative. The Issue 6 investigation partnership — "Luma rides in Theodore's waistcoat pocket."
- Theodore asks Luma questions the way he asks the world questions — genuinely wanting to understand.
- Issue 10: Luma tells him something about the paradox. Theodore's voice here would be quiet, absorbing, trying to fit something enormous into his understanding.
- <AI>Luma might be the squad member Theodore is most comfortable being uncertain around. With Sable he performs competence. With Jink he performs bravery. With Luma he can admit he doesn't understand.</AI>

**With Thresh:**
- Wary, then careful. Theodore's first encounter with Thresh would be intimidating — Thresh is the most armored, the most visibly a soldier, the one holding a weapon in a white-knuckle grip.
- <AI>Theodore might be the one who eventually sees through Thresh's fear — he's a boy who watches everything, who "notices details others miss." He might recognize Thresh's trembling hands before anyone in the squad acknowledges it. What Theodore does with that recognition could matter.</AI>

### The Storytelling Voice

Issue 9: Theodore tells the grandmother's fairy tale.

- "Earnest, formal, a boy telling a story he loves to people he cares about" (Script Prompts).
- This is Theodore as narrator. The voice changes — it's not clockwork language or street language. It's the voice his grandmother used, passed through his own mouth. Slower, more rhythmic, with the cadence of oral storytelling.

Issue 12: Theodore writes the fairy tale.

- We don't hear this voice aloud — we see pen on paper. But the writing voice is the bridge: the grandmother's storytelling cadence applied to Theodore's own experience. He's writing about people he knows in the voice of the stories he was told.
- <AI>The Issue 12 writing is where all of Theodore's voices converge: the mechanical precision from Cribbage's shop (he describes their technology accurately), the wonder from the grandmother's fairy tales (he uses the language of magic), and his own voice (he calls them friends). The fairy tale he writes isn't his grandmother's story retold. It's a new kind of story — fairy tale language applied to people who are real.</AI>

## 6. Mr. Cribbage — Brain-Plugged

### What's Established

The victim becomes a controlled near-zombie — dark veins spreading from the implant site, iridescent sheen in the eyes, oily skin, subtly wrong movement. ### What This Means for Theodore

- The power dynamic inverts completely — the master who didn't need Theodore now genuinely needs his help
- Victorian observers would say Cribbage was "taken by the fairies" — which feeds the fairy tale echo: people stolen by dark forces and brought back... changed

<AI>Possible beats:

- Theodore is the one who has to help remove the device (guided by Wren via comm link)

- Cribbage might not remember the period of control. He doesn't know what was done to him or who saved him.
- The "simpler" Cribbage might lose his clockmaking precision — a craftsman who can no longer craft. What does that mean for the workshop? For Theodore's apprenticeship?</AI>
