## State 1: Normal — "Competent but Unkind in a Mundane Way"

### What the User Stated

- Not villainous — mundanely crushing
- Dismissive, impatient, uninterested in Theodore's cleverness
- Takes credit for Theodore's fixes
- Skilled but cold
- Needs development: the workshop itself, his daily rhythm, his reputation in the neighborhood

### Unkindness Patterns

The credit-taking is established. What else makes Cribbage specifically, mundanely unkind:

- He takes credit for Theodore's fixes — this is canon
- <AI>Possible additional patterns:
  - Talks past Theodore to customers as though he isn't there. A customer asks who fixed their clock and Cribbage says "I" without hesitation.
  - Eats without offering. Not cruelty — it genuinely doesn't occur to him that Theodore is hungry.
  - Corrects Theodore's work in front of clients, but when alone, uses Theodore's methods on the next job.
  - Calls Theodore "boy" more often than his name. Not malicious — just hasn't bothered to make the name habitual.
  - Assigns Theodore the cleaning and fetching but never lets him work the front bench where the interesting mechanisms are. Theodore works the front bench after hours, alone.</AI>

### What Makes Him Not a Villain

- Genuine skill — he can teach Theodore a real trade
- Provides shelter, a place, a function
- He just... doesn't see Theodore. The unkindness is absence, not malice.
- <AI>Cribbage might have been a decent man once — or still is, to people he considers peers. He treats the neighborhood watchmaker with professional respect. He nods to the landlady. He simply doesn't extend that recognition downward to a boy. Theodore's invisibility is about hierarchy, not hatred.</AI>

### The Workshop

- Modest operation (canon: "not a prosperous establishment") (Plot Outline, Issue 5)
- Theodore's "world is small: the workshop, errands, a narrow orbit of streets" (Script Prompts, Issue 3)
- The workshop at night: after Cribbage goes home, Theodore examines the shard by candlelight (Script Prompts, Issue 3)
- The workshop contains: "workbenches cluttered with clockwork parts, hanging tools, a half-assembled automaton on a shelf, gaslight warm and smoky" (Character Prompts — Theodore action pose)

<AI>Physical details to consider:
- The smell: brass polish, machine oil, the faint sulfur of a struck match. Gaslight adds warmth but also a permanent haze.
- The sound: ticking. Dozens of clocks in various states of repair, none perfectly synchronized. A constant, layered, slightly off rhythm.
- The layout: front room where customers come (Cribbage's domain), back room where the real work happens (where Theodore is usually banished). The workbench in the back has Theodore's hidden projects in a drawer Cribbage doesn't check.
- Near telegraph infrastructure — wires run along the roofline outside. The clicking of the telegraph relay is audible from the shop on quiet nights. This is why the Titans target Cribbage: proximity.</AI>

### Daily Rhythm

<AI>Possible daily structure:
- Cribbage opens the shop. Theodore has already been there an hour — swept, organized, laid out tools.
- Mornings: customer work. Cribbage at the front bench, Theodore fetching parts, cleaning mechanisms, doing the invisible labor.
- Midday: Cribbage eats at his bench. Theodore eats whatever he's managed — bread, an apple, nothing if the cupboard is bare.
- Afternoons: repairs. This is where Theodore's skill shows — Cribbage hands him the jobs he can't be bothered with, then takes credit for the results.
- Evening: Cribbage leaves. Theodore stays. The workshop belongs to Theodore after dark — this is when he tinkers, when he examines the shard, when he meets Jink.</AI>

### Neighborhood Reputation

<AI>Options:
- Respectable but unremarkable. "A competent clockmaker" — not the best, not the worst. People bring him the routine work.
- The neighborhood knows he has an apprentice. They assume it's a normal arrangement. No one asks Theodore how he's treated because no one thinks to ask.
- Cribbage might be known for being particular — not warm, but reliable. "He'll fix your clock but he won't smile about it."
- After brain-plugging, the neighbors would say: "Cribbage has been odd lately. Not himself. Maybe he's taken ill." The wrongness would be visible to anyone paying attention — but people don't pay close attention to a man who was unremarkable to begin with.</AI>

## State 2: Brain-Plugged — "The Horror Inversion"

### What the User Stated

- Dark fairy commanders implant him because his shop sits near telegraph infrastructure
- From the outside: dark veins, iridescent eyes, oily skin sheen, movement lacking natural micro-adjustments
- How does Theodore discover what happened?
- The power dynamic inverts completely — the master who dismissed Theodore now needs his help

### Discovery Sequence

"Theodore notices Cribbage acting strangely before learning the cause — the wrongness builds slowly" (Theodore Inner World brainstorm, Section 6)

<AI>How the wrongness builds — what a 12-year-old boy notices, in order:
1. **The ticking stops.** Cribbage stops winding the shop clocks. For a clockmaker, this is wrong the way a baker not baking is wrong. Theodore winds them himself and doesn't think about why.
2. **The hands.** Cribbage's hands — his primary tools — move differently. Smooth where they should be precise. He reaches for a tool and his fingers close around it a half-beat too late, or his grip is too even, too steady. A clockmaker's hands have tremor and correction built in. Brain-plugged hands don't.
3. **The eyes.** In gaslight, Cribbage's eyes catch the light wrong — an oily shimmer that wasn't there before. Theodore might first notice it when handing Cribbage a lamp. The reflection is iridescent, not warm.
4. **The neck.** Dark veins visible at the collar line. Theodore sees them when Cribbage bends over the bench. He might think it's bruising, illness, something medical. He doesn't know what it is.
5. **The silence.** Cribbage was dismissive, impatient — he spoke to Theodore in clipped commands and grunts. Brain-plugged Cribbage doesn't speak at all, or speaks in flat, operational phrases that sound rehearsed. The unkindness was at least human. The silence isn't.</AI>

### The Workshop Under Brain-Plugged Cribbage

<AI>Options:
- The shop stays open but wrong. Cribbage goes through motions — sits at the bench, handles tools — but doesn't actually repair anything. Customers come and leave confused. "He just stared at me."
- The shop becomes a waypoint. Brain-plugged Cribbage, under standing orders, uses the workshop as a staging point near the telegraph infrastructure. The clocks stop. The tools gather dust. The shop smells different — that oily sheen has a scent, metallic and wrong.
- Theodore still comes to work. The horror is that his daily routine continues in a distorted version. Same workshop, same bench, same master — but the master is a puppet and the workshop is a shell.</AI>

### What Theodore Does

The power dynamic inverts. The master who dismissed Theodore is now a puppet.

<AI>Possible progression:
- Theodore first tries to care for Cribbage the way you'd care for a sick man — brings water, speaks gently. No response. This is worse than dismissal.
- Theodore tells the squad. Wren or Luma recognizes the symptoms from what they've already seen.
- Theodore is the one who has to help remove the device — guided by Wren via comm link. The removal is mechanical work. Clockwork-scale precision. Exactly what Theodore is good at. Exactly what Cribbage taught him.
- The irony: the skills Cribbage gave Theodore are what save Cribbage. Theodore removes the device with the steady hands Cribbage trained.</AI>

### Issue Placement

<AI>Most likely:
- Issue 10 is the first encounter with brain-plugged humans during the telegraph office Titan encounter (Plot Outline). Cribbage may be among them — or discovered separately.
- If Cribbage's brain-plugging is discovered separately from the main Titan encounter, it could happen in Issue 10 (discovery) and Issue 12 (removal during the final operation).
- If Cribbage IS one of the brain-plugged humans in the telegraph encounter, Theodore's personal connection raises the stakes of the Issue 10-12 arc.

## State 3: Recovered — "Comes Back Wrong"

### What the User Stated

- Confused, diminished, missing time, personality flattened
- The origin of changeling stories
- What specifically is lost? Fine motor skills (a clockmaker who can't hold a tool)? Memory gaps? Emotional flatness?
- Does he remember Theodore differently?

### What's Lost

Recovery is "mostly reversible" — but Cribbage returns as a "simpler version" of himself (canon, LOCKED).

The specific losses for a clockmaker:

- **Fine motor skills** — a clockmaker who can't hold a tool steady. His hands, which were his identity, betray him. He reaches for a screwdriver and can't close his fingers precisely enough. He drops mainsprings. He over-torques escapements. The thing that made him Cribbage is the thing the brain-plugging took.
- **Memory gaps** — he doesn't remember the period of control. Days or weeks are simply gone. He doesn't know what was done to him.
- **Emotional flatness** — the unkindness is gone, but so is everything else. The dismissiveness required energy, engagement — even negative engagement. Recovered Cribbage is quiet. Not kind. Not unkind. Just... flat.
- **Remembers Theodore but diminished** — LOCKED. He knows Theodore. He knows the workshop. But something is missing — the sharpness, the precision, the unkindness. He's simpler. He calls Theodore "boy" but can't remember why he used to be angry. The shift from "boy, fetch the file" to "Theodore... could you..." is small and enormous.

### The Changed Dynamic

The master-apprentice relationship is permanently altered.

- Before: Cribbage had authority, skill, dismissiveness. Theodore had resentment, hidden competence, isolation.
- After: Cribbage has diminishment, confusion, need. Theodore has capability, knowledge, and a choice about how to respond.
- <AI>Theodore's choice is the arc payoff. He could leave — find another master, walk away from a man who never valued him. Instead he stays. Not out of obligation. Because he's learned what "choosing to stay" means from the squad. The same principle that makes the squad's decision to stay in 1885 meaningful makes Theodore's decision to stay with Cribbage meaningful. He chooses presence over absence. That's "something better" applied to his own life.</AI>

### The Changeling Echo

"He was taken by the dark ones and when he came back he wasn't himself anymore" — this is one of the oldest fairy tale patterns (Ogre Origin brainstorm, Section 9).

- Victorian neighbors would see Cribbage and say he was "bewitched," "taken," returned but changed.
- The changeling myth: the real person was stolen and something lesser was left in their place.
- Cribbage IS the changeling. He looks right but isn't. He's been hollowed out by contact with the otherworld.
- <AI>The neighborhood response could be a small scene: the landlady brings soup. The watchmaker down the street shakes his head. "Poor old Cribbage. He was never the warmest man, but at least he was sharp. Now look at him." The community response reads as Victorian charity toward someone who's "gone simple" — the exact social pattern that changeling stories grew from.</AI>

### What Theodore Writes

In Issue 12, Theodore writes the fairy tale by candlelight. The Cribbage story is now part of that mythology.

- He's writing about "tiny luminous people with wings of light, who fell from the sky and fought monsters in the fog" — and also about a man who was taken and came back changed.
- The fairy tale he writes includes the changeling in disguised form — the dark side of the fairy mythology.
- <AI>Theodore's fairy tale might not name Cribbage directly. But the story of "the craftsman who was stolen by the dark folk and came back with hollow hands" — that's Cribbage. That's the changeling tale that survives centuries. Theodore is writing the bright fairy story AND the dark fairy story, because he lived both.</AI>

Humans brain-plugged across multiple convergence points, across generations, accumulate the physiological changes that eventually produce full ogre biology. Cribbage's recovery is the benign version of what becomes, over centuries of repetition, an irreversible species transformation.
