# Theodore Edmund Hartley

The squad's human ally. A Victorian tinker's apprentice, twelve or thirteen years old, clever with his hands, overlooked by the adult world. Theodore discovers the squad and becomes their translator, guide, and cover story -- the giant pair of hands that can move through the human world on their behalf. His bond with Jink is the core emotional relationship of the volume. His arc is "wants fairies to be magical; learns they're something better" -- and "something better" is real people who chose to stay. In the final scene of Volume 1, alone by candlelight, he writes a story about his friends. He does not know he is writing the first fairy tale. (Source: Story Bible; Plot Outline, Arc Threads)

The weight of his full name -- Theodore Edmund Hartley -- "speaks to parents who had aspirations his station hasn't delivered on yet." The gap between the grandness of the name and the smallness of his life does character work before a single line of dialogue. (Source: Story Bible)

## Physical Description

Slight build, average height for his age but narrow in the shoulders -- he has not grown into himself yet. Narrow face, slightly pointed chin, untidy sandy-brown hair that falls over his forehead and ears, pushed behind the right ear but not the left. Wide hazel eyes, a scattering of freckles across the nose and cheeks. His expression is neutral but alert -- "a boy who watches more than he speaks." (Source: Character Turnarounds)

### Hands

His hands are his most important feature. Stained with machine oil that has worked into the creases of his knuckles and under his fingernails. A small circular burn scar on the knuckle of his right index finger. A brass gear ring worn on his left index finger -- a fidget object he made himself, with a tiny working gear that spins. These are the hands that build the charging cradle in Issue 4, create diversions in Issue 5, and disarm the final Titan device in Issue 12. (Source: Character Turnarounds; Character Prompts)

### Clothing

Working-class apprentice clothing: a collarless linen shirt, off-white, with sleeves rolled to the elbows revealing thin forearms. A worn brown leather waistcoat that is slightly too large for him -- it was made for someone broader, the shoulder seams sit too wide, the hem hangs past his hips. The waistcoat has two small front pockets and a visible repair stitch along the left side seam. Patched wool trousers in dark gray-brown, with a visible square patch on the right knee in slightly mismatched fabric. A simple leather belt. Scuffed brown leather boots, laced, with worn soles. His pockets are full of small tools and salvaged parts. "A tinker's apprentice, not a gentleman's son." (Source: Character Turnarounds; Character Prompts)

Theodore has no clean/degraded split. He only has one state. His clothing does not change. (Source: Character Turnarounds, Notes for 3D Generation)

### Action Pose

Kneeling on the wooden floor of a cramped tinker's workshop, peering into a gap between the floorboards where a faint, impossible light glows up at his face. His expression is "the exact moment between fear and wonder, mouth slightly open, not yet decided what he's seeing." The workshop surrounds him at human scale: workbenches cluttered with clockwork parts, hanging tools, a half-assembled automaton on a shelf, gaslight warm and smoky. The only cool light in the frame is the holographic glow from between the floorboards -- structured, geometric, alien -- bleeding up around his fingers and across his face. "He is ordinary in an extraordinary moment." (Source: Character Prompts)

## Personality

### Core Traits

- **Clever.** Observant, notices details others miss, fixes things, tinkers with his own small projects in stolen moments. He fixes something Cribbage cannot, and Cribbage takes credit.
- **Resourceful.** Manages his days, feeds himself, does the work. "Alone in the way a resourceful child is alone: managing, but not thriving."
- **Curious.** Watches everything. Notices details others miss. His curiosity is his own creative space.
- **Earnest.** Sincere, genuine, takes things seriously. "Not a museum exhibit and not a modern kid in costume."
- **Isolated.** His world is small: the workshop, errands, a narrow orbit of streets. No one sees his cleverness. No one notices him.

(Source: Story Bible; Script Prompts, Issue 3; Plot Outline, Issue 3)

### What He Needs

Theodore needs to be seen. Cribbage dismisses him. His father is absent. The adult world treats him as furniture -- useful, unremarkable. When Jink grins at him, talks to him, comes back -- that fills the wondering gap. He needs the fairies to be magical because magical means: this is happening for a reason. You were chosen. You are special. The alternative -- that it is random, that they crashed here by accident, that he could have been anyone -- is harder for a lonely boy to accept. (Source: Theodore Inner World brainstorm; Story Bible)

## Backstory

### Mr. Cribbage

Apprentice to a clockmaker named Mr. Cribbage, "a man who is competent but unkind in a mundane way -- dismissive, impatient, uninterested in Theodore's cleverness." Skilled enough to teach but uninterested in what Theodore can do. The learning is cold. No encouragement. No recognition. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 3)

Cribbage's workshop sits near telegraph infrastructure the Titans are corrupting. Cribbage is brain-plugged by the dark fairy commanders -- the man who dismissed Theodore, took credit for his work, and treated him as invisible becomes a shambling, iridescent-eyed puppet. Theodore has to reconcile his resentment with the horror of seeing Cribbage reduced to this. The power dynamic inverts completely: the master who did not need Theodore now genuinely needs his help. (Source: Ogre Origin brainstorm, Section 8)

### Family

Father is alive, runs a printing press. Works long hours. Present in the city but absent from daily life. Not absent by choice -- absent by circumstance. Mother passed away. Theodore was placed with Cribbage for practical reasons, not abandonment. But the effect on a child is similar. (Source: Theodore Inner World brainstorm)

### The Grandmother

Theodore's grandmother told him fairy tales -- the source of his fairy mythology, the frame he puts around Jink when they meet. In Issue 9 he tells the squad "a story his grandmother told him -- about a fairy queen who sent her knights on a quest, but the quest was a riddle, and the answer was that they were already where they needed to be." The fairy tales are inheritance. They are what Theodore has left of her. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 9; Theodore Inner World brainstorm)

### The Two Workshops

Theodore moves between two worlds of making. Cribbage's clockshop: clocks, mechanisms, precision -- one makes time. His father's printing press: words, ink, paper, type -- the other makes stories. Both are vocabularies of making. The two workshops converge in Theodore at Issue 12, when his clockwork hands disarm the device and then he writes the story. (Source: Theodore Inner World brainstorm)

## The Bond with Jink

The core emotional relationship of the volume. "Jink starts treating the past as an adventure and learns it has weight; Theodore starts wanting the fairies to be magical and learns they're something better -- real people who chose to stay." Their arcs are mirrors: Jink moves from lightness to weight. Theodore moves from magic to reality. They meet in the middle -- at something honest. (Source: Plot Outline, Arc Threads; Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm)

### Bond Progression

| Phase | Jink's Position | Theodore's Position | What They Are |
|-------|----------------|--------------------| --------------|
| Issue 3 | Delighted, reckless, breaking rules | Awestruck, believing, the fairy tale is real | A fairy and a boy |
| Issue 4 | Exposed, facing consequences | Disillusioned, choosing to stay anyway | A soldier and an asset |
| Issues 5-7 | Enjoying the partnership, not yet serious | Earning his place, building competence | Allies becoming friends |
| Issue 9 | Furious, fractured | Comforting, insightful | Friends where the roles flip |
| Issue 11 | Serious for the first time, trying to protect | Refusing protection, choosing to stay | Equals |
| Issue 12 | Part of the squad's final stand | Doing the work with his own hands, then writing the story | The story and its author |

(Source: Plot Outline; Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm)

### What Jink Offers Theodore

**Being seen.** Theodore's world is full of adults who do not notice him. Jink notices. Jink grins at him. Jink comes back. (Source: Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm)

**The extraordinary.** In Theodore's narrow orbit -- workshop, errands, streets -- Jink is the first evidence that the world is bigger than Cribbage's clockshop. (Source: Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm)

**Play.** Jink does not treat Theodore as an asset (Wren's pragmatic case), a threat (Sable's calculation), or a subject (Luma's scientific interest). Jink treats him as fun. For a boy who eats alone and works alone, someone who treats him as fun is revolutionary. (Source: Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm)

### What Theodore Offers Jink

**Not being the kid.** With Theodore, Jink is the older one. The experienced one. The one who knows things. In the squad, Jink is the junior, the reckless one. With Theodore, the dynamic inverts. (Source: Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm)

**Wonder.** No one in the squad looks at Jink with wonder. They see a junior pilot with a reckless streak. Theodore sees something magical. Even after the fairy tale breaks, Theodore still watches Jink fly with awe. (Source: Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm)

**A connection outside the hierarchy.** The squad has rank, roles, protocols. Theodore has none. The bond with Theodore is Jink's only relationship not mediated by military structure. "Theodore is the first thing in 1885 that belongs to Jink alone." (Source: Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm; Jink Recklessness brainstorm)

### The Mutual Inversion

Jink is extraordinary to Theodore (a fairy, impossibly small, glowing with light). Theodore is extraordinary to Jink (a giant, impossibly large, capable of crushing her without noticing). Each is the other's impossible creature. But the friendship is not built on the extraordinary. It is built on the ordinary -- Jink's humor, Theodore's competence, shared time, accumulated trust. The extraordinary is how they met. The ordinary is why they stayed. (Source: Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm)

### The Scale

Theodore is a giant to Jink. Jink is a fairy to Theodore. The physical power dynamic is inverted from the emotional one. Theodore could crush Jink without meaning to. Jink has already changed Theodore's life without meaning to. "Scale difference makes emotional equality more powerful." (Source: Script Prompts, Issue 11; Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm)

## Relationships

### Theodore and Sable

Formal. Careful. Theodore instinctively addresses Sable as authority -- she carries herself like someone who commands, and a working-class boy in Victorian London knows how to recognize rank. He is slightly afraid of her. Not because she is four inches tall -- because she is precise and unsmiling and makes him feel like he is being evaluated. "Yes, ma'am" energy -- not because she asked for it, but because Theodore's world teaches deference to authority. Sable told Theodore "this is not a story." His voice around her reflects that: careful, factual, useful. He reports to Sable. He talks to Jink. (Source: Theodore Inner World brainstorm; Plot Outline, Issue 4)

### Theodore and Wren

Technical kinship. "They speak the same language of how things work, just centuries apart." This is where Theodore's clockwork vocabulary is most at home. He can describe a mechanism to Wren and she understands, even though the technology is separated by millennia. Theodore is more confident with Wren than with anyone else in the squad, because with Wren his cleverness is visible. Cribbage does not notice what Theodore can do. Wren notices immediately -- she is "reluctantly impressed" by the charging cradle he builds in Issue 4. (Source: Script Prompts; Theodore Inner World brainstorm; Plot Outline, Issue 4)

### Theodore and Luma

The investigation partnership. From Issue 6 onward, they move through the city together -- Luma riding in Theodore's waistcoat pocket. Theodore knows the streets, the old buildings, which structures predate the current era. Luma has the scanner and the analytical framework. He asks Luma questions the way he asks the world questions -- genuinely wanting to understand. Luma may be the squad member Theodore is most comfortable being uncertain around. With Sable he performs competence. With Jink he performs bravery. With Luma he can admit he does not understand. (Source: Plot Outline, Issues 6 and 8; Theodore Inner World brainstorm)

### Theodore and Thresh

Wary, then careful. Theodore's first encounter with Thresh is intimidating -- Thresh is the most armored, the most visibly a soldier, the one holding a weapon in a white-knuckle grip. Theodore is a boy who watches everything, who "notices details others miss." He may recognize Thresh's trembling hands before anyone in the squad acknowledges it. (Source: Theodore Inner World brainstorm; Plot Outline, Issue 4)

## Arc Summary

### Arc Statement

"Wants fairies to be magical; learns they're something better." "Something better -- real people who chose to stay." (Source: Story Bible; Plot Outline, Arc Threads)

### What He Sees vs. What He Believes

| Phase | What Theodore Sees | What He Believes |
|-------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Issue 3 | A tiny glowing figure with wings | A fairy. Magic is real. |
| Issue 4 | Five soldiers arguing, weapons, military language | They are not magical. This is not a story. |
| Issues 5-7 | A squad that lets him help, trusts his hands, needs his streets | They are people. Tired, specific, real. |
| Issue 9 | A squad that has fractured, a captain who lied | They are flawed. They make mistakes. They are afraid. |
| Issue 11 | Jink serious for the first time, telling him to walk away | They care about him. Enough to try to protect him from themselves. |
| Issue 12 | A squad that chooses to stay | They are not trapped here. They CHOOSE to be here. |

(Source: Theodore Inner World brainstorm; Plot Outline)

### Why Choice Is More Meaningful than Magic

The fairy tale version: magical beings who appeared for a purpose, whose presence is fated, whose power solves problems. The "something better" version: ordinary people -- extraordinary by circumstances, not nature -- who had the option to leave and stayed. Not because they were compelled. Because they decided this mattered.

For a boy who has been left by everyone -- mother dead, father absent, master indifferent -- someone choosing to be present is the most extraordinary thing imaginable. Not magic. Loyalty. Magic is given. Choice is earned. (Source: Theodore Inner World brainstorm; Plot Outline, Issue 12)

### The Issue 9 Fairy Tale

Theodore tells the squad a story his grandmother told him -- about a fairy queen who sent her knights on a quest, but the quest was a riddle, and the answer was that they were already where they needed to be. He tells the story without knowing why it is relevant. The squad does. "Not naive -- insightful in a way the squad can't be because they're too close to their own situation." Theodore understands the paradox through narrative, not analysis. Luma maps the evidence. Theodore tells the story that makes it make sense. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 9; Script Prompts, Issue 9)

### The Issue 12 Writing

The fairy tale he lost in Issue 4 returns transformed. Not the fairy tale his grandmother told -- a new one. His own. About people he knows. He is writing about friends, not fairies. The fairy tale language returns naturally because that is how Theodore processes wonder -- but the content is real. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 12; Theodore Inner World brainstorm)

## Voice

### Canon Foundations

- "Period-appropriate Victorian English -- not incomprehensible Dickensian dialect, but with the cadence and vocabulary of an articulate working-class boy of the era." (Source: Script Prompts)
- "Theodore should sound like a real boy of his time -- not a museum exhibit and not a modern kid in costume." (Source: Script Prompts)
- "Theodore's dialogue contrasts sharply [with Jink's] -- modern-feeling, quick, casual [for Jink]. The gap between how they speak is part of the culture clash." (Source: Script Prompts, Issue 3)

### Verbal Habits

**Mechanical metaphors for emotional states.** "I'm wound too tight." "Something doesn't sit right in the works." He sees function, mechanism, craft -- and applies it to feelings. This is what makes him understand Wren before anyone else does. (Source: Squad Voice Tics brainstorm; Theodore Inner World brainstorm)

**Two trade vocabularies.** Clockwork language from Cribbage: gears, escapements, mainsprings, balance wheels, pinions. Things "mesh" or they do not. Something "runs true" or it is "off its pivot." Printing language from his father's press: type, galleys, impressions, broadsheets. He knows how words become physical objects -- how a story gets set in type, pressed into paper, made permanent. (Source: Theodore Inner World brainstorm)

### Register Shifts

| Speaking To | Register |
|-------------|----------|
| **Jink** | Relaxed by Theodore's standards, which still sounds formal by Jink's. Wonder leaks through. Culture clash: Jink says something modern, Theodore does not understand. Theodore says something formal, Jink teases him. |
| **Sable** | Formal. Careful. "Yes, ma'am" energy. He reports to Sable. He talks to Jink. |
| **Wren** | Technical. The clockwork vocabulary in its element. Theodore is most confident here -- his cleverness is visible. |
| **Luma** | Curious, collaborative, willing to admit uncertainty. |
| **Thresh** | Wary, then careful. Observant. |

(Source: Theodore Inner World brainstorm; Squad Voice Tics brainstorm)

### The Storytelling Voice

Issue 9: "Earnest, formal, a boy telling a story he loves to people he cares about." This is Theodore as narrator. The voice changes -- not clockwork language or street language, but the voice his grandmother used, passed through his own mouth. Slower, more rhythmic, with the cadence of oral storytelling. (Source: Script Prompts, Issue 9; Theodore Inner World brainstorm)

## Hidden Layer

<hidden>Theodore's fairy tale IS the convergence mythology -- the story that survives centuries and shapes which civilizational branch wins. If Theodore's story survives centuries and eventually reaches the fairy civilization as a historical document, then Theodore is -- unknowingly -- the most important human in the timeline.

The printing press is the propagation engine. Theodore's father runs a printing press. Theodore knows printing. He grew up around type, ink, galleys. If the fairy tale he writes by candlelight eventually reaches his father's press -- or any press -- the convergence mechanism has a physical survival engine. A handwritten story can be lost. A printed story can survive centuries. The printing press is how Theodore's fairy tale becomes the founding myth of a civilization.

The boy who lost his fairy tale writes the most important one in history. And the machinery to spread it is already in his life.

The hidden symmetry with Jink: Jink's unauthorized contact with Theodore in Issue 3 is the single most consequential action in Volume 1. Theodore writes the first fairy tale because he met Jink. The fairy tale mythology that becomes the convergence point starts with Jink grinning at a boy in a workshop. The reckless scout and the tinker's boy are, unknowingly, the two halves of the convergence mechanism: Jink provides the story; Theodore provides the telling; the press provides the survival.

(Source: Theodore Inner World brainstorm; Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm; temporal mechanics brainstorm)</hidden>
