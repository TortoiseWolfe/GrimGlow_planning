# Jink

Scout of the fairy squad stranded in 1885 London. The youngest member, the fastest flyer, and the most reckless. Her callsign is a military aviation term -- a jink is a sudden, sharp evasive maneuver -- earned by the squad's most unpredictable pilot. Her arc across Volume 1 is "learning actions in the past have weight" -- she starts treating the past as an adventure and ends understanding that what she does here lasts forever. Her unauthorized first contact with Theodore in Issue 3 is the inciting event for the core emotional relationship of the volume. (Source: Story Bible; Plot Outline, Arc Threads)

## Physical Description

Late teens or early twenties. Lean androgynous build, narrow waist, long limbs -- "built for speed and agility." Light brown skin. Bright golden blonde curly hair kept short on the sides but wild and voluminous on top, falling slightly to one side. Bright dark eyes, sharp jawline, expressive mouth. Expression: "a grin -- reckless, alive, loving this." Even at rest, her stance carries restless energy -- weight on one leg, slight asymmetry. (Source: Character Prompts; Character Turnarounds)

### Suit -- Clean State

Sleeker and lighter than the others, emerald metallic with an emerald shimmer. Less armor, more mobility. The suit is thinner at the joints, with visible flex panels at elbows, knees, and waist that allow extreme range of motion. Minimal utility belt -- just a small satchel at the left hip and a compact signaling device at the right. No weaponry visible. (Source: Character Turnarounds)

**Wing-pack:** Slightly larger than the others relative to body size, optimized for speed. The stowed module extends from between the shoulder blades down to the mid-back, with sharper angular geometry and four distinct wing-fold lines visible on the surface. Wing-pack seams glow emerald green, brighter than the other characters' -- more power routed to flight systems. (Source: Character Turnarounds)

### Suit -- Mid-Mission (Degraded)

Soot-streaked across the chest and back -- evidence of flying through chimney smoke. A visible tear along the right forearm has been patched with tightly wound copper wire, a rough field repair. The left knee panel is scraped and dull. Emerald holographic elements glow brightly through the grime, especially along the spine and wing-pack mount points -- the flight systems run hot and burn through the soot layer. Wing-pack seams pulse with visible emerald light, brighter than any other squad member's degraded suit. (Source: Character Turnarounds)

The visual story: "a character who moves fast, gets dirty, and treats the damage as trophies." (Source: Character Turnarounds)

### Action Pose

Mid-flight through a Victorian chimney forest -- a roofscape of terracotta pots, iron cowls, and billowing smoke. Expression is a grin -- reckless, alive. Wing-pack fully deployed: two pairs of translucent holographic wings shaped like dragonfly wings, rendered in structured emerald-green light with visible geometric lattice, leaving faint prismatic trails as she banks hard between chimney stacks. One hand trails behind leaving a faint arc of emerald holographic light, the other reaches forward. Massive Victorian chimneys tower around her like a canyon. (Source: Character Prompts)

### The Collecting Habit

The satchel at her hip is "stuffed and bulging -- Jink collects things." A thin leather cord, scavenged and Victorian-era, is tied around one wrist as a bracelet -- a souvenir. (Source: Character Turnarounds)

## Personality

### Core Traits

- **Reckless.** "Treats the past like an adventure, which makes her both the most fun character and the most dangerous one." Exhilarated rather than terrified by the Victorian world. Runs toward danger when others assess.
- **Irreverent.** Quips even when it is not appropriate. "Jink's enthusiasm should feel slightly inappropriate given the danger, which is the point." Instinct over procedure, connection over caution.
- **Fastest flyer.** Her entire skill set is evasion. The callsign was earned -- the squad's most unpredictable pilot. The recklessness predates the mission.
- **The youngest.** The squad treats her accordingly -- protected, frustrated by, indulged. She performs the role: being the fun one, the fast one, the irresponsible one is easier than being taken seriously.

(Source: Story Bible; Script Prompts, Issues 1-2; Jink Recklessness brainstorm)

### What Exhilaration Hides

Jink uses speed and humor to avoid feeling weight. Flying fast enough means consequences cannot catch her. The Victorian world is a playground until it is not. The arc is about what happens when she cannot jink out of the way -- when someone she cares about is in danger because of her choices. (Source: Jink Recklessness brainstorm)

## Backstory

The recklessness predates the mission. The callsign was earned in the future. Beyond these facts, backstory details remain deliberately open. She is the youngest squad member, the fastest pilot, and the one most likely to break protocol for reasons that feel right in the moment. (Source: Story Bible; Jink Recklessness brainstorm)

## The Theodore Bond

The core emotional relationship of the volume. (Source: Plot Outline, Arc Threads)

### Discovery (Issues 3-4)

**Issue 3 -- First contact.** Jink, scouting alone against protocol, is caught in the open on Theodore's workbench when he enters unexpectedly. "Jink doesn't panic. Jink grins." Theodore thinks he has found a fairy. Jink does not correct him, "partly because it's simpler and partly because Jink finds the whole thing delightful." Against every protocol Sable has established, Jink talks to the boy. Before leaving, she shows Theodore the wing-pack in full deployment -- "Jink hovers at eye level, wings of structured light filling the workshop. Theodore's face in that light is the defining image." Jink returns to the squad and does not tell Sable what happened. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 3; Script Prompts, Issue 3)

**Issue 4 -- Exposure.** Jink cannot keep the secret -- the squad needs help, and Theodore is help. She brings him to the squad. "Sable is furious at Jink, terrified of the exposure, and immediately calculating." Theodore demonstrates his value with the charging cradle. Sable relents with conditions. The fairy tale breaks here -- Theodore loses the magic, Jink loses the secret. What survives determines the rest of the volume. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 4)

### Partnership (Issues 5-8)

The relationship develops a practical foundation. Theodore creates diversions, acquires components, becomes "genuinely useful and embedded." By Issue 7, "Jink and Theodore have developed a rapport that Sable considers a liability -- they're friends now, not just allies, and friends take risks for each other." The transition from allies to friends is the transition from function to choice. (Source: Plot Outline, Issues 5, 7; Script Prompts, Issue 7)

### Reckoning (Issues 9-12)

**Issue 9 -- The fracture.** The squad fractures over Sable's secret. Jink is furious. Theodore visits, reads the mood, and tells his grandmother's fairy tale -- "not naive -- insightful in a way the squad can't be because they're too close to their own situation." The dynamic inverts: Jink is the one who needs comfort; Theodore is the one who offers it. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 9; Script Prompts, Issue 9)

**Issue 11 -- The rooftop.** "Jink and Theodore have a conversation on a rooftop at night. Jink, for the first time, is serious. She tells Theodore that if things go wrong, he has to walk away -- pretend he never met them, go back to his life. Theodore refuses. Jink says it isn't his choice. Theodore says it isn't Jink's either. It's the most honest conversation they've had." This is the emotional centerpiece of the volume. Everything about the scene is a reversal: Jink, who never takes things seriously, is serious; Jink, who broke protocol to make contact, is trying to undo it; Theodore, who was in awe, is standing firm. "Scale difference makes emotional equality more powerful." (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 11; Script Prompts, Issue 11)

**Issue 12 -- Completion.** Jink diverts to help Thresh during the final operation, abandoning her own target -- recklessness transformed, the same instinct now in service of loyalty rather than adventure. She is absent from the final scene, but the final scene exists because of her: Theodore writes the first fairy tale because he met Jink. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 12)

### What Each Offers the Other

**What Jink offers Theodore:** Being seen -- Theodore's world is full of adults who do not notice him; Jink notices, grins, comes back. The extraordinary -- wings of light, evidence the world is bigger than Cribbage's shop. Play -- "Finds the whole thing delightful." For a boy who works alone and eats alone, someone treating him as fun is transformative. (Source: Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm; Script Prompts)

**What Theodore offers Jink:** Not being the kid -- with Theodore, Jink is the older one, the experienced one, the one who knows things. In the squad, she is junior and reckless; with Theodore, she is capable and extraordinary. Wonder -- no one in the squad looks at Jink with wonder; Theodore does, even after the fairy tale breaks. Connection outside hierarchy -- the bond with Theodore is Jink's only relationship not mediated by military structure. "Theodore is the first thing in 1885 that belongs to Jink alone." (Source: Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm; Jink Recklessness brainstorm)

### The Mutual Inversion

"Each is the other's impossible creature. But the friendship isn't built on the extraordinary. It's built on the ordinary -- Jink's humor, Theodore's competence, shared time, accumulated trust. The extraordinary is how they met. The ordinary is why they stayed." (Source: Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm)

## Relationships

### Jink and Sable

"Jink represents everything Sable's command style can't contain -- instinct over procedure, connection over caution." Sable's fury in Issue 4 is a mask slip -- "Sable's control usually prevents visible anger. Jink got past the mask." Sable considers the Jink-Theodore friendship a "liability" in Issue 7. In Issue 9, Jink is "furious at being lied to" -- Jink trusted Sable the way a soldier trusts a commander, and Sable betrayed that trust. The fury has a mirror: Jink was the first secret-keeper, hiding the Theodore contact in Issue 3. She is furious at Sable for the same kind of calculated omission she herself made. (Source: Plot Outline, Issues 4, 7, 9; Sable Backstory brainstorm; Jink Recklessness brainstorm)

### Jink and Thresh

"Thresh, surprising everyone, sides with Jink" in the Issue 7 debrief about investigating the Titan. The most afraid and the most reckless reach the same conclusion from opposite emotional positions. The alliance is significant because it is the adult validating the kid, and because Thresh's fear-driven logic ("if Titans are here, running won't help") arrives at the same place as Jink's courage-driven instinct. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 7; Jink Recklessness brainstorm)

### Jink and Wren

Jink's opposite. "Feelings first, assessment later" versus "reports findings, not feelings." Jink is fast and instinctive where Wren is methodical and technical. They solve problems in completely opposite ways but depend on each other: Jink's scouting gives Wren the intel she needs for heist planning. (Source: Wren Technology brainstorm, Voice Contrast; Squad Voice Tics brainstorm)

### Jink and Luma

No direct Jink-Luma beats in canon. Both share a positive orientation toward 1885 -- Jink is exhilarated by the past; Luma is fascinated by it. Different reasons, same direction. (Source: Wren Technology brainstorm, Voice Contrast)

## Arc Summary

**Arc statement:** "Learning actions in the past have weight." She starts treating the past as an adventure and ends understanding that what she does here lasts forever. (Source: Story Bible; Plot Outline, Arc Threads)

### The Weight Sequence

| Issue | What Happens | What Weight Feels Like |
|-------|-------------|----------------------|
| 2 | Exhilaration while others are terrified | No weight yet. Adventure mode. |
| 3 | Talks to Theodore. Keeps the secret. | First action with hidden consequences. Does not feel heavy -- it is fun. |
| 4 | Secret exposed. Sable "furious." | First consequence. Someone's anger is a direct result of Jink's choice. |
| 7 | Spots Titan. Friendship is now a "liability." | Stakes escalate beyond fun. |
| 9 | "Furious at being lied to." | The mirror -- Jink was the first secret-keeper (Issue 3). Now furious at Sable for the same thing. |
| 11 | Rooftop: "If things go wrong, he has to walk away." | Full weight. Jink understands her choices have put Theodore in danger. |
| 12 | Diverts to help Thresh, abandoning own target. | Recklessness transformed -- same instinct, different motivation. Not adventure but loyalty. |

(Source: Plot Outline; Jink Recklessness brainstorm)

### The Permanence Concept

"The past can't be undone. That's the fundamental difference between Jink's future and her present. In the future, there are extraction teams, mission resets, command structures that absorb consequences. In 1885, what Jink does STAYS done. Theodore can't un-meet her. The fairy tales can't be un-told. The Titan sighting can't be un-seen." (Source: Jink Recklessness brainstorm)

### What Changes

Jink at the end still acts fast, still takes risks. But now she knows what those risks cost and chooses them anyway. The Issue 12 line -- "So we're not stuck here. We're *needed* here" -- reframes the arc. Recklessness without understanding is the problem. Recklessness with understanding is courage. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 12; Jink Recklessness brainstorm)

## Voice

### Speech Patterns

**Fast, fragments, present tense.** Short sentences, fragments, thoughts that do not finish before the next one starts. "Modern-feeling, quick, casual." The gap between how Jink speaks and how Theodore speaks is part of the culture clash. (Source: Script Prompts, Issues 1, 3; Squad Voice Tics brainstorm)

**"Okay but" / "yeah but" as filler.** The verbal equivalent of dodging. Starting sentences with "okay but" or "yeah but" -- redirecting before the previous thought lands. (Source: Squad Voice Tics brainstorm -- LOCKED; Jink Recklessness brainstorm)

**Deflective.** Uses humor to redirect. When something is serious, Jink makes it funny. When something is scary, Jink makes it exciting. The quipping is a shield. (Source: Squad Voice Tics brainstorm; Jink Recklessness brainstorm)

**"So we're not stuck here. We're *needed* here."** The Issue 12 response to Luma's synthesis. Still fast, still Jink -- but the speed carries weight now. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 12)

### When Jink Goes Quiet

Canon gives one explicit silence: Issue 11 -- "Jink, for the first time, is serious." (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 11)

The progression of Jink's silences marks the arc:

- **Issue 4 -- After Sable's fury.** Jink did not expect the anger. Processing that fun had a cost someone else is paying.
- **Issue 7 -- After the Titan sighting.** A beat of silence before the quipping resumes. The first encounter with something that is not adventure.
- **Issue 9 -- After the fury.** When the squad fractures and everyone retreats, there is nothing funny to say.
- **Issue 11 -- The rooftop.** The silence is not suppression -- it is arrival. Jink has reached something she cannot quip past.

(Source: Plot Outline; Script Prompts; Jink Recklessness brainstorm)

### Voice Contrast

- **Sable and Jink:** Formal versus informal. Authority compressed into few words versus many words to say little. "The contrast is volume and density."
- **Jink and Theodore:** "The gap between how they speak is part of the culture clash." Future slang meets Victorian English -- the comedy and the warmth. Speed versus deliberation. Over the volume, they develop a shared vocabulary.
- **Jink and Thresh:** Silence and speed. "Jink fills silence; Thresh creates it." Both managing the same fear differently -- Jink avoids, Thresh compresses.

(Source: Squad Voice Tics brainstorm; Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm; Script Prompts)

## Hidden Layer

<hidden>Jink's unauthorized contact with Theodore in Issue 3 is the single most consequential action in Volume 1. Theodore writes the first fairy tale because he met Jink. The fairy tale mythology that becomes the convergence point -- the thing both sides of the temporal cold war are fighting over -- starts with Jink grinning at a boy in a workshop.

The reckless action that Sable was furious about, that broke every protocol, that Jink treated as a game -- that is the action that saves the timeline. The irony: the squad member who learns "actions have weight" is the one whose lightest, most careless action carries the most weight of all.

The fairy tale Jink accidentally created by not correcting Theodore -- "I'm a fairy" -- is the prototype for the fairy tale Theodore deliberately creates in Issue 12. The first fairy tale was a lie that felt true. The last fairy tale is a truth that sounds like a lie. Both are real in the way that matters.

Jink's reckless contact does not just create a friendship. It creates the foundational myth of a civilization. And Theodore's father runs a printing press -- the machinery to make that myth permanent is already in Theodore's life. The reckless scout and the tinker's boy are, unknowingly, the two halves of the convergence mechanism: Jink provides the story; Theodore provides the telling; the press provides the survival.

(Source: Jink Recklessness brainstorm; Jink-Theodore Relationship brainstorm)</hidden>
