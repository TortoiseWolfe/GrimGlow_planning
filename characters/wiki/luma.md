# Luma

The squad's medic and scientist, and the character who sees the paradox first. Luma is fascinated by 1885 London in a way no other squad member is -- for her, the past is not a prison but a once-in-a-lifetime research opportunity. She is also the one most tempted to stay. Across Volume 1 she runs a parallel investigation that uncovers the truth the entire narrative builds toward: the convergence point is not a technological event but a mythological one, and the squad's presence in 1885 is the mission itself. Her arc is "sees the paradox first; most tempted to stay" -- and the uncomfortable question of whether her synthesis is genuine insight or motivated reasoning. (Source: Story Bible; Plot Outline, Arc Threads)

## Physical Description

Late twenties in appearance. South Asian features, warm brown skin, large expressive dark eyes, a gentle rounded face. Long dark hair worn in a single braid that falls over her right shoulder to mid-chest, threaded with a thin luminous filament that winds through the braid and pulses softly with blue-violet light -- a combination of aesthetic and functional bio-monitor. Delicate holographic lenses hover just in front of her eyes, not physically attached, suspended by small emitters at her temples -- semi-transparent discs with faint geometric data patterns, resembling spectral spectacles. The lenses glow softly with warm gold-toned light, distinct from the blue-violet of her suit's holographic accents. (Source: Character Prompts; Character Turnarounds)

### Suit -- Clean State

Sapphire-violet metallic, lighter and less armored than the others -- designed for flexibility and sensitivity rather than protection. A medical/scanner module is mounted on her left forearm: a slim raised panel with a small holographic projector that can display biological readouts. Utility belt carries specialized pouches for sample collection, medical supplies, and scanning instruments. Wing-pack is standard-sized with a slightly different seam pattern -- additional sensor filaments woven into the wing-fold lines for environmental data collection during flight. No weapons visible. (Source: Character Turnarounds)

### Suit -- Mid-Mission (Degraded)

The least damaged suit of the squad -- she hangs back from the front lines -- but not clean. Dust coats her lightly overall. Ink stains mark her right hand and right forearm where she has been examining Victorian documents. A smudge of pollen or botanical residue on her left knee from kneeling to examine specimens. One sample pouch on her belt is open, a small pressed flower visible inside -- she has been collecting. The luminous filament in her braid still pulses but slightly dimmer than clean state. Holographic lenses show a faint patina of atmospheric grime but remain functional. The medical scanner on her left forearm has a small crack in its projector housing but still glows blue-violet. (Source: Character Turnarounds)

Every mark on her suit is from investigation, not combat. Her damage is curiosity, not combat. The suit is the temptation made visible. (Source: Character Turnarounds)

### Action Pose

Standing on the open page of an enormous leather-bound book in a Victorian study, examining a botanical illustration larger than she is. Holographic medical scanner hovering near her left hand, projecting a small rotating three-dimensional model of a plant cell that casts structured blue-violet light on the book's page. Her expression is wonder -- not fear, not tension, pure fascination. The study around her is vast: inkwell like a cauldron, quill pen like a fallen tree, candlelight casting everything in amber warmth. She is the one who does not want to leave. (Source: Character Prompts)

### Fairy Tale Echo

Victorian observers call her "the blue fairy" -- the gentle one, the healer. Her warm gold lenses against the sapphire suit create a unique visual signature that no other character shares. (Source: Suit Color Coding brainstorm)

## Personality

### Core Traits

- **Curious before all else.** She cannot help studying things even in a crisis. Before the crash she is reviewing convergence point data with genuine interest. After the crash she treats injuries while simultaneously fascinated by the biology and environment around her.
- **Pattern recognition.** She sees connections between data points before others do. She identifies the cat as a threat before anyone else based on behavioral observation. She maps anomalies across the city before anyone asks her to.
- **Questions as instruments.** Her questions sound casual but are not. Surface level: curiosity, conversation. Below: constructing an evidence chain. The squad may not notice the transition from casual to purposeful because she has been asking questions since Issue 1.
- **Ethically grounded.** She argues against the memory wipe in Issue 4 -- it is risky with damaged equipment, and ethically complicated. The medic's ethical instinct surfaces before the scientist's curiosity.

(Source: Story Bible; Script Prompts, Issues 1-2; Plot Outline, Issue 4; Luma Temptation brainstorm)

### What Draws Her to 1885

The fascination is specific. Canon gives the visual evidence: pollen on her knees from kneeling to examine specimens, ink stains from examining Victorian documents, a pressed flower collected in a sample pouch. Every mark on her suit is a record of curiosity. "Her expression is wonder -- not fear, not tension, pure fascination." "She is the one who doesn't want to leave." (Source: Character Prompts; Character Turnarounds)

The draw begins as scientific -- "once-in-a-lifetime research opportunity" -- and becomes something else as the paradox reveals itself. (Source: Story Bible; Luma Temptation brainstorm)

## Backstory

Research and medical background in the far future. Beyond these facts, backstory details remain deliberately open. Her curiosity is the defining trait -- it precedes the mission, predates the crash, and outlasts every crisis. (Source: Story Bible)

## The Investigation

The investigation spine is Luma's central narrative contribution to Volume 1. She runs a parallel inquiry that begins with a single anomaly and ends with the revelation that reframes the entire volume.

### Investigation Progression

| Issue | Discovery | What Luma Knows After |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | Reviews convergence point data before the crash with genuine interest | Professional baseline -- this is her field |
| 2 | Treats injuries while fascinated by the biology and environment; identifies the cat threat by behavioral observation | Scientific reflex -- cannot stop observing |
| 4 | Finds a glyph on a brick wall, hidden beneath soot -- "Not Victorian. Not Titan. *Fairy.* Old." Does not tell the others yet. | Someone from their side has been here before |
| 6 | Cataloging anomalies: factory worker with anachronistic knowledge, shadow that moved wrong, dead bird with future-tech cellular damage. Finds Titan bio-trace and a fairy glyph at human height. | Both sides have been here for generations. Someone was communicating with humans. |
| 8 | Presents all findings to the squad. Traces the glyph trail with Theodore to a sealed church basement. Cache: corroded wing-pack, dead data core, and carved into the wall -- "STAY." "Luma translates with shaking hands." | Prior operatives existed, stayed, left a message. "The tenses stop working." |
| 9 | Connects Sable's classified file to the cache -- the prior operatives, the word "STAY." Were they ordered to remain? Did they choose to? | The mission file and her field evidence point to the same truth. |

### The Issue 8 Turn

Issue 8 is the biggest turn in the investigation spine. The glyph, the energy signatures, the Titan traces -- all snap into focus. Someone with their technology was here before. Or will be here. "The tenses stop working." Past and future stop being separate categories. The paradox enters Luma's vocabulary. (Source: Script Prompts, Issue 8; Plot Outline, Issue 8)

### The Investigation Partnership

Luma and Theodore investigate together from Issue 6 onward. "Luma rides in Theodore's waistcoat pocket -- a scene that plays the scale and the tenderness simultaneously. She's a scientist with a research subject the size of a mountain range, and he's a boy with a fairy in his pocket. They're both in their element." (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 6)

## The Temptation

The temptation to stay is Luma's defining internal conflict and the source of the volume's most uncomfortable ambiguity.

### Before the Paradox

Luma wants to stay because the past fascinates her. This is selfish -- she is prioritizing her curiosity over the squad's mission to go home. The draw is real, the investigation is genuine, but the motivation is personal. (Source: Luma Temptation brainstorm; temporal mechanics brainstorm)

### After the Paradox

Luma wants to stay because the timeline needs them here. Staying IS the mission. The evidence supports this. The logic holds. But the conclusion is awfully convenient for the one squad member who wanted to stay all along. (Source: Luma Temptation brainstorm; temporal mechanics brainstorm)

### The Motivated Reasoning

Luma recognizes the ambiguity in herself, and it torments her. She has done the epistemological work: am I seeing this because it is there, or because I want it to be there? She cannot answer. The evidence is real. The conclusion is supported. But the motivation behind the investigation was never pure. She presents the Issue 12 synthesis with rigor and visible doubt -- not uncertain about the evidence, but uncertain about herself. (Source: Luma Temptation brainstorm)

### The "STAY" Message

Four readings coexist:
- **Confirmation** -- someone else reached the same conclusion
- **Horror** -- they stayed and died alone
- **Herself** -- she is looking at her own future if the squad does not come around
- **Evidence** -- it is not just a message but a data point in the causal loop

The shaking hands are all of these at once. Scientific excitement, personal dread, ethical weight, and the recognition that the person who carved this word felt exactly what she feels. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 8; Luma Temptation brainstorm)

## Relationships

### Luma and Theodore

The investigation partnership. From Issue 6 onward, they move through the city together -- Luma in Theodore's waistcoat pocket, both in their element. Theodore knows the streets, the old buildings, which structures predate the current era. Luma has the scanner and the analytical framework. Together they cover what neither could alone. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 6; Luma Temptation brainstorm)

### Luma and Sable

Every discovery Luma makes is a countdown timer on Sable's secret. Sable carries the classified version of the same truth Luma is finding through field evidence -- they hold pieces of the same puzzle without knowing the other has one. Sable's "too controlled" reaction to Luma's findings in Issue 8 reveals to Wren that Sable already knew. Sable must eventually reckon with the possibility that the squad member she might have dismissed as self-indulgent was the first to see the truth. (Source: Sable Backstory brainstorm; Plot Outline, Issue 8)

### Luma and Wren

Both analytical, closest in speech among the squad. But Luma asks questions where Wren makes statements. Luma sees the paradox as a scientist -- patterns, evidence, hypothesis. Wren sees it as an engineer -- systems, causation, feedback. Wren pushes back on the Issue 12 synthesis: "Stories don't build civilizations." But she has already proved Luma's point -- the telegraph carries information that shapes development. (Source: Wren Technology brainstorm; Plot Outline, Issue 12)

### Luma and Jink

Both respond positively to 1885, but for completely different reasons. Jink is exhilarated -- the past is an adventure, a sensory experience. Luma is fascinated -- the past is a dataset, a system to understand. Jink experiences the past; Luma studies it. Both are drawn to it. (Source: Luma Temptation brainstorm)

### Luma and Thresh

The medic-soldier dynamic. Luma talks Thresh down from the controlled spiral after the dockworker's boot near-miss in Issue 7. She asks questions, makes connections, speaks in full thoughts; Thresh answers in fragments. (Source: Plot Outline, Issue 7; Thresh Fear Arc brainstorm)

## Arc Summary

### Arc Statement

"Sees the paradox first; most tempted to stay." The squad member most drawn to 1885 is the one who proves they must remain. (Source: Story Bible; Plot Outline, Arc Threads)

### What She Starts With

Curiosity. A scientist's reflexive need to observe, catalogue, and understand. Professional interest in the convergence point that becomes personal fascination with the world around it. A medical ethic that says "do no harm" and a scientific hunger that says "know everything." (Source: Story Bible; Script Prompts, Issues 1-2)

### What She Finds

The paradox. The evidence chain that connects fairy glyphs, Titan traces, a corroded wing-pack, a dead data core, and one carved word into a conclusion that reframes the entire volume: the squad's presence in 1885 is not an accident but a causal loop, and the convergence point is the stories humanity tells about fairies and monsters. (Source: Plot Outline, Issues 4-12)

### The Cost

The synthesis is inseparable from the desire. She wanted to stay before she knew she had to. The squad cannot be certain her conclusion is not motivated reasoning. She cannot be certain herself. She asks them to stake their lives on her analysis, and her analysis happens to recommend the thing she wants. The weight is not just "should we stay?" but "should the squad trust the person whose conclusion conveniently matches her desire?" (Source: Luma Temptation brainstorm; temporal mechanics brainstorm)

### How the Squad Receives the Synthesis

- **Wren** pushes back: "Stories don't build civilizations." But she has already proved Luma's point -- the telegraph carries information that shapes development. She argued herself into understanding.
- **Jink** does not care about the theory: "So we're not stuck here. We're *needed* here."
- **Thresh:** "I don't want to stay. I'll stay."
- **Sable** listens. Quiet. Looks at her squad. "They look like fairy tales. They always did."

(Source: Plot Outline, Issue 12; Luma Temptation brainstorm)

## Voice

### Three-Verb Arc

Canon gives Luma three verbs across the volume that form a progression:

| Phase | Verb | What It Sounds Like |
|-------|------|---------------------|
| Issues 1-3 | **Asks** | Curiosity. Questions about everything. Some relevant, some not. The questions sound idle but they are building a picture. |
| Issues 4-7 | **Observes** | Investigation. The questions become directed. The casual surface hides purpose. |
| Issues 8-9 | **Connects** | Synthesis beginning. "What if" replaces "what is." |
| Issues 11-12 | **States** | The questions stop. For the first time, Luma does not ask. She tells. The synthesis is delivered as conclusion, not inquiry. |

(Source: Script Prompts, Issues 1, 2, 9; Luma Temptation brainstorm)

### Verbal Habits

**"But what if--" qualifying questions.** Her signature verbal tic. Questions that reframe another person's statement as inquiry, opening space for alternative explanations. (Source: Squad Voice Tics brainstorm -- LOCKED; Luma Temptation brainstorm)

**Questions that do double duty.** Surface level: curiosity, conversation, getting along with the boy. Below: constructing an evidence chain. The squad may not notice the transition from casual to purposeful because Luma has been asking questions since Issue 1. The habit provides cover. (Source: Luma Temptation brainstorm)

### Voice Contrast

Luma asks where Wren states. Both are analytical, both precise -- but Wren says "It's shattered" and Luma says "What if it's not?" The analytical pair, operating in opposite modes. (Source: Wren Technology brainstorm; Squad Voice Tics brainstorm)

### When Luma Stops Asking

The Issue 12 synthesis is the longest sustained speech from Luma in the volume. Every other time, she asks. Here, she tells. The change in mode is the arc beat -- the scientist who has gathered enough data to reach a conclusion. The steadiness of the delivery matters. She has been building to this for issues. She lays out the evidence: the glyphs, the cache, the folklore, the pamphlet, the two mythologies. (Source: Luma Temptation brainstorm; Plot Outline, Issue 12)

## Hidden Layer

<hidden>When scanning a brain-plugged human, Luma notices something that unsettles her: the physiological changes -- dark veins, iridescent eyes, bone density shifts, nervous system alterations -- map directly to ogre physiology in the squad's medical databases. The brain-plugging process is not just controlling humans. It is the early stage of what eventually becomes full ogre biology in the far future. The Titans are not using an existing slave race. They are creating one, from humanity, across centuries of convergence point interventions.

Luma notices the resemblance in Volume 1. She files it, does not share it with the squad. It is a seed, not a revelation. The full implications -- that the squad could potentially erase the entire ogre army from the future timeline by stopping the process here -- belong to Volume 2.

This gives Luma a devastating new dimension to her temptation to stay. The stakes are not just fairy tales and cultural mythology. The stakes are the future shape of an entire species. And acknowledging this means staying even longer -- which is exactly what she already wants.

(Source: Ogre Origin brainstorm; Luma Temptation brainstorm)</hidden>
