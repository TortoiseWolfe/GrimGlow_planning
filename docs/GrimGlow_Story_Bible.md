# GRIMGLOW — Story Bible

*Working Document — Concept Development Notes*

---

## Concept Overview

This is a genuinely strong concept. The "future tech reads as magic" inversion is clever on its own, but what makes it sing is the scale dynamic — tiny operatives navigating a world of giants, running heist-style missions just to scavenge parts. That's where your gameplay loop and your narrative tension live in the same space.

## The Enemy & The Stakes

The enemy is from a low-gravity world. They're enormous. In the future, size differences between species are trivially managed by technology and diplomacy. But when both sides crash in the steampunk past, stripped of that infrastructure, the war suddenly maps onto something ancient and mythic. Your soldiers look like fairies. The enemy looks like giants. Ogres. Demons.

The temporal cold war becomes the origin of fairy tales.

That gives you an incredible thematic engine. The steampunk humans aren't just bystanders — they're witnesses, and the stories they tell about what they see will ripple forward through centuries of folklore. Which means every visible action the fairies or their enemies take in the past has two consequences: the physical effect on the timeline, and the narrative effect on human mythology. Your characters aren't just trying not to break history. They're accidentally writing it.

As for what the war is about: consider that both sides have discovered the timeline has convergence points — moments where small changes cascade into civilizational-scale outcomes. The steampunk era is one of them. The industrial revolution is the hinge where technology either branches toward the fairies' future or the Titans' future. The cold war was both sides probing these convergence points, gathering intelligence, nudging carefully. It went hot because someone made a move on a major one. The fairies' squad was deployed to this convergence point, got shot down in transit, and crashed right into the most contested moment in temporal history — except now they're stranded with no backup and no comms.

Their mission might have been reconnaissance. Or it might have been something more decisive that their commander hasn't fully disclosed to the squad.

## The Crew

You want a small squad, maybe four to six, each with a distinct role that maps to both a gameplay function and a story arc.

### Sable — The Captain

Career officer from a military family. She knows the mission parameters but may be holding back the full scope from her team. Her arc is about the weight of command when there's no chain of command above you anymore. In the steampunk world, she's the one most committed to not interacting with humans at all, which puts her in direct tension with Wren.

### Wren — The Engineer

The one who has to actually fix the ship, which means she must engage with the steampunk world. She's the most hands-on with human technology and develops a grudging admiration for it. Gears and steam are primitive to her, but there's an ingenuity to them she didn't expect. She's your primary "heist planner" — she identifies what parts they need, where to find them, and how to extract them without being seen. Gameplay-wise, she's the character who translates objectives into missions.

### Jink — The Scout

Youngest on the squad, fast, reckless, the best flyer. The name is a military aviation term — a jink is a sudden, sharp evasive maneuver — earned as a callsign by the squad's most unpredictable pilot. Jink is the one who finds the steampunk boy, and against all protocol, makes contact. Jink treats the past like an adventure, which makes them both the most fun character and the most dangerous one. Their arc is learning that actions in the past aren't a game — they have weight.

### Thresh — The Soldier

The squad's heavy hitter, which is ironic given their size relative to humans. Thresh is the one most traumatized by the crash and most frightened by the vulnerability of being tiny in a giant world with no support network. A character who was brave in the future and is terrified in the past — that's a rich inversion.

### Luma — The Medic/Scientist

Fascinated by everything. For Luma, the past isn't a prison, it's a once-in-a-lifetime research opportunity. She's the one most tempted to stay. She's also the one who starts to notice that the timeline may already be more damaged than they think, and that some of the "fairy tales" humans tell... are too accurate to be coincidence. Someone else has been here before them.

### Theodore Edmund Hartley — The Steampunk Boy

A tinker's apprentice, maybe twelve or thirteen, clever with his hands, overlooked by the adult world. The weight of his full name — Theodore Edmund Hartley — speaks to parents who had aspirations his station hasn't delivered on yet, a gap between the grandness of the name and the smallness of his life that does character work before a single line of dialogue. He discovers them, and his role becomes this impossible balancing act — he's their translator, their guide to the giant world, their cover story. But he's also a kid, and he wants the fairies to be what he imagines they are. The tension between what Theodore wants them to be (magical, wonderful) and what they actually are (tired soldiers trying to survive) is one of your best emotional threads.

## Visual Language

### Fairy Tech

Should feel like light trapped in geometry. Holographic elements that are translucent, prismatic, with clean vector-like edges — think less "sparkle" and more "structured luminescence." Their wing-packs should look like dragonfly wings rendered in light, with visible geometric scaffolding. When they use tools or interfaces, the holograms should manifest as floating glyphs and rotating shapes — a visual language that reads as runes to steampunk observers.

### The Degradation Arc

Their suits start pristine silver, almost mirror-like. As they spend time in the past, they accumulate soot, oil, scratches. But here's the visual trick — the holographic elements still glow through the grime. So a fairy who's been in the past for weeks looks like a soot-covered figure with light bleeding through the cracks. That's a gorgeous image, and it tells the story of their situation visually: future beauty being slowly consumed by the past, but not yet extinguished.

### Steampunk World

Should feel heavy, warm-toned, dense. Brass and copper, thick rivets, smoke, gaslight. Everything is oversized from the fairies' perspective — a pocket watch is a wall-sized clock, a keyhole is a doorway, a workbench is a landscape. This scale contrast is your strongest visual asset. Every environment is essentially two environments: what it is to humans and what it is to fairies.

### The Titans

The enemy's tech should read as the dark mirror. Where fairy tech is light and geometric, Titan tech is shadow and organic. Dark iridescence, like oil on water. Their cloaking doesn't make them invisible — it makes them wrong, a shimmer in the air that feels predatory. To steampunk humans, they look like the shadows that move when they shouldn't, the shape in the fog. If the fairies are the origin of the fair folk, the Titans are the origin of the things that go bump in the night.

## Plot Structure

The macro plot has three layers running simultaneously:

### Layer 1 — Survival

Fix the ship. This is your episodic/mission structure. Each repair requires a specific component that must be sourced from the steampunk world. A copper coil from a telegraph office. A precision gear from a clockmaker's shop. A glass lens from a lighthouse. Each acquisition is a heist with its own complications.

### Layer 2 — The Enemy

The Titans also crashed. Maybe not at the same time or place, but they're here, and they're pursuing their own objective at the convergence point. The fairies start finding evidence of Titan activity — disruptions in the local timeline, humans behaving strangely, technology appearing that shouldn't exist yet. The fairies have to decide: do they just escape, or do they stop the Titans from rewriting history?

### Layer 3 — The Paradox

The longer they stay, the more they realize they might be the convergence point. Their presence, their interactions with Theodore, the stories spreading about fairies and monsters — all of it might be what creates the future they came from. The most destabilizing revelation: they can't leave without completing their mission, because their mission is being here. The timeline needs them to be the fairies in the stories.

That third layer is where your story gets its real depth. The question stops being "can we get home" and becomes "were we always supposed to be here?"
