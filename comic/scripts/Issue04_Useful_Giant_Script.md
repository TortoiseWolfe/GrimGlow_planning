# GRIMGLOW Issue 4 — "Useful Giant" — Panel-by-Panel Production Script

---

## Production Notes

- **Degradation state:** Early wear. All suits show dust accumulation, minor scuffs from wall-cavity living, and the first oil stains from contact with Victorian machinery and camp improvisation. Holographic accent nodes remain active but are slightly dimmer than Issue 1-2 — power rationing. Sable's silver suit has a grey soot bloom across the right shoulder and forearms. Wren's copper suit is the dirtiest — oil at the forearms, copper dust at the knees, a scorch mark on the left shoulder from the crash still visible. Jink's emerald suit has the chest soot streak from Issue 3 plus new dust from scouting. Thresh's ruby-crimson suit shows dented left pauldron (Issue 1) plus new scuff marks along the legs. Luma's sapphire-violet suit is the cleanest but shows dust and ink stains at the hands.
- **Lighting baseline:** This issue is dominated by dual-light environments. The wall-cavity camp is lit from above by warm wood tones and filtered gaslight leaking through floorboard gaps, and from below/within by cool holographic glow from the squad's perimeter emitters and power cell. Theodore's world above is warm gaslight and grey daylight. The contrast between human warmth and fairy coolness is the visual engine of every scene.
- **Dialogue style:** Five distinct military voices plus one Victorian boy. Sable commands — clipped, furious, precise. Wren is pragmatic — arguments framed as engineering problems. Jink is defensive — the quips are gone, replaced by tight justification. Thresh speaks minimally — observation, not opinion. Luma is measured — medical and scientific authority deployed carefully. Theodore speaks period-appropriate Victorian English — formal, earnest, not archaic for its own sake.
- **Key narrative plants:** Theodore's charging cradle — a crude brass-and-copper-wire device cradling a faintly glowing fairy power cell. He reverse-engineered charging from studying the crash shard. This is proof of value. Sable's memory-wipe threat is real — the tech exists in their damaged kit. Luma's medical objection is also real — developing human brain, unknown neural architecture, genuine risk. Wren's pragmatic argument is the tiebreaker. The negotiation resolves with Sable imposing strict conditions, not with warmth.
- **Scale note:** Theodore is enormous to the squad. His face fills their sky when he peers through gaps. His fingers are the size of their bodies. His voice resonates through the wall cavity. This is not metaphorical — it is the literal, constant, visual reality of every shared scene. Thresh's discomfort is the sharpest register of this.

## SECTION 1: THEODORE SEARCHING (Pages 1-4)

### Lighting Baseline
Grey-gold Victorian morning light. Overcast sky with occasional breaks of pale sun. The courtyard is sheltered — warm brick tones, damp cobblestones, the residual warmth of nearby workshops leaking through walls. Fairy tech residue glows faintly only where Theodore is actively looking — geometric light traces on brick, a scorch mark that still holds warmth. The crash shard in Theodore's pocket provides the only consistent cool light — a faint prismatic glow against his waistcoat fabric.

### Environment Setup
The courtyard behind a row of Clerkenwell buildings — the crash site from Issue 2, where the largest ship fragment landed. The wreckage itself has been recovered by the squad, but traces remain: a scorch mark on cobblestones (covered by a shifted newspaper), scratches on brick where equipment was dragged, faint geometric residue where holographic tools contacted surfaces. Theodore has been mapping these traces systematically since Issue 3. He has a small notebook with sketches — he is applying tinker's logic to fairy archaeology. The wall gap is at the courtyard's far end, in the base of an old brick wall — a gap between bricks where mortar has crumbled, just large enough for a fairy to pass through, invisible at human scale unless you are on your hands and knees looking.
