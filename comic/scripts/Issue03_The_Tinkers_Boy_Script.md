# GRIMGLOW Issue 3 — "The Tinker's Boy" — Panel-by-Panel Production Script

---

## Production Notes

- **Degradation state:** Soot streaks on all suits. Jink's emerald suit has a soot streak across the chest from the crash plus minor scuffs. Water marks and dried mud persist from Issue 2. All suits are still structurally sound but visibly lived-in — the pristine mirror-finish is gone, replaced by a dull metallic sheen with grime in the panel seams. Sable's cracked right shoulder panel persists. Thresh's dented left pauldron persists. Wren's forearms are oil-stained and picking up copper dust. Luma's one cracked holographic lens persists, the other functional. Jink's wing-pack is fully functional, emerald glow still strong through grime. In this issue, only Jink appears in extended scenes; the rest appear briefly in the camp sequence (Pages 4-5).
- **Lighting baseline:** This issue introduces warm amber as the dominant palette for the first time. Issues 1-2 were survival horror shot in grey, rain, and darkness. Issue 3 is the tonal pivot — Theodore's world is warm. Candlelight, gaslight, brass catching firelight, wood grain, oil-stained hands. The only cool light intrusions are the crash shard's geometric glow (Pages 5-6) and Jink's holographic accents (Pages 7-11). This contrast — warm human world invaded by cool fairy light — is the visual story of the entire issue.
- **Dialogue style:** Theodore speaks period-appropriate Victorian English — naturalistic, not stagey. An articulate working-class boy of the 1880s. "I ain't" not "I am not," but also not incomprehensible dialect. Mr. Cribbage is curt, dismissive, speaks in instructions. Jink's dialogue contrasts sharply — modern-feeling, quick, casual, irreverent. The gap between how Theodore and Jink speak is part of the culture clash that defines first contact.
- **Scale philosophy:** Pages 1-6 are shot entirely at human scale — standard cinematic compositions, Theodore as a full-sized person in a full-sized world. When Jink appears (Page 7), SCALE becomes the visual tool. Pages 7-10 use REVERSE SCALE: show the workbench first at Theodore's human-scale perspective, then cut to the same environment at Jink's fairy-scale perspective. Gears become boulders. A thimble becomes a bucket. A candle becomes a towering column. The workbench is a landscape. Close-ups on Theodore's face carry the emotional beats — wide eyes, the wonder.
- **Key narrative plants:** Theodore's cleverness shown, not told — he fixes something Cribbage cannot (Pages 2-3). Theodore's isolation established through silent meals and solitary walks. The crash shard responds to warmth (Page 6) — plant for the charging cradle in Issue 4. Jink makes contact against all protocol — the recklessness that defines their character.

## SECTION 1: THEODORE'S WORLD (Pages 1-6)

### Lighting Baseline

Warm amber dominates. The workshop interior is lit by gaslight wall sconce and workbench candles — dense, golden, catching every brass surface. Daytime exteriors use grey Victorian daylight filtered through perpetual overcast, with soot-stained buildings muting everything further. Theodore's world is warm but small. The only cool light intrusion in this section is the crash shard (Pages 5-6), where geometric blue-white glow bleeds into the amber palette for the first time — the fairy world reaching into Theodore's.

### Environment Setup

Cribbage's workshop is a cramped, cluttered space on a Clerkenwell side street. Two workbenches: Cribbage's main bench (larger, better lit, facing the window) and Theodore's smaller secondary bench (pushed against the far wall, dimmer, crowded with overflow). Shelves of clocks in various states of repair line the walls. Tools hang on wall pegs. A half-assembled automaton sits on a high shelf. The shop window faces the street — "HOROLOGY" in gold leaf on the glass, visible in reverse from inside. A back door leads to the alley. The atmosphere is dense with the smell of machine oil, brass polish, and candle wax. This is a working space, not a showroom.
