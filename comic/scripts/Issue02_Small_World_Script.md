# GRIMGLOW Issue 2 — "Small World" — Panel-by-Panel Production Script

## Production Notes

- **Degradation state:** First environmental marks. Suits show water spots from rain, mud accumulation on boots, minor scuffs from gutter debris. Still mostly pristine but no longer factory-new — the sheen is dulled. Crash damage from Issue 1 persists: Sable's cracked right shoulder panel (hairline fractures glow faintly at the seam), Thresh's dented left pauldron, Wren's oil-stained forearms, Luma's one cracked holographic lens. Jink's suit is the cleanest — minor scuffs only, wing-pack fully functional.
- **Lighting baseline:** This section covers two lighting environments. Pages 1-2 (Dawn): grey pre-rain overcast, muted natural light filtering through cloud cover, warm amber gaslight from the street below reaching the rooftop as a distant glow. Pages 3-6 (The Crossing): rain begins — the light flattens further, grey-on-grey, with gaslight reflections multiplied in wet surfaces. Holographic suit accents are the only cool light source, faint through fresh grime.
- **Dialogue style:** Military professionals under sustained stress. Sable gives orders — clipped, no room for discussion. Wren reports and redirects to mission focus. Jink quips — inappropriate enthusiasm that is the point. Thresh speaks less than Issue 1 — short, functional sentences. The silence IS her character beat. Luma observes, identifies, asks. Nobody explains anything to the reader.
- **Scale philosophy:** This issue's dominant visual tool is scale contrast. Shoot from fairy perspective to make the mundane enormous. Rain gutter as river. Kerbstone as cliff. Granite setts as boulder field. Gas lamp as lighthouse tower. Every environment description must sell the 4-inch perspective. The crossing is survival horror — low angles, tight framing, the ordinary made monstrous.

## SECTION 1: DAWN (Pages 1-2)

### Lighting Baseline

Grey pre-dawn overcast transitioning to grey morning. No direct sunlight — cloud cover diffuses everything. Distant amber gaslight from the street below reaches the rooftop as a faint warm underglow. The squad's holographic suit accents provide the only cool light — department colors glowing faintly through the first layer of grime and water spots. The contrast between warm gaslight and cool holographic light is muted here — both subdued, both struggling against the grey.

### Environment Setup

The rain gutter from Issue 1's final scene. A lead-lined half-round channel (4.5 inches across at human scale), which at fairy scale functions as a broad highway or dry riverbed. Dead leaves serve as lean-to shelters. Grit and debris form the terrain floor. Beyond the gutter lip, the rooftop stretches as overlapping slate shelf-terrain. Victorian morning sounds are the invisible threat: church bells as rolling thunder, cart wheels on cobblestones as seismic grinding, a factory whistle as a piercing shriek. The world is waking up, and it is enormous.

## SECTION 2: THE CROSSING — First Half (Pages 3-6)

### Lighting Baseline

The rain begins on Page 3 and builds through Page 6. The light shifts from grey overcast to active rain — darker, flatter, with water multiplying every reflection. Gaslight from the street below becomes diffused through rain, creating a warm amber haze at street level. Wet surfaces catch and scatter both warm gaslight and cool holographic suit accents. The drainpipe interior (Pages 3-4) is nearly dark — lit only by the squad's holographic suit glow and grey light filtering from the openings above and below. When they emerge onto the street (Pages 5-6), the gas lamps are lighthouses in a wet grey world.

### Environment Setup

The crossing begins vertically — the squad descends from rooftop to street via the drainpipe. At fairy scale, the drainpipe's 66mm bore is a tight vertical tunnel, socket joints providing platforms every four feet (which is 12x their height), bracket rests on the exterior offering ledges. Then the rain hits. The gutter they just left becomes a river. The drainpipe becomes a waterfall. At street level, granite setts are boulder plateaus (3x character width), the gaps between them are ravines and trenches, the kerbstone is a cliff face (2x character height), and a gas lamp post is a lighthouse tower (24x character height). Everything is wet. Everything is enormous. Everything is a threat.

Grey overcast courtyard light — muted, flat, filtered down between the surrounding buildings. No direct sunlight. The rain has eased to a drizzle. The wreckage itself is dark and inert — no holographic glow, no alien light. The ship's systems are dead. When Wren begins her diagnostic (starting Page 12), her copper-gold holographic interface will be the first colour to touch the wreckage. Until then, the crash site is a grey monument.

### Environment Setup
A narrow courtyard behind a row of terraced buildings — accessible through the alley from the street. Cracked flagstones, weeds in the gaps, a broken barrel against one wall, a rusted iron standpipe. The largest ship fragment rests against the base of the east wall, partially buried under a drift of blown newspaper pages (the Daily Telegraph, dates scattered), dead leaves, and a torn scrap of canvas that blew off a market stall. At human scale, the courtyard holds what looks like accumulated rubbish against a wall. At fairy scale, the wreckage is a cathedral of shattered hull plating and dead holographic panels — enormous, dark, and silent.
