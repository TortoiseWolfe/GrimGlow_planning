# GRIMGLOW Issue 7 — "The Lens" — Panel-by-Panel Production Script

---

## Production Notes

- **Degradation state:** Continued and advancing. The squad has been operating in Victorian London for nearly two weeks. All suits show sustained environmental wear — soot accumulation, scuffs, grime layered over earlier damage. Holographic accent nodes are dim, pulsing faintly through grime on power rationing. **Wren's** copper suit remains the dirtiest — oil stains at forearms and elbows now permanent, copper dust ground into the knees, clockwork grease from the Issue 5 heist, fresh river-damp and iron oxide from the waterfront. **Sable's** silver suit carries the grey soot bloom across the right shoulder, forearm dust, water marks, the tension-wear crease at the collar; her suit reads more pewter than silver now. **Jink's** emerald suit has the chest soot streak from Issue 3 plus chimney soot, scouting scuffs, and new salt-damp staining from the river environment — the emerald is visibly dulled. **Thresh's** ruby-crimson suit shows the dented left pauldron from Issue 1, leg scuffs, accumulated grime, and after the near-miss in this issue, fresh boot-leather scrape marks across the right shoulder plate. **Luma's** sapphire-violet suit has dust, ink stains, brick dust and cobweb residue from the Issue 6 investigation; her left holographic lens still carries the hairline crack. All suits: holographic accent glow visible but subdued — light bleeding through grime, the core visual metaphor of the series.
- **Lighting baseline:** Industrial waterfront — a fundamentally different visual environment from the Clerkenwell domestic warmth of Issues 3-6. The docks are iron, stone, and tarred timber. River water is black and reflective. Fog sits heavier here, thick with coal smoke and river damp. Warmth comes from the lighthouse beam (steady amber, rotating), distant dock lanterns, and the orange glow of furnace light from warehouses. Cool light comes from the squad's holographic tech (subdued, department-colored) and moonlight filtered through cloud cover. The lighthouse beam is the dominant light source for the heist sequence — a sweeping amber cone that creates rhythmic cycles of light and shadow across every surface.
- **Dialogue style:** Wren leads the operational briefing — technically precise, planning the most complex mission yet. Sable is competent but emotionally distant, carrying the secret from her file; her orders are clipped, her silences longer. Jink's banter with Theodore is warmer and more natural than with the squad — a genuine friendship developing. Thresh speaks in short bursts, controlled. After the near-miss, Thresh's dialogue fragments — half-sentences, forced steadiness. Luma is calm and clinical when talking Thresh down, warm when off-duty. Theodore speaks period-appropriate Victorian English — increasingly confident, less formal with Jink.
- **Key narrative plants:** Sable's emotional distance is visible to the squad — she gives correct orders but offers nothing beyond them. Jink and Theodore's friendship scene (Pages 5-6) establishes the bond that will be tested in Issue 11's rooftop scene. Thresh's near-miss (Pages 8-9) physically escalates the fear arc — this is the closest a human has come to killing one of them by accident. The lighthouse heist establishes that these operations are getting harder, not easier.
- **Scale note:** The waterfront environment introduces new scale challenges. Mooring ropes are as thick as tree trunks. Iron bollards are the size of buildings. The river is an ocean. The lighthouse interior — iron stairs, glass lens housing, mechanical lamp assembly — is an industrial cathedral at fairy scale. The lighthouse keeper is the first human obstacle during an active operation (the clockmaker in Issue 5 was passive; the keeper is moving through the space).

## SECTION 1: PLANNING AND TRANSIT (Pages 1-3)

### Lighting Baseline
Pages 1-2: Wall-cavity camp. Cool holographic glow from perimeter emitters and the power cell, dimmer than earlier issues. Warm amber bands filter through floorboard gaps above. Wren's lighthouse schematic dominates — a structured copper-gold holographic projection rotating at the center of the space, casting geometric shadows across faces and timber beams. The briefing has a more serious tone than Issue 5's clockmaker planning — the squad knows the stakes now.

Page 3: Transition lighting. Clerkenwell's narrow domestic streets (warm gaslight, brick, close quarters) give way to wider commercial roads, then the waterfront — where the light opens up but turns colder. River fog diffuses everything. Dock lanterns are orange-amber, spaced wider apart. The sky is visible here — overcast grey, unlike the chimney-forest ceiling of Clerkenwell. The openness is disorienting after the enclosed streets.

### Environment Setup
The wall-cavity camp is established from Issues 4-6 — between timber beams, rough wood, cobwebs, holographic perimeter glow. The squad has lived here long enough that it reads as home: equipment positions are settled, worn paths visible in the dust. Wren's briefing position is established — she projects from the same wrist emitter, but the schematic is more complex than Issue 5's clockmaker layout. The transit sequence should visually mark the shift from known territory (Clerkenwell) to unknown (the waterfront) — architecture changes, street width changes, air changes. The squad is leaving their operational comfort zone.
