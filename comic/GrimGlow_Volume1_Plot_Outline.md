# GRIMGLOW — Volume 1: *The Stories They Tell*

## 12-Issue Plot Outline

---

## Structural Framework

The 12 issues break into three acts:

**Act I (Issues 1–4): Falling / Finding**
Establish the crash, the world, the squad dynamics, and Theodore. The Titans are a background threat — evidence, not encounters. The dominant tone is survival and wonder.

**Act II (Issues 5–8): Scavenging / Sighting**
The heist-mission loop kicks in. The squad begins repairing their ship while the Titan presence escalates from traces to direct threat. Theodore becomes embedded with the squad. The dominant tone shifts to tension and growing attachment.

**Act III (Issues 9–12): Convergence / Cost**
The Titans make their move on the convergence point. The squad has to choose between escaping and intervening. The paradox layer surfaces — they may have always been here. The dominant tone is revelation and sacrifice.

Each issue has a **fairy tale echo** — a moment where something the squad does maps onto a piece of folklore that the reader (and eventually the characters) will recognize. These start as easter eggs and become structural by Act III.

---

## ISSUE 1 — *"Descent"*

**Cold open in the future.** Not a long worldbuilding dump — just enough to establish the temporal cold war as context. The squad is aboard a small recon vessel in transit through a temporal corridor. The mission briefing is clipped, professional, routine. Sable is calm and in command. Jink is restless. Wren is running system checks. Thresh is composed and confident — this is important, because we need to see who Thresh *was* before we see who they become. Luma is reviewing convergence point data on a holographic display.

Then the attack. Something hits them in transit — an ambush in the corridor. It's fast, violent, and disorienting. The ship breaks apart. Systems fail. The temporal corridor destabilizes.

**The crash.** They come through into the steampunk world at speed, trailing fire and light across a Victorian night sky. The ship fragments scatter. From the ground, we see it the way humans would see it — a streak of strange light, a falling star that breaks into pieces.

**Final pages:** The squad pulls themselves from wreckage in a space that's incomprehensibly vast to them — it's a rain gutter on a rooftop. They're battered, disoriented. Sable does a headcount. Everyone alive. Ship destroyed beyond anything they expected. Comms dead. No backup coming.

Last panel: Sable looking out over the edge of the gutter at a sprawling Victorian city lit by gaslight, stretching to the horizon. She is four inches tall and the world is enormous.

**Fairy tale echo:** The falling star. Multiple witnesses across the city see the streak of light. One of them is Theodore, watching from a workshop window.

---

## ISSUE 2 — *"Small World"*

**The scale issue hits.** This issue is about the squad's first night in the steampunk world and the visceral reality of being tiny. Everything is a survival problem. Rain is a flood. A stray cat is a predator. A cobblestone street is a boulder field. The squad has to cross a single city block to regroup at the largest piece of wreckage, and it's an odyssey.

**Character work:** Thresh's fear surfaces here. In the future, Thresh was the one who kicked down doors. Now a pigeon is a legitimate threat. The gap between who Thresh was and who they are in this context begins to open. Jink, conversely, is exhilarated — wing-pack still works, and flying through this world is incredible. Sable keeps the squad moving with clipped authority, but we see the first cracks — she checks a data module privately, and whatever she reads tightens her expression.

**Wren's assessment:** They reach the main wreckage. Wren surveys the damage and delivers the verdict — the ship's core is intact but the drive system is shattered. She can rebuild it, but she needs components that don't exist in miniature. She'll have to source equivalent parts from human technology and adapt them. Gears, coils, lenses, conductors. Each one a mission.

**The list.** Wren produces the component list that will drive the heist structure for the next several issues. It's long. Each item means infiltrating a different part of the human world.

**Fairy tale echo:** A lamplighter on his rounds sees a cluster of strange lights flickering in a rain gutter. He crosses himself and walks faster. At the pub later, he tells someone he saw will-o'-the-wisps.

---

## ISSUE 3 — *"The Tinker's Boy"*

**Theodore's issue.** We shift perspective to see the steampunk world through human eyes. Theodore Edmund Hartley, apprentice to a clockmaker named Mr. Cribbage, a man who is competent but unkind in a mundane way — dismissive, impatient, uninterested in Theodore's cleverness. Theodore's world is small: the workshop, errands, a narrow orbit of streets. He has no parents present (they're away, or dead, or simply absent — left deliberately ambiguous for now). He's alone in the way a resourceful child is alone: managing, but not thriving.

**The discovery.** Theodore finds evidence of the crash — a scorch mark in an alley, a shard of something that isn't metal or glass or anything he recognizes, faintly warm, with light moving inside it. He pockets it. That night, following another trace, he encounters Jink.

**The contact.** Jink, who was scouting alone against protocol, is caught in the open on Theodore's workbench when he enters unexpectedly. The scene plays the scale for everything it's worth — Theodore is a giant to Jink, a looming figure in candlelight. But Jink doesn't panic. Jink grins.

The conversation is halting and strange. Theodore thinks he's found a fairy. Jink doesn't correct him, partly because it's simpler and partly because Jink finds the whole thing delightful. Against every protocol Sable has established, Jink talks to the boy.

**Final page:** Jink returns to the squad and doesn't tell Sable what happened. But Theodore is already looking for them again.

**Fairy tale echo:** This *is* the fairy tale — a boy finds a fairy in his workshop. We're watching the origin of the story happen in real time. A subtle panel shows Theodore, later, telling a friend that he saw something impossible. The friend laughs. The story begins to spread anyway.

---

## ISSUE 4 — *"Useful Giant"*

**Theodore becomes an asset.** Jink can't keep the secret — the squad needs help, and Theodore is help. When Theodore finds them again (he's persistent and clever with his hands, able to follow traces Wren's equipment leaves), Jink brings him to the squad. The meeting with Sable is tense. Sable is furious at Jink, terrified of the exposure, and immediately calculating. Theodore is overwhelmed — five tiny luminous people are real and living in the walls of his world.

**The negotiation.** Sable wants to wipe Theodore's memory of them (the technology exists, barely, in their damaged kit). Luma argues against it — it's risky with their equipment damaged, and ethically complicated. Wren makes the pragmatic case: they need someone who can move through the human world. Theodore can acquire things they can't reach. He's useful.

Theodore, for his part, is desperate to be part of this. He makes his case not with logic but with demonstration — he's already figured out that the shard he found is a power cell, and he's rigged a clumsy but functional charging cradle for it from workshop parts. Wren is reluctantly impressed.

**Sable relents,** with conditions. Theodore tells no one. He follows instructions. He understands that they are not magical, they are not here to grant wishes, and this is not a story. (The irony, of course, is that it absolutely is.)

**B-plot:** Luma, exploring the area around their camp, finds something that stops her cold — a marking on a brick wall, hidden beneath soot. It's a glyph. Not Victorian. Not Titan. *Fairy.* Old. Someone from their side has been here before them. She doesn't tell the others yet.

**Fairy tale echo:** Theodore tries to explain to the squad what humans think fairies are. He describes the folk tales — tiny winged beings, mischievous, magical, granting wishes or stealing children. The squad's reactions are a spectrum from offended (Sable) to amused (Jink) to fascinated (Luma). Thresh says nothing, staring at Theodore with the unsettling realization that this enormous child could crush any of them with one hand and doesn't even realize it.

---

## ISSUE 5 — *"The Clockmaker's Heart"*

**First heist issue.** Wren needs a precision gear assembly from a high-end clockmaker's shop — not Cribbage's modest operation, but a prosperous establishment across the city. This is the first full heist-structure issue: Wren identifies the target, plans the infiltration, assigns roles.

**The operation:** Jink flies recon and maps the interior. Thresh provides overwatch from a window ledge, scanning for threats (and struggling with the exposure — they're high above a street, visible, vulnerable). Sable coordinates. Wren infiltrates.

**The complication:** The clockmaker works late. Wren is inside the mechanism of a display clock when the man sits down at his bench and begins working inches away from her. She's pinned, surrounded by spinning gears the size of doors, unable to move without being seen or crushed. The tension is claustrophobic.

**Theodore's role:** He creates a diversion — enters the shop on a pretended errand, engages the clockmaker in conversation, buys Wren time. It's the first moment where his value as a "giant" ally is undeniable. But he's also a terrible liar, and the scene is equal parts tense and endearing.

**Wren gets the gear.** But in the process, she pauses, studying the clockwork around her. The engineering is beautiful. Primitive, yes, but elegant in a way she didn't expect. She has a moment of genuine admiration for human ingenuity — the first crack in her assumption that the past is simply inferior.

**Fairy tale echo:** The clockmaker, after Theodore leaves, finds a tiny handprint in the oil on his workbench. He tells his wife a brownie visited his shop — a helpful house spirit from folklore. He leaves out a saucer of milk the next night.

---

## ISSUE 6 — *"Traces"*

**The Titans arrive in the narrative.** Not a direct encounter — evidence. Luma has been cataloging anomalies: a factory worker who suddenly understands metallurgical principles decades ahead of his time. A pub where patrons describe a shadow that moved against the light. A dead bird found with cellular damage consistent with technology that shouldn't exist for centuries.

**Luma's investigation** takes center stage. She and Theodore visit a section of the city where the anomalies cluster. Luma rides in Theodore's waistcoat pocket — a scene that plays the scale and the tenderness simultaneously. She's a scientist with a research subject the size of a mountain range, and he's a boy with a fairy in his pocket. They're both in their element.

**What they find:** A basement where something large has been. Claw marks in the brick, too high for any animal. A residue on the floor that Luma's scanner identifies as Titan bio-trace. And, scratched into a support beam at human height — a symbol. It's not Titan. It's a simplified version of the fairy glyph Luma found on the wall in Issue 4. Someone has been communicating. Someone has been here before, and they were talking to humans.

**Meanwhile:** Sable, back at camp, finally accesses a corrupted mission file on her data module. We don't see what it contains, but her reaction is severe. She encrypts it and tells no one.

**Fairy tale echo:** Theodore mentions that his neighborhood has old stories about "the Tall Ones" — figures seen in fog, too large, too still. Shadows that followed people home. The stories are generations old. The Titans have been here before too.

---

## ISSUE 7 — *"The Lens"*

**Second heist, higher stakes.** Wren needs a precision optical lens from a lighthouse on the city's river. The mission requires the full squad and Theodore, and it takes them out of their familiar territory into an industrial waterfront — bigger, louder, more dangerous.

**Character dynamics under pressure.** Sable is increasingly withdrawn, her secret weighing on her. Jink and Theodore have developed a rapport that Sable considers a liability — they're friends now, not just allies, and friends take risks for each other. Thresh is functional but brittle; a near-miss with a dockworker's boot sends them into a controlled spiral that Luma has to talk them down from.

**The heist itself** is the most complex yet. The lighthouse is occupied. They have to navigate a working industrial structure during operating hours. The lens they need is *in use* — they can't just take it without the lighthouse going dark. Wren devises a swap: Theodore acquires a replacement lens of lower quality during the day, and the squad performs the switch at night.

**Complication:** During the operation, Jink spots something from the lighthouse gallery — a dark shape moving on the far bank of the river. Too large to be human. Moving in a way that's wrong, a shimmer in the fog. Jink calls it in. Sable orders radio silence and mission completion. They get the lens, but Jink saw what they saw.

**The debrief** is heated. Jink wants to investigate. Sable says their mission is repair and extraction, not engagement. Thresh, surprising everyone, sides with Jink — if Titans are here, running won't help. Sable shuts it down. Orders are orders.

**Fairy tale echo:** The lighthouse keeper reports to the harbor authority that the light flickered strangely one night, and he saw small dancing lights in the gallery. The harbor men chalk it up to drink. But a journalist notes it down.

---

## ISSUE 8 — *"What Luma Found"*

**The mystery issue.** Luma brings her findings to the squad — the old fairy glyph, the Titan traces, the evidence that someone from their side was here before them, possibly much earlier. This raises questions no one is comfortable with. Were there previous missions to this convergence point? Were they sanctioned? What happened to those operatives?

**Sable's reaction** is too controlled. She asks precise questions. She doesn't seem surprised enough. Wren notices.

**Luma and Theodore investigate further,** tracing the old glyph pattern through the city. Theodore proves invaluable — he knows the streets, the old buildings, which structures predate the current era. The trail leads to a sealed basement beneath a decommissioned church. Inside, they find a cache: fairy technology, centuries old, degraded but unmistakable. A wing-pack, corroded to a skeleton of struts. A data core, dead. And carved into the wall, in the fairy written language, a single word that Luma translates with shaking hands.

The word is **"STAY."**

**Parallel thread:** Thresh, on solo watch, has a genuine encounter with a human — a child, younger than Theodore, who wanders into the alley where Thresh is posted. The child sees Thresh and isn't afraid. Reaches out a hand. Thresh, whose arc is about fear, freezes — not at the danger but at the gentleness. The child's parent calls them away. The moment is small, quiet, and devastating for Thresh.

**Fairy tale echo:** The church where the cache is found has local legends attached to it — that it was built on a site where "the good folk" once lived, and that the foundation stones were laid with their blessing. The legend is true. It just isn't what anyone thought it meant.

---

## ISSUE 9 — *"Sable's File"*

**The truth comes out.** Wren confronts Sable about the encrypted mission file. She's been watching Sable's behavior — the controlled reactions, the too-specific questions about Luma's findings, the refusal to engage the Titan threat. Wren is an engineer; she reads systems, and Sable's system doesn't add up.

**Sable breaks.** Not dramatically — she's too disciplined for that. But she shares the file. The mission wasn't reconnaissance. It was intervention. Their command identified this convergence point as the critical juncture — the moment where the timeline either branches toward their civilization's future or the Titans'. The squad was sent to ensure the branch goes the right way. The file includes references to **prior insertions** — previous operatives sent to this era, none of whom reported back.

**The weight of this:** They weren't sent to observe. They were sent to *do something,* and Sable doesn't know what, because the operational details were stored in a file segment that was destroyed in the crash. She knows the mission is critical. She doesn't know what the mission *is.*

**Squad fracture.** Jink is furious at being lied to. Thresh withdraws further. Luma connects this to the cache — the prior operatives, the word "STAY." Were they ordered to remain? Did they choose to? Wren, ever practical, points out that none of this changes the immediate problem: they still need to fix the ship, and the Titans are still out there.

**Sable's arc crystallizes:** She held back the truth to protect her team's focus. Now the truth is out, and focus is shattered. The weight of command when there's no chain of command above you.

**Fairy tale echo:** That night, Theodore tells the squad a story his grandmother told him — about a fairy queen who sent her knights on a quest, but the quest was a riddle, and the answer was that they were already where they needed to be. Theodore doesn't know why this story feels relevant. The squad does.

---

## ISSUE 10 — *"The Titan"*

**First direct Titan encounter.** The squad is on a component run — a copper induction coil from a telegraph station — when they walk into an active Titan operation. The Titan is *inside* the telegraph office, doing something to the equipment. It's enormous. Its cloaking flickers — dark iridescence, oil on water, a shape that's *wrong* in the gaslight. It's modifying the telegraph system, inserting something into the mechanism.

**The fight** is asymmetric and terrifying. The squad is four inches tall. The Titan is the size of a building to them. Thresh engages — and this is Thresh's moment, because the fear that's been consuming them meets the thing they were trained to do, and training wins. Thresh doesn't stop being afraid. They fight afraid.

**The squad drives the Titan off** — not through superior force but through disruption. Wren rigs a feedback pulse through the telegraph wires that scrambles the Titan's cloaking, exposing it. The Titan, suddenly visible in a human-occupied building, is forced to withdraw. But it's not defeated. It's inconvenienced. And now it knows they're here.

**The aftermath:** The telegraph office is damaged. Humans arrive. The squad hides and watches as the Victorians try to make sense of what happened — smashed equipment, strange residue, claw marks. The story that emerges in the morning papers: a demon attacked the telegraph office. The monster mythology grows.

**Theodore's reaction** to the Titan encounter — hearing about it secondhand — is the first time he's truly frightened. The fairies are small and wondrous. The Titans are something else. The fairy tales have a dark side.

**Fairy tale echo:** The newspaper account of the "telegraph demon" runs alongside a column about the recent sightings of mysterious lights and helpful brownies. The folklore is writing itself in real time, split into two mythologies — the fair folk and the monsters. A folklorist at a local university begins collecting the accounts.

---

## ISSUE 11 — *"Convergence"*

**The Titans' plan becomes clear.** Wren analyzes the device the Titan was installing in the telegraph system. It's a temporal nudge engine — a technology designed to subtly alter the information flowing through communication networks, shifting the trajectory of human technological development toward outcomes that favor the Titans' future. The telegraph is the nervous system of the industrial age; control it, and you control which innovations succeed and which die.

**The scope hits:** The Titans aren't trying to change one event. They're trying to *steer the industrial revolution.* And they've been doing it for longer than the squad has been here — the prior evidence, the old stories about Tall Ones, all of it points to a long-term Titan operation. The squad's crash didn't start the conflict at this convergence point. It escalated it.

**Sable makes the call:** They can't just leave. Even if the ship were ready (it isn't — they still need components), they can't abandon this era to the Titans. The mission, whatever its specifics were, was about this. They have to stop the Titan operation.

**The plan:** Wren identifies a network of Titan devices planted across the city's telegraph infrastructure. They need to neutralize them. It's the biggest operation yet — multiple simultaneous targets, the full squad deployed, Theodore coordinating from the human side.

**Character moments before the storm:** Jink and Theodore have a conversation on a rooftop at night. Jink, for the first time, is serious. They tell Theodore that if things go wrong, he has to walk away — pretend he never met them, go back to his life. Theodore refuses. Jink says it isn't his choice. Theodore says it isn't Jink's either. It's the most honest conversation they've had.

Luma visits the church cache one more time. She sits with the corroded wing-pack and the word "STAY." She's starting to understand what it means.

**Fairy tale echo:** The folklorist at the university publishes a pamphlet collecting the recent sightings — lights, brownies, demons, shadows. He titles it *"The Stories They Tell."* It sells well. The fairy tales are becoming codified, written down, made permanent. The mythology is setting like concrete.

---

## ISSUE 12 — *"The Stories They Tell"*

**The operation.** The squad hits the Titan devices simultaneously across the city. It's the most ambitious sequence in the volume — Jink flying through chimney forests, Wren disarming devices inside massive machines, Thresh holding a position against a Titan drone, Sable coordinating from a church steeple, Luma providing real-time analysis. Theodore moves through the streets, creating diversions, carrying equipment, being the giant hands the squad needs.

**It goes wrong.** One of the Titan devices is guarded. Thresh is overwhelmed. Jink diverts to help, leaving their own target unfinished. Sable has to adapt the plan in real time. Wren discovers that the devices are networked — disabling them individually triggers a failsafe. They need to be taken out simultaneously, and they've lost their synchronization.

**Theodore's moment.** He can reach the last device. It's inside a telegraph relay station, human-scaled, human-accessible. Wren talks him through the disarm over a holographic comm link. His clever hands — oil-stained, burn-scarred, trained on clockwork — do the work. He is ordinary in an extraordinary moment.

**The devices go down.** The Titan network in this city is neutralized. Not destroyed — the Titans will adapt, rebuild, try again. But the squad has bought time. The convergence point is held.

**The revelation.** In the quiet after, Luma presents her full analysis to the squad. The prior operative's message — "STAY" — the old glyphs, the cache, the fairy tales now being published, the monster stories, all of it. She's mapped it. The mythology the humans are building — the fair folk, the monsters, the stories — isn't a side effect. It *is* the convergence point. The timeline doesn't branch based on a specific technological event. It branches based on *what humanity believes.* The stories they tell about fairies and monsters shape their relationship with the unknown, with the other, with technology and magic and fear and wonder. Those stories ripple forward through centuries and determine which civilization humanity becomes.

The squad was sent here because the mission *is* being here. Being seen. Being the fairies in the stories. The prior operatives knew. That's why they stayed.

**The squad responds.** Luma presents with rigor and visible doubt — she knows the synthesis is convenient, knows she can't fully separate her desire to stay from the evidence. Wren pushes back: "Stories don't build civilizations." But she's already proved Luma's point — the telegraph carries information that shapes development. Jink: "So we're not stuck here. We're *needed* here." Thresh: "I don't want to stay. I'll stay."

**Sable's response is quiet.** She looks at her squad — battered, soot-covered, holographic light bleeding through the grime. They look like fairy tales. They always did.

**Final scene:** Theodore, in his workshop, by candlelight. He's writing. Not a report, not a letter — a *story.* About tiny luminous people with wings of light, who fell from the sky and fought monsters in the fog. He doesn't know he's writing mythology. He thinks he's writing about his friends.

**Last panel:** His pen on paper. The first words of the oldest fairy tale.

**Fairy tale echo:** This *is* the fairy tale echo. The whole volume has been one.

## Arc Threads Across the Volume

**Sable's secret** — seeded in Issues 2, 4, and 6, confronted in Issue 9, resolved in her decision in Issue 11. Her arc is about learning that command doesn't mean control, it means choosing which truths to carry and which to share.

**Thresh's fear** — established in Issue 2, deepened in Issues 5 and 7, the encounter with the child in Issue 8, and the breakthrough in combat in Issue 10. Thresh doesn't stop being afraid. They learn to act anyway, which is a different and harder kind of bravery.

**Jink and Theodore's bond** — the core emotional relationship of the volume. Contact in Issue 3, partnership building through Issues 5–8, the honest rooftop conversation in Issue 11, and Theodore's final act of writing their story in Issue 12. Jink starts treating the past as an adventure and learns it has weight; Theodore starts wanting the fairies to be magical and learns they're something better — real people who chose to stay.

**Wren's admiration for human tech** — subtle and cumulative. The clockwork in Issue 5, the lighthouse in Issue 7, the telegraph in Issue 10. She arrives thinking the past is primitive and leaves recognizing that ingenuity isn't a function of era. This matters because it's the thematic argument for the convergence point itself — human creativity is what both sides are fighting over.

**Luma and the mystery** — the investigative spine. Glyph discovery in Issue 4, Titan traces in Issue 6, the cache and "STAY" in Issue 8, full synthesis in Issue 12. Luma is the character who sees the paradox first, because she's the one looking at the past as something to understand rather than escape.

**The degradation arc** — visual storytelling across all 12 issues. The suits start pristine in Issue 1 and accumulate damage, soot, and improvised repairs. By Issue 12, the squad looks like what they've become: future soldiers consumed by the past, with light still bleeding through.
